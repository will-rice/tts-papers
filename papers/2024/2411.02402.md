# Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters

###### Abstract

Recently, neural network-based methods for computing optimal transport maps have been effectively applied to style transfer problems. However, the application of these methods to voice conversion is underexplored. In our paper, we fill this gap by investigating optimal transport as a framework for voice conversion. We present a variety of optimal transport algorithms designed for different data representations, such as mel-spectrograms and latent representation of self-supervised speech models. For the mel-spectogram data representation, we achieve strong results in terms of Fréchet Audio Distance (FAD). This performance is consistent with our theoretical analysis, which suggests that our method provides an upper bound on the FAD between the target and generated distributions. Within the latent space of the WavLM encoder, we achived state-of-the-art results and outperformed existing methods even with limited reference speaker data.

###### keywords:

voice conversion, optimal transport, neural networks

## 1 Introduction

The goal of VC is to generate a modified voice that maintains the linguistic content of the source speaker while adopting the prosody and vocal characteristics of a target speaker \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib1)\]. It has various applications including voice modification \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib2)\], singing voice conversion \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib3)\], and privacy \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib4)\]. The most common case is non-parallel VC, i.e. when the speech content is different for the source and target speaker datasets. Existing VC methods such as StarGAN-VC \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib5), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib6)\], Diff-VC \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib7)\] and kNN-VC \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib8)\] deliver impressive results. But these models have an drawbacks, such as complex training procedures, significant computational resources required for inference or large quantity of target speaker data (\wasyparagraph[2.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S2.SS2 "2.2 Voice Conversion ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")).\
There has been recent interest in the use of neural network-based optimal transport (OT) maps for generative modelling in high-dimensions \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib9), [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib11)\]. However, the potential of optimal transport maps in voice conversion has not been comprehensively explored. In this paper, we fill this gap by proposing variants of OT methods for voice conversion.

- •
  For mel-spectogram representation we present a neural network-based OT approach called NOT-VC (\wasyparagraph[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3 "3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")). This method proves to be resource-efficient in terms of inference and simpler in terms of training compared to its predecessors as DiffVC and StarGAN-VC respectively. We justify our approach theoretically by analyzing the recovered maps and show that the learned map upper bounds the FAD \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib12)\] between real and generated data (\wasyparagraph[3.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.SS1 "3.1 Conditional Neural Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")). Furthermore, we extend this method to create an Extremal NOT \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib13)\] named XNOT-VC (\wasyparagraph[3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.SS2 "3.2 Extremal Conditional Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")) and demonstrate state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance according to FAD.
- •
  For voice conversion within the latent space of the WavLM \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib14)\] model (\wasyparagraph[3.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.SS3 "3.3 Flow-Matching Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")), we propose an OT-based Flow-Matching \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib15), [16](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib16)\] approach named FMVC. This method is lightweight and effectively circumvents the limitations of the current best-performing any-to-any VC method named kNN-VC \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib8)\], providing SOTA results even with limited target data (\wasyparagraph[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S5 "5 Experiments within Audio Latent Space Representation ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")).\

In our paper, we explore the applicability of optimal transport to non-parallel VC. The main contribution is the development and theoretical justification of several OT methods that achieve impressive results and avoid the limitations of existing VC approaches.

## 2 Background and Related Work

### 2.1 Optimal Transport

Optimal transport is a mathematical tool designed to minimize the cost of moving mass between distributions. Suppose there are two probability distributions $`\mu`$ and $`\nu`$ over measurable spaces $`\mathcal{X}`$ and $`\mathcal{Y}`$ respectively, where $`{\mathcal{X},\mathcal{Y}} \subset {\mathbb{R}}^{D}`$. We want to find a measurable map $`T:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y}}`$ such that mapped distribution is equal to the target $`\nu`$, $`{T_{\#}\mu} = \nu`$. For a cost function $`c:{{\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}}`$, the OT problem between $`\mu,\nu`$ is

``` math
{\inf\limits_{{T_{\sharp}\mu} = \nu}{\int_{\mathcal{X}}{c{(x,{T{(x)}})}{d\mu}{(x)}}}}.
```

In the Kantorovich OT formulation \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib17)\], we are seek for a probability measure $`\pi`$ over $`\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}`$ where marginals over $`\pi`$ satisfying $`\pi_{x} = \nu`$ and $`\pi_{y} = \mu`$, respectively \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib18), \wasyparagraph1\]. Denote the set of all such $`\pi`$ by $`\Pi{(\mathcal{X},\mathcal{Y})}`$, the problem is to find an optimal transportation plan $`\pi^{\ast}`$ that minimizes

``` math
{\inf\limits_{\pi \in {\Pi{(\mu,\nu)}}}{\int_{\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}}{c{(x,y)}{d\pi}{(x,y)}}}}.
```
\

For the cost function $`{c{(x,y)}} = {\frac{1}{2}{|{x - y}|}_{2}^{2}}`$, the solution value of ([1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S2.E1 "In 2.1 Optimal Transport ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters"), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S2.E2 "In 2.1 Optimal Transport ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")) is called Wasserstein-2 ($`{\mathbb{W}}_{2}`$) distance \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib18), \wasyparagraph1\]. To solve the OT problem in practice, different discrete solvers such as Sinkhorn can be used \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib19)\]. For continuous OT in high dimensions, maximin form of ([1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S2.E1 "In 2.1 Optimal Transport ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")) with a neural approximators is used \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib9), [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib11)\]. Recently the Flow-Matching (FM) approach \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib15)\] with a simple training objective that regress onto a target vector field was proposed. For the target vector field that corresponds to an OT displacement interpolant, this methods provides a straight paths between points, and approximates OT map \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib15), [20](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib20)\].

### 2.2 Voice Conversion

Most VC methods operate with the mel-spectrogram representations \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib1)\]. These representations convert raw audio signals into a 2D time-frequency representation that displays the frequency of speech over time. After conversion, vocoder models are typically used to transform these mel-spectrograms back into raw audio. Despite their effectiveness, this VC methods have specific drawbacks related to their distinct approaches. Diffusion-based models as Diff-VC \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib7)\] require substantial computational resources due to the numerous steps involved in inference. On the other hand, generative adversarial networks-based (GANs) \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib5)\] methods, such as StarGAN-VC \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib5), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib6)\], optimize multiple losses which – when combined with different coefficients – complicate the optimization and hyperparameter tuning process. A more detailed discussion on related works in provided in Appendix (\wasyparagraph[B.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#A2.SS3 "B.3 Related Work and Baselines ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters"))\
Within the latent space of the speech representation model, unit-selection methods have been introduced. These methods convert speech between the pair of the source and target speakers by replacing each frame of the source speech with its selected correspondence in the target representation. A strong result was achieved by the kNN-VC \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib8)\] method. It was shown that replacing the source speech frames with their nearest neighbors in the target speech provides a SOTA result for any-to-any conversion. Despite its advantages, this method requires extensive recordings (5-10 minutes) of the target speaker for accurate speech conversion, see Figure 2 in \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib8)\]. This is an obvious limitation, because if the recording of the target speech is too short, we simply won’t be able to find where to match the phones and biphones present in the source utterance \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib8), \wasyparagraph5.2\].

## 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport

### 3.1 Conditional Neural Optimal Transport

To solve OT problems in high dimensions, the most popular approaches \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib9), [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib11)\] consider maximin form of ([1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S2.E1 "In 2.1 Optimal Transport ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")):

``` math
{{{{\max\limits_{f}\min\limits_{T}}{\int_{\mathcal{X}}{c\left( x,{T{(x)}} \right)}}} - {f\left( {T{(x)}} \right)d\mu{(x)}}} + {\int_{\mathcal{Y}}{f{(y)}{d\nu}{(y)}}}}.
```

Where potential $`f`$ is Lagrangian multiplier \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib21)\], that aims to ensure that the generated distribution by $`T`$ matches the target distribution $`\nu`$, and penalizes the former if it does not. Within neural approximations for $`T`$ and $`f`$, this type of methods are called Neural Optimal Transport (NOT) \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib10)\]. To build OT map for VC across multiple domains, we propose a conditional reformulation of the NOT method \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib22)\]. Let denote distributions, of source speaker $`\mu`$, target speaker$`\nu`$, and reference speaker $`\eta`$ over measurable spaces $`\mathcal{X}`$, $`\mathcal{Y}`$ and $`\mathcal{S}`$ respectively, where $`{\mathcal{X},\mathcal{Y},\mathcal{S}} \subset {\mathbb{R}}^{D}`$. We want to find the transport map $`T`$ that for any $`s`$ map conditioned distribution $`\mu{( \cdot |s)}`$ to the respected one $`\nu{( \cdot |s)}`$. Formally we can write our objective as:

$`\mathcal{L}_{OT} = \max\limits_{f}\min\limits_{T}\int_{\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{S}}{(c\left( x,T{(x,s)} \right) -}`$$`f\left( T{(x,s)},s \right))d\mu(x,s) + \int_{\mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{S}}f(y,s)d\nu(y,s).`$

Now, the Lagrangian multiplier $`f`$, aims to ensure that the generated distribution $`T_{\sharp}\mu{( \cdot |s)}`$ matches the target distribution $`\nu{( \cdot |s)}`$ for any given $`s`$. The solution to the problem ([4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.E4 "In 3.1 Conditional Neural Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")) can be practically carried out by using neural networks $`T_{\theta}:{{{\mathbb{R}}^{D} \times {\mathbb{R}}^{S}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{D}}`$ and $`f_{\psi}:{{\mathbb{R}}^{D}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}}`$ to parameterize $`T`$ and $`f`$ respectively. In practice, the speaker encoder model $`E_{\omega}`$ to embed the reference speaker $`s`$ is used. The entire optimization procedure can be found in Appendix (\wasyparagraph [B.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#A2.SS1 "B.1 Algorithm ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")), the visual illustration is given in Figure [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Conditional Neural Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters").\
Given a pair ($`\hat{f},\hat{T}`$) that approximately solves equation ([4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.E4 "In 3.1 Conditional Neural Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")), it is natural to question the quality of the recovered map $`T`$. To answer this, we provide a bound on the difference between the optimal $`T^{\ast}`$ that maps into the target and $`T`$.\
Theorem 1. (informal). Assume that there exists a unique deterministic OT plan for quadratic cost between $`\mu`$ and $`\nu`$, i.e., $`\pi^{\ast}`$ for $`T^{\ast}:{{{\mathbb{R}}^{D} \times {\mathbb{R}}^{S}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{D}}`$. Assume that $`\hat{f}`$ is $`\beta`$-strongly convex $`({\beta > 0})`$ and $`\hat{T}:{{{\mathbb{R}}^{D} \times {\mathbb{R}}^{S}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{D}}`$. Then the map obtained by minimizing ([4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.E4 "In 3.1 Conditional Neural Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters") upper bounds the FAD between the $`\nu`$ and distribution generated by $`T`$. More details and proof are given in Appendix (\wasyparagraph[A](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#A1 "Appendix A Analysis of the Recovered Map ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")).

### 3.2 Extremal Conditional Optimal Transport

Usually, samples in the target domain may be noisy or too different from the source, especially in non-parallel VC setups. Thus, in some cases, it would be beneficial to not use only the part of the target distribution. For this, the extremal transport maps \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib13)\] can be used. Extremal transport performs outlier detection in the target space by ignoring samples that are too different from the source distribution with respect to the given cost. In this formulation, we learn the $`T`$ that maps only into the part of the target distribution that reduces the cost. To obtain the extremal formulation from NOT-VC we simply need to add $`f \leq 0`$ constraints over $`f`$ and multiply $`f{(y,s)}`$ by a weight parameter $`w \geq 1`$. This results in the following optimization:

$`\mathcal{L}_{EOT} = \max\limits_{f \leq 0}\min\limits_{T}\int_{\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{S}}{(c\left( x,T{(x,s)} \right) -}`$$`f\left( T{(x,s)},s \right))d\mu(x,s) + w\int_{\mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{S}}f(y,s)d\nu(y,s).`$

This method can be seen as a tool for finding the nearest neighbors of the input samples to the target, according to the cost function, (see Figure 1 in \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib13)\]). Importantly, the parameter $`w`$ in this formulation controls the closeness of the generated samples to the input ones, see  \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib13)\] for more details.

### 3.3 Flow-Matching Optimal Transport

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/extracted/5935976/Images/WAVOT_interspeech.png)

Figure 2: Wav audio is fed into the WavLM. At the same time, the reference is fed into the WavLM. Then the OT matching $`\mathcal{L}_{OTM}`$ (SinkVC or FMVC) converts the voice into the given latent representations. After inference, the results are transformed back into raw audio using the vocoder.

It has been shown that self-supervised models extract representations where nearby features have similar phonetic content. We propose a method that uses a self-supervised speech representation model to encode the audio \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib14)\]. This approach provides an encoder-converter-vocoder structure. Firstly, we extract self-supervised representations of the source and reference speech. Then, conversion to the target speaker takes place, by replacing each frame of the source representation by frame from the reference via OT map. Finally, a pre-trained vocoder synthesizes audio from the converted representation. We solve a matching OT problem ([2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S2.E2 "In 2.1 Optimal Transport ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")) between source and target bag-of-vectors, using the Sinkhorn algorithm \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib23)\]. The OT plan $`\pi`$ provides optimal pairs between source and reference speaker representations. This plan already provides a solution to the VC problem. By replacing each source frame with its corresponding frame given by the plan, we can solve the VC problem in real-time. We call this approach SinkVC.\
However, SinkVC, as well as kNN-VC, provides a discrete solution which may prove inefficient in cases where the amount of reference speech is limited. Denoting pairs given by a plan as $`{(x_{0},x_{1})} \sim \pi`$, we can train an FM algorithm ([6](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.E6 "In 3.3 Flow-Matching Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")) that generates a continuous OT map for VC (FMVC). OT-based Flow Matching predicts a target velocity field, which is defined by the OT interpolation between points. Formally, using an OT plan given by Sinkhorn, we sample pairs $`{(x_{0},x_{1})} \sim \pi`$ \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib20), [16](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib16)\] while minimize:

``` math
{{L_{OTM}{(\theta)}} = {{\mathbb{E}}_{{t,{(x_{0},x_{1})}} \sim \pi}{\|{{v_{\theta}{(t,x_{t})}} - {({x_{0} - x_{1}})}}\|}^{2}}}.
```

FMVC produces a flow induced by a neural velocity field $`{{(v_{t},\theta)},t} \in {\lbrack 0,1\rbrack}`$, constructing probability paths between individual data samples. The solution generates individual straight paths between source and target pairs. It has been shown that the obtained flow asymptotically approximates an OT map see Theorem 4.2 in \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib20)\]. A visual illustration is shown in Figure [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3.1 Conditional Neural Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters").

### 4.1 Dataset and Baselines

In this section we provide experiments using the common multi-speaker VCTK dataset  \[[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib24)\]. This dataset contains recorded speech data from 109 native English speakers, amounting to approximately 44 hours of speech. Similar to StarGANv2-VC, we used 20 randomly chosen speakers from the dataset for training. Data prepossessing details are given in Appendix (\wasyparagraph[B.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#A2.SS2 "B.2 Dataset Preprocessing ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")).\
We compared our method with the range of methods such as AutoVC \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib4)\], a 127M parameters pre-trained Diff-VCTK \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib7)\], and StarGANv2-VC \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib6)\]. In all experiments, we used pre-trained modes provided by the authors. The detailed explanation of experiments is given in Appendix (\wasyparagraph[B.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#A2.SS3 "B.3 Related Work and Baselines ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")).

### 4.2 Settings

For the transport map $`T_{\theta}`$, a UNET network with 46 M parameters was utilized. Meanwhile, for the potential $`f_{\psi}`$, a ResNet model with 55.1 M parameters was employed. AdamW \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib25)\] was selected as the optimizer for both the map $`T`$ and the potential $`f`$. Our methods were trained using Algorithm [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#alg1 "In B.1 Algorithm ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters") given in the Appendix, incorporating a quadratic cost $`{c{(x,y)}} = {\frac{1}{2}{|{x - y}|}_{2}^{2}}`$. The complete details of our training are available in Appendix (\wasyparagraph[B.5](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#A2.SS5 "B.5 Training ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")). For a fair comparison with StarGANv2-VC, we applied the JDC \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib26)\] model for $`F_{0}`$ and Parallel WaveGAN as vocoder \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib27)\]. In our many-to-many evaluation, speakers from the training dataset were used, but with their new, unseen utterances. For the any-to-many scenarios, also known as unseen-to-seen settings, we used an utterance from the previously unseen speakers as input and translated it into the speaking style of the 20 reference speakers from training. To evaluate the performance of the models, we computed the FAD \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib12)\], the Equal Error Rate (EER), and the perceptual Mean Opinion Score (pMOS) given by \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib28)\]. Further information about the metrics can be found in Appendix (\wasyparagraph[B.4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#A2.SS4 "B.4 Metrics ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")). In all Tables, an average score is reported.

## 5 Experiments within Audio Latent Space Representation

### 5.1 Dataset and Baselines

For a fair comparison with kNN-VC, we used the LibriSpeech test-clean set and sampled 200 utterances, allowing for 5 per speaker. We converted each utterance to the remaining 39 speakers, resulting in a total of 7800 outputs per model. We compared our method to the kNN-VC as well as other any-to-any voice conversion systems including VQMIVC \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib29)\], FreeVC \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib30)\], and YourTTS \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib31)\]. We applied the same settings as presented in kNN-VC experiments \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib8), Section 4\].

### 5.2 Settings

In addition to the metrics used in previous experiments, we also evaluated the word/character error rate (W/CER) of the converted speech using a pre-trained Whisper-base automatic speech recognition model \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib32)\], utilizing its default decoding parameters for transcription.\
In line with the baseline kNN-VC, we utilized features extracted from layer 6 of WavLM-Large \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib14)\], which generates a single vector for every 20 ms of 16 kHz audio. For SinkVC, we employed the Sinkhorn algorithm with a cost matrix determined by cosine similarity and entropy regularization set to 0.1. The four vectors with the highest scores in the recovered optimal plan were averaged to generate the resulting feature vector. We did not engage in pre-matching training \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib8)\] of the vocoder in our experiments and used default Hifi-GAN provided by \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib8)\]. For FMVC, we separated the speaker’s utterances into training and test categories. Subsequently, we trained the flow matching continuous map on pairs derived from the discrete Sinkhorn plan and tested on the unseen 100 utterance for the given speaker. A 3-layer MLP network was used to parameterize $`v_{\theta}{(t,x_{t})}`$, with an additional input for time $`t`$ and a hidden size of 512. A batch size of 1000 feature vectors was used for 1000 iterations, alongside the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $`0.001`$.

## 6 Conclusion

In contrast to StarGANv2-VC approach, our proposed method for mel-spectrogram representation is far simpler in terms of optimization, with its sole objective being to identify the Wasserstein-2 optimal transport map. We evaluated the quality of the speech conversion models using several automatic quality metrics (\wasyparagraph[4.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S4.SS3 "4.3 Results ‣ 4 Experiments with Mel-Spectogram Audio Representation ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters"))(\wasyparagraph[5.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S5.SS3 "5.3 Results ‣ 5 Experiments within Audio Latent Space Representation ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")). Compared to DiffVC, our method demonstrated superior performance according to the FAD metric and proved to be more resource-efficient (as shown in Table [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#A2.T4 "Table 4 ‣ B.1 Algorithm ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")). For the WavLM representation, we proposed a computationally lightweight FMVC approach (see Table [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S5.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 5.3 Results ‣ 5 Experiments within Audio Latent Space Representation ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")), which avoids the limitations of the SOTA any-to-any kNN-VC approach. We justify our algorithm by analyzing the recovered maps and showing that the learned map upper-bounded the FAD \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib12)\] between real and generated target data. We anticipate that our method will pave the way for future developments in the application of optimal transport in voice conversion tasks.

FAD is based on the audio embedding, from a pre-trained audio or speech recognition model on the real and converted speech signals. Lower FAD scores indicate that the converted voice is closer to the distribution of the real voice. We calculated the metric value separately for each speaker, and then calculated the average value for all speakers. As the original data, we calculated statistics on the training dataset, for the generated data, we randomly sampled 1000 audio examples from the test data and converted to the target speaker.\ EER: Equal Error Rate is calculating the False Acceptance Rate and False Rejection Rate of the voice recognition system and find the point at which they are equal. We chose WavLM Base+ \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib37)\] as the speaker verification model for all experiments. The Equal Error Rate provides a single value that balances these two rates against each other. In voice conversion models, an EER close to 0 would represent a high-performing model.\ pMOS: The Perceptual Mean Opinion Score is represents the average ratings given by human evaluators. Evaluators rate the quality of the voice on a scale, typically from 1 (worst) to 5 (best). Using the provided human demonstrations, neural approximation is built. Higher pMOS indicating a better performance. In experiments we used model proposed in \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib28)\]. ### B.5 Training For the NOT-VC and XNOT-VC, transport map network $`T_{\theta}`$ with 46.0 M parameters UNET neural network was used. For the potential $`f_{\omega}`$ 55.1 M parameters model was utilized. The optimization parameters: AdamW \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#bib.bib25)\] was chosen as the optimizer for both the map $`T`$ and the potential $`f`$, the learning rate equal to $`5 \cdot 10^{- 5}`$, weight decay was chosen to be $`1 \cdot 10^{- 10}`$. Learning rates of generator and potential is equal to 0.001. The number of generator optimization steps $`K_{T}`$ in algorithm[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#alg1 "In B.1 Algorithm ‣ Appendix B Mel-Spectogram Optimal Transport: Method and Evaluation Details ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters") was set to 10 per one potential $`f`$ parameters update. The batch size was equal to 5. The code is written using PyTorch framework and will be made publicly available. We trained two models, model with the loss ([4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.E4 "In 3.1 Conditional Neural Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")) called NOT-VC, and the model trained in the eXtremal formulation ([3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.02402v1#S3.Ex2 "3.2 Extremal Conditional Optimal Transport ‣ 3 Voice Conversion with Optimal Transport ‣ Optimal Transport Maps are Good Voice Converters")) with the parameter $`w = 12`$ called XNOT-VC.\ Additionally, to show that our approach is suitable for learning a mapping with a loss other than $`\ell^{2}`$, we have provided additional experiments using the ASR-based cost function. ### B.6 Testing We used two evaluation pipelines: the many-to-many and the any-to-many settings. In the many-to-many settings, we evaluated the performance of the models using the input and reference speakers from our training dataset, but on the new utterances of these speakers. In the any-to-many settings (often referred to as unseen-to-seen), we used as input an utterance from the a previously unseen speakers and converted it to the speaking style of the 20 reference speakers used in training. We computed the FAD metric by sampling 1000 examples from the target speakers. We then randomly selected 100 input samples from speakers in the test set. Each new sample was then converted to match the speech style of 10 reference speakers. We calculated the FAD by comparing the real target data with the generated data and reported the average.\ In the any-to-many setting, we followed the same procedure, but selected 100 samples from speakers not presented in the training set. We used the same data for the pMOs metric. To compute the EER, we sampled 50 unseen audio clips from each speaker in the training set. For each of these clips, we converted it to match all reference speaker sets, for a total of 20. Then we randomly sampled one audio clip from the training set for the reference speaker, called the target audio.
