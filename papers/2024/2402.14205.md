# Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer

Amit Kumar Singh Yadav$`^{\dagger}`$       Ziyue Xiang$`^{\dagger}`$       Kratika Bhagtani$`^{\dagger}`$       Paolo Bestagini$`^{\ddagger}`$ Stefano Tubaro$`^{\ddagger}`$       Edward J. Delp$`^{\dagger}`$ $`^{\dagger}`$Video and Image Processing Lab (VIPER), School of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Purdue University, West Lafayette, Indiana, USA $`^{\ddagger}`$Dipartimento di Elettronica, Informazione e Bioingegneria, Politecnico di Milano, Milano, Italy

###### Abstract

Many deep learning synthetic speech generation tools are readily available. The use of synthetic speech has caused financial fraud, impersonation of people, and misinformation to spread. For this reason forensic methods that can detect synthetic speech have been proposed. Existing methods often overfit on one dataset and their performance reduces substantially in practical scenarios such as detecting synthetic speech shared on social platforms. In this paper we propose, Patched Spectrogram Synthetic Speech Detection Transformer (PS3DT), a synthetic speech detector that converts a time domain speech signal to a mel-spectrogram and processes it in patches using a transformer neural network. We evaluate the detection performance of PS3DT on ASVspoof2019 dataset. Our experiments show that PS3DT performs well on ASVspoof2019 dataset compared to other approaches using spectrogram for synthetic speech detection. We also investigate generalization performance of PS3DT on In-the-Wild dataset. PS3DT generalizes well than several existing methods on detecting synthetic speech from an out-of-distribution dataset. We also evaluate robustness of PS3DT to detect telephone quality synthetic speech and synthetic speech shared on social platforms (compressed speech). PS3DT is robust to compression and can detect telephone quality synthetic speech better than several existing methods.

###### Index Terms:

synthetic speech detection, deep learning, signal processing, ASVspoof2019, transformer networks

## I Introduction

By “synthetic speech” we mean a human sounding speech signal generated using a model rather than an actual human speaker \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib2)\]. Conventional approaches for generating synthetic speech include simple waveform concatenation (WC) and source modeling (vocoders) \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib3)\]. Other approaches use neural networks \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib4), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib2)\]. The recent use of neural networks have reduced the perceptual difference between a synthetic speech and speech from an actual human speaker (often referred to as pristine or bona fide speech) \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib4), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib2)\]. Some methods can synthesize speech impersonating any person or language accent \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib5), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib6)\]. These methods are useful for voice applications such as voice assistants, e-learning, movies, and advertisement. But they have also been used to generate high quality speech for malicious purposes such as spreading misinformation \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib7)\], committing financial fraud, and impersonations of humans speaking \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib8)\]. For instance, synthetic speech was used for impersonation to commit a financial fraud worth \$40 million in 2021 \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib8)\]. A deepfake video of the president of Ukraine containing synthetic speech was used to support a misinformation campaign in 2022 \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib7)\].

There are several challenges in developing synthetic speech detection techniques. For example, a detector should work well on synthetic speech generated from unknown techniques \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib9)\]. Synthesized speech used to support misinformation campaigns are often shared on social media platforms such as YouTube. These platforms compress the speech signal using lossy compression standards such as Advanced Audio Coding (AAC) \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib11)\]. Hence, it is also important for a detector to be robust to compression. Some incidents report the use of synthetic speech over telephone channels to impersonate a person or fool Automatic Speaker Verification (ASV) systems. Telephone channels distort the speech signal due to compression, packet loss, and other artifacts resulting from different bandwidths, transmission infrastructures and data rates. Therefore, it is also important for a detector to be robust to detect synthetic speech over telephone channels.

In this paper, we propose a transformer based approach for synthetic speech detection using mel-spectrograms. Existing methods using transformers for synthetic speech detection either process mel-spectrogram first using Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and then a transformer \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib12)\], or they process all the regions of the mel-spectrogram together \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib13)\]. Our proposed method Patched Spectrogram Synthetic Speech Detection Transformer (PS3DT) processes the mel-spectrogram \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib14)\] in patches and then rearranges patch representations corresponding to same temporal location together. PS3DT shows promising detection results on ASVspoof2019 dataset compared to several existing approaches for synthetic speech detection using spectrograms \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib15)\], mel-spectrograms \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib14)\] or their derivatives. Moreover, PS3DT generalizes well compared to several existing approaches to detect synthetic speech from an out-of-distribution In-the-Wild dataset \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib16)\]. Finally, our experiments on ASVspoof2021 dataset \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\] show that PS3DT is more robust to compression and detecting synthetic speech over telephone than existing methods.

## II Related Work

Conventional approaches to detect synthetic speech are based on classifiers using hand crafted features or time-frequency representations of the speech signal, e.g., Constant-Q Transform (CQT) \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib18)\], Constant Q Cepstral Coefficients (CQCCs) \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib19)\], Mel Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs) \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib20)\], and Linear Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (LFCCs) \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib18)\]. These approaches require tedious feature selection procedures \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib21)\]. Other approaches are based on analyzing the speech signal in the time domain. For example, Guang et al.  propose a neural network that uses time domain speech and processes it as a sequence of information using a recurrent neural network \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib22)\]. They show promising results as compared to the hand-crafted feature based approaches. Another family of approaches rely on the use of CNNs, treating time-frequency audio representations as images. Bartusiak et al.  \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib12)\] and Subramani et al. \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib23)\] show that spectrograms and computer vision approaches can be used for detecting synthetic speech. A spectrogram is a 2D representation of a speech signal \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib15)\]. The horizontal axis represents time and the vertical axis represents frequency \[[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib24), [25](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib25)\]. If the frequency axis is in the mel scale and not in the Hertz scale, it is known as a mel-spectrogram \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib14)\]. The conversion between Hertz frequency scale $`f_{\text{Hz}}`$ and the mel frequency scale $`f_{\text{mel}}`$ \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib14)\] is obtained by

$`{f_{\text{mel}} = {2595 \cdot {\log_{10}\left( {1 + \frac{f_{\text{Hz}}}{700}} \right)}}}.`$

Recently, transformer networks have been used with spectrograms \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib15), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib14)\] for general audio classification tasks \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib26), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib27), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib28)\]. Gong et al. proposed an audio spectrogram transformer, which was further improved by augmenting self-supervision \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib26), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib27), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib28)\]. Bartusiak et al. \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib13)\] and Müller et al. \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib16)\] process spectrogram and mel-spectrogram using transformers for synthetic speech detection on ASVspoof2019 dataset. These approaches either process the spectrogram first using CNNs and then use a transformer, or they process all regions of spectrogram together. Koutini et al. showed that processing spectrogram in patches can lead to higher performance in general audio classification tasks such as speaker recognition and environment sound classification. Gong et al. referred to frames as the set of all the patches in the spectrogram that correspond to the same temporal location and showed that frame representation leads to better performance on speech processing tasks than using patch representation. Motivated by the promising performance of frame representation in general audio classification \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib26), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib27)\] and speech attribution tasks \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib29), [30](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib30), [31](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib31)\], we propose Patched Spectrogram Synthetic Speech Detection Transformer (PS3DT). Our proposed method processes mel-spectrogram in patches and then combines the representation from all the patches corresponding to same temporal location to get frame representation.

## III Proposed Approach

We propose Patched Spectrogram Synthetic Speech Detection Transformer (PS3DT) for detecting synthetic speech signals. The block diagram of our proposed PS3DT is shown in [Fig. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ I Introduction ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). Let $`\overset{\rightarrow}{x}`$ be the time domain speech signal, we compute the magnitude of its Short Time Fourier Transform using a Hanning window of size 25 ms with a shift of 10 ms to obtain the mel-spectrogram \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib15)\]. Stevens et al. showed that the mel-scale correlates better with the human auditory system w.r.t to the frequency content than the Hz scale \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib14)\]. Mel-spectrograms have been used in general audio classification tasks \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib26), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib27), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib28)\] and we use them in PS3DT. Following previous work \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib28)\], we consider $`80`$ frequency bins for creating the mel-spectrogram. The size of the input speech signal is fixed to $`5.12`$ seconds, resulting in a mel-spectrogram of size $`80 \times 512`$. If the speech signal is less than $`5.12`$ seconds, we repeat the speech signal. We divide the mel-spectrogram into patches of size $`16 \times 16`$. We obtain $`N = 160`$ patches. Using a linear layer, we convert each patch to a 768-dimensional vector representation $`{{\overset{\rightarrow}{e}}_{i},i} \in {\{ 1,2,\ldots,N\}}`$ (see [Fig. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ I Introduction ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer")). To each patch representation (i.e., $`{\overset{\rightarrow}{e}}_{i}`$), we add a 768-dimensional vector $`{\overset{\rightarrow}{p}}_{i}`$ corresponding to the position of the patch.

The resultant 768-dimensional representation for each patch (i.e., $`{\overset{\rightarrow}{e}}_{i} + {\overset{\rightarrow}{p}}_{i}`$) is processed by a transformer encoder to get $`{{\overset{\rightarrow}{o}}_{i},i} \in {\{ 1,2,\ldots,N\}}`$ ([Fig. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ I Introduction ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer")). The architecture of the transformer encoder is similar to the masked transformer encoder proposed for image classification \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib32)\] and for general audio classification \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib28)\]. The transformer encoder has a depth of 12 layers and uses 12 attention heads to obtain the patch representation. To avoid training the transformer from scratch we initialized our transformer encoder with pre-trained weights from \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib28)\]. Existing methods using transformer and spectrogram for synthetic speech detection mainly process all the regions of the spectrogram together and do not process patch representations \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib13)\]. Contrary to existing methods, PS3DT uses patch representations. It rearranges patch representations to get frame representations. The rearrangement is done by concatenating all the patch representations corresponding to same temporal position as shown in [Fig. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ I Introduction ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). This results in a frame representation (i.e., $`{{\overset{\rightarrow}{f}}_{i},i} \in {\{ 1,2,\ldots,L\}}`$, and $`L = 32`$) ([Fig. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ I Introduction ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer")). We use the frame based representation because it has shown good performance in speech tasks such as synthetic speech attribution \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib29)\] and general speech recognition tasks such as speaker identification \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib26)\]. Also, we believe this makes PS3DT less sensitive to noise in one patch.

Finally, using a mean pooling layer we estimate the mean of all $`{\overset{\rightarrow}{f_{i}},i} \in {\{ 1,2,\ldots,L\}}`$ to get $`\overset{\rightarrow}{f}`$ as shown in [Fig. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ I Introduction ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). We process the mean frame representation $`\overset{\rightarrow}{f}`$ using a Multi Layer Perceptron Network (MLP) network that consists of two linear layers separated by ReLU activation and a sigmoid activation at the last layer. The MLP network ([Fig. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ I Introduction ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer")) provides two probabilities as outputs: $`p_{1}`$ and $`p_{2}`$ representing the probability of the speech signal being bona fide and synthetic, respectively. To make the final decision on the input speech, we compare the two probabilities. If the probability of the speech signal being bona fide is higher w.r.t it being synthetic (i.e., $`p_{1} > p_{2}`$), we classify it as bona fide. Otherwise, we classify it as synthetic.

We use Binary Cross Entropy (BCE) loss for training because of its better performance in our experiments. We trained for 50 epochs using a batch size of 256 and AdamW optimizer \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib33)\] with an initial learning rate of $`10^{- 5}`$ and a weight decay of $`10^{- 4}`$. We select the model weights which provide best accuracy on the validation set for evaluation.

## IV Experimental Results

In this section, we describe the datasets used in each of our experiments, our experiments and discuss the results. We use Equal Error Rate (EER) \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\] as the performance metric for all of our experiments. It is an official metric adopted by each of the datasets on which we evaluate our method. We obtain EER from Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve by finding the rate where False Negative Rate (FNR) and False Positive Rate (FPR) are equal. Lower the EER, the better the performance of the method. A perfect detector will have EER of 0% and a random classifier will have EER of 50%.

### IV-A Training and Validation Dataset

We use Logical Access (LA) part of the ASVspoof2019 dataset \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1), [34](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib34)\] for training and validation. In total, there are 121,461 bona fide and synthetic speech signals divided with an approximate ratio of 1:1:3 into training set $`D_{tr}`$, validation set $`D_{dev}`$, and evaluation set $`D_{eval}`$ \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\]. Synthesized speech signals are generated using three diverse techniques - neural networks, vocoders, and waveform concatenation \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib34)\]. $`D_{tr}`$ and $`D_{dev}`$ contain approximately 89% synthesized and 11% bona fide speech signals. The synthesized speech signals in $`D_{tr}`$ and $`D_{dev}`$ are generated from 6 different synthesizers (A01-A06). Bona fide speech signals in each set are recorded from diverse human speakers that do not overlap among the two sets. All speech signals are monophonic and lossless encoded using Free Lossless Audio Codec (FLAC) format.

### IV-B Experiment 1: Detection 

In this experiment, we investigate the detection performance of Patched Spectrogram Synthetic Speech Detection Transformer (PS3DT) on ASVspoof2019 dataset \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\] and compare the performance with existing methods. For evaluation, we use the evaluation set $`D_{eval}`$ of the ASVspoof2019 dataset \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\]. Section [IV-B1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.SS2.SSS1 "IV-B1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2019) ‣ IV-B Experiment 1: Detection ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") provides details about the evaluation set $`D_{eval}`$ \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\] of the ASVspoof2019 dataset and Section [IV-B2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.SS2.SSS2 "IV-B2 Detection Performance ‣ IV-B Experiment 1: Detection ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") discusses our experimental results on detecting synthetic speech signals.

#### IV-B1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2019)

The evaluation set $`D_{eval}`$ contains approximately 7.4k bona fide speech signals and 63.9k synthetic speech signals. The synthetic speech is generated from 13 different synthesizers (A07-A19). The synthesizers A16 and A19 have same underlying architectures as A04 and A06 synthesizers present in the training $`D_{tr}`$ and validation set $`D_{dev}`$ of the ASVspoof2019 dataset. The remaining 11 synthesizers in the evaluation set $`D_{eval}`$ are unknown synthesizers i.e., they have different underlying architectures than any synthesizers present in $`D_{dev}`$ or $`D_{tr}`$. Overall, out of 63.9k synthesized speech signals in $`D_{eval}`$, 61.5k are generated from unknown synthesizers. The bona fide speech signals in $`D_{eval}`$ are recorded from human speakers that do not overlap with the speakers in the $`D_{tr}`$ and $`D_{dev}`$. Similar to $`D_{tr}`$ and $`D_{dev}`$, all speech signals in $`D_{eval}`$ are monophonic and lossless encoded using the Free Lossless Audio Codec (FLAC) format.

#### IV-B2 Detection Performance

First, we report the detection performance of PS3DT on the union of the $`D_{dev}`$ and the $`D_{eval}`$ sets. It contains a total of 19 synthesizers (A01-A19). Synthesizers A01-A06 are present in $`D_{dev}`$ while A07-A19 are present in $`D_{eval}`$. [Fig. 2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ II Related Work ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") shows the performance of PS3DT on different synthesizers and bona fide speech. Our results show that PS3DT has perfect detection accuracy for synthetic speech from known synthesizers that were also present in the training set (A01-A06). PS3DT even generalizes to unknown synthesizers and has almost perfect detection accuracy for synthetic speech from all of them except the $`A17`$ synthesizer. $`A17`$ is also found as one of the most challenging class in the ASVspoof2019 Challenge result \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\]. A possible reason for poor performance on $`A17`$ could be small duration of speech signal to analyze. On average, signals from $`A17`$ have 26% silence region in the start of the signal. This is significantly higher than typical 8% silence region in the start in other unknown synthesizers e.g., $`A14`$. Note that on average both of them have approximately same total duration of 3.4 seconds.

Second, we report the Equal Error Rate (EER) of PS3DT and compare it with that of 16 existing synthetic speech detection methods on the ASVspoof2019 dataset. The proposed Patched Spectrogram Synthetic Speech Detection Transformer (PS3DT) uses mel-spectrogram to detect synthetic speech. Therefore, for comparison, we only include methods that either use spectrogram or mel-spectrogram or features obtained from spectrogram ($`S01`$- $`S14`$). We also include the two baseline methods proposed and provided in the ASVspoof2019 Challenge ($`B01`$ and $`B02`$). The two baseline comparison methods $`B01`$ and $`B02`$ process hand-crafted features like Constant Q Cepstral Coefficients (CQCCs), and Linear Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (LFCCs) using a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The remaining methods that we use for comparison convert the time domain signal to a spectrogram by either using Short Time Fourier Transform (STFT) or using Constant-Q Transform (CQT). We refer to the spectrogram obtained using STFT and CQT as Spectrogram and Spectrogram$`_{CQT}`$, respectively in [Table I](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T1 "TABLE I ‣ IV-B2 Detection Performance ‣ IV-B Experiment 1: Detection ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). Both of them are 2D spectral representations obtained from the time-domain speech signal. The Spectrogram is obtained applying the Fourier transform to short overlapping segments of the signal and is computationally efficient. The Spectrogram$`_{CQT}`$ is obtained by decomposing the signal into a series of sinusoidal components that are logarithmically spaced in frequency. It is more computationally demanding than the Spectrogram but the frequency resolution is not dependent on the length of segment as it does not process the signal segment by segment. Müller et al. \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib16)\] showed that for a fixed neural network as in $`S07`$ to $`S13`$ using Spectrogram$`_{CQT}`$ results in better performance. Therefore, for comparison we only include versions of $`S07`$ to $`S13`$ that use Spectrogram$`_{CQT}`$ to detect synthetic speech.

Some methods compute logarithm of the spectrogram (shown as Log-Spectrogram in Table [I](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T1 "TABLE I ‣ IV-B2 Detection Performance ‣ IV-B Experiment 1: Detection ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer")) or convert the spectrogram to mel-scale (shown as Mel-Spectrogram in Table [I](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T1 "TABLE I ‣ IV-B2 Detection Performance ‣ IV-B Experiment 1: Detection ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer")). Methods $`S05`$ and $`S06`$ further process the Spectrogram$`_{CQT}`$ or inverted Spectrogram$`_{CQT}`$ using octave subbanding and Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) to obtain Constant-Q Octave Subband Transform (CQ-OST) and Inverted Constant-Q Cepstral Coefficients (ICQCC) features, respectively. Method $`S04`$ uses multiple features together, it uses both Spectrogram and CQT \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib15)\] features to detect synthetic speech. We refer to it as Spectrogram$`+ {CQT}`$ in [Table I](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T1 "TABLE I ‣ IV-B2 Detection Performance ‣ IV-B Experiment 1: Detection ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). Each of the $`S01`$ to $`S14`$ methods use a neural network to process the spectrogram or its derivatives. Similar to our proposed PS3DT, methods $`S07`$ and $`S14`$ use transformer neural networks to detect synthetic speech. $`S14`$ is the best performing transformer neural network based method proposed in \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib13)\]. We select it as a representative of all the transformer based methods proposed in \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib13)\]. $`S14`$ is first trained using self-supervision on a large audio dataset i.e., Audioset \[[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib35)\]. Then using supervised learning, it is finetuned on ASVspoof2019 dataset \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\] to detect synthetic speech. Methods $`S01`$ and $`S04`$ are proposed in \[[36](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib36)\] and they use VGG neural network \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib37)\] and SincNet \[[36](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib36)\]. Details about the neural networks used in $`S02`$, and $`S07`$ to $`S13`$ can be found in \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib16)\]. Method $`S03`$ \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib38)\] uses a 3-layer Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). Methods $`S05`$ \[[39](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib39)\] and $`S06`$ \[[40](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib40)\] use Deep Neural Network (DNN) to detect synthetic speech.

We compare the performance of these 16 methods with PS3DT. All 16 methods are trained, validated, and tested on the original $`D_{train}`$, $`D_{dev}`$, and $`D_{eval}`$ sets of the ASVspoof2019 dataset \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\]. The results of this experiment are shown in [Table I](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T1 "TABLE I ‣ IV-B2 Detection Performance ‣ IV-B Experiment 1: Detection ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). PS3DT has around 3.5 percentage points and 5 percentage points improvement over the baseline methods $`B01`$ and $`B02`$, respectively. It has more than 5 percentage points improvement in EER from spectrogram methods $`S02`$ and $`S03`$ using VGG and CNN neural networks, respectively. Compared to methods $`S07`$ and $`S14`$, which process Spectrogram$`_{CQT}`$ and Mel-Spectrogram using transformer neural networks, the improvement is around 3 percentage points and 0.7 percentage points, respectively. From performance of $`S14`$, we can also note that using Mel-Spectrogram and large scale transformer such as Patchout faSt Spectrogram Transformer (PaSST) \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib27)\] results in better performance than even methods such as $`S13`$ and $`S04`$, which use fusion of neural networks for processing spectrogram. Overall PS3DT has highest performance among the 14 different spectrogram based methods and the 2 baselines on ASVspoof2019 dataset.

### IV-C Experiment 2 - Generalization

Existing work reports that speech signals from unknown synthesizers in ASVspoof2019 have similar silence distributions \[[41](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib41)\]. Some methods overfit on ASVspoof2019 dataset and use silence distribution instead of using features related to spoofing to detect synthetic speech \[[41](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib41)\]. Such methods can have high performance on detecting synthetic speech from unknown synthesizers in the ASVspoof2019 dataset. However, these methods are not good, as a simple attack such as removing silence can drastically drop their performance. Müller et al. proposed In-the-Wild dataset \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib16)\] to better investigate the generalization performance of methods trained on ASVspoof2019 dataset \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\]. In Experiment 2, we use this dataset to estimate the generalization performance of PS3DT. Note that for this experiment, we do not retrain PS3DT or any existing methods. We just evaluate cross dataset performance on the In-the-Wild dataset \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib16)\]. We compared the generalization performance of PS3DT with top 8 existing detection methods ($`S07`$ to $`S14`$) discussed in Experiment 1 ([Table I](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T1 "TABLE I ‣ IV-B2 Detection Performance ‣ IV-B Experiment 1: Detection ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer")). The details about In-the-Wild dataset are provided in Section [IV-C1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.SS3.SSS1 "IV-C1 Evaluation Dataset (In-the-Wild) ‣ IV-C Experiment 2 - Generalization ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") and Section [IV-C2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.SS3.SSS2 "IV-C2 Generalization Performance ‣ IV-C Experiment 2 - Generalization ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") describes the EER performance of PS3DT and existing methods on In-the-Wild dataset.

#### IV-C1 Evaluation Dataset (In-the-Wild)

The In-the-Wild dataset \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib16)\] contains approximately 17 hours of high-quality synthetic speech impersonating 58 English-speaking politicians and celebrities and 21 hours of bona fide speech for the same politicians and celebrities. They are downloaded from social networks and popular video sharing platforms. The bona fide and synthetic speech are from diverse speakers, having different language accents, styles, and emotions. The bona fide speech and the corresponding synthetic speech for a speaker have similar background noise, emotions, and duration. Each speech signal has an average length of 4.3 seconds and is transcoded to wav file format after downloading. Each speech signal is converted to 8 bits and downsampled to 16 kHz.

#### IV-D1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 LA)

We use the evaluation set of the Logical Access (LA) part of ASVspoof2021 dataset \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\] for this experiment. It contains 148K bona fide and synthetic speech signals. The dataset is created by transmitting each speech signal in the evaluation set of the ASVspoof2019 dataset that we used in Experiment 1 over real telephone systems. Two different systems were used for transmission namely, voice-over-internet-protocol (VoIP) system and a public switched telephone network (PSTN). The distance between the two endpoints of the telephone channel is significantly large e.g., one endpoint is hosted in France and the other endpoint is either in Italy or Singapore. More details about the dataset can be found in  \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\].

### IV-E Experiment 4: Robustness to Compression

In this experiment, we investigate the performance of PS3DT when deployed in practical scenarios such as authenticating speech signals uploaded on social platforms. We use the DeepFake (DF) part of the ASVspoof2021 dataset \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\] for our evaluation. Section [IV-E1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.SS5.SSS1 "IV-E1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 DF) ‣ IV-E Experiment 4: Robustness to Compression ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") provides more details about the dataset and Section [IV-E2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.SS5.SSS2 "IV-E2 Robustness to Compression Performance ‣ IV-E Experiment 4: Robustness to Compression ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") describes our results.

#### IV-E1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 DF)

We use the DeepFake (DF) part of the ASVspoof2021 dataset for this experiment \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\]. It contains approximately 612K bona fide and synthetic speech signals. The synthetic speech is generated from more than 100 different synthesizers. The speech signals are lossy encoded using different standards and using both high and low variable data/bit rates. The uncompressed speech samples are obtained by a union of speech samples from three datasets, namely the evaluation set of ASVspoof2019 LA dataset \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\], the 2018 and 2020 Voice Conversion Challenge (VCC) datasets \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib42), [43](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib43)\]. Depending on the encoding standard and data rate used for compression, there are nine partitions in the dataset referred to as nine different conditions ($`{DF} - {C1}`$ to $`{DF} - {C9}`$). The uncompressed partition of speech set is referred as $`{DF} - {C1}`$ in Table [IV](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T4 "TABLE IV ‣ IV-D2 Telephone Quality Robustness Performance ‣ IV-D Experiment 3 - Telephone Quality Robustness ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). The other eight partitions are created by compressing $`{DF} - {C1}`$ partition using different standards and data rates. Table [IV](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T4 "TABLE IV ‣ IV-D2 Telephone Quality Robustness Performance ‣ IV-D Experiment 3 - Telephone Quality Robustness ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") shows compression standards and variable bit rates used in each of the conditions. Notice that conditions $`{DF} - {C2}`$ to $`{DF} - {C7}`$ are encoding uncompressed speech to compressed speech. While conditions $`{DF} - {C8}`$ and $`{DF} - {C9}`$ first encode the uncompressed speech signal to compressed speech using a particular compression standard and then further transcode compressed speech signal to Advanced Audio Coding (AAC) \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib10)\] compression standard. Together, the conditions $`{DF} - {C1}`$ to $`{DF} - {C9}`$ represent diverse compression scenarios which occur when synthetic speech is uploaded on different social platforms. For instance, YouTube typically uses AAC \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib11)\].

#### IV-E2 Robustness to Compression Performance

For investigating robustness of PS3DT to compression, we use the version of PS3DT trained on $`D_{tr}`$ set of ASVspoof2019 dataset \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib1)\] and evaluated its performance on the ASVspoof2021 DF dataset (described in detail in Section [IV-E1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.SS5.SSS1 "IV-E1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 DF) ‣ IV-E Experiment 4: Robustness to Compression ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer")). First, in Table [IV](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T4 "TABLE IV ‣ IV-D2 Telephone Quality Robustness Performance ‣ IV-D Experiment 3 - Telephone Quality Robustness ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") we show performance of PS3DT on each of the nine partitions of the dataset. As expected the performance of PS3DT is good on uncompressed conditions i.e., $`{DF} - {C1}`$. Also, out of MP3, OGG, and AAC compression standards \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib10), [44](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib44)\] PS3DT is most robust to AAC compression that is used by several social platforms such as YouTube and Twitter \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib11)\]. Surprisingly, the performance is better on AAC compressed signal than on uncompressed speech signal. A possible reason could be that AAC compression is one of the most efficient and recent compression techniques \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib10)\]. The spectral processing in it includes blocks to process synthetic speech differently that might be making spoofing artifacts more evident in the spectrogram \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib10)\]. Some previous work show that AAC compression has information in the encoding bit stream that can even help to detect synthetic speech \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib30)\]. PS3DT is least robust to MP3 compression standard. Also, as expected lower data rates during compression will distort speech signal more and hence PS3DT has lower performance for all MP3, OGG, and AAC compression standards at lower data rates as compared to that for higher data rates. Further, the performance is lower on transcoding where multiple compressions are done than on single compression i.e., encoding. Out of transcoding conditions i.e., $`{DF} - {C8}`$ and $`{DF} - {C9}`$, the performance is lower if the first compression is done using MP3 standard (i.e., in $`{DF} - {C8}`$) as compared to that if the first compression is done using OGG standard. A possible reason could be that single low rate MP3 compression reduces performance of PS3DT more significantly as observed by lowest performance in $`{DF} - {C2}`$ condition. We also found accuracy of PS3DT for each condition in [Table IV](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T4 "TABLE IV ‣ IV-D2 Telephone Quality Robustness Performance ‣ IV-D Experiment 3 - Telephone Quality Robustness ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). The accuracy is the ratio of the total correct classifications to the total number of classifications. There are around 68K bona fide and synthetic speech signals in each condition out of which 65K speech signals are synthetic. For each condition, PS3DT has accuracy of 97% or higher.

Second, we compare the robustness of PS3DT to detect compressed synthetic speech with other methods. Table [V](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T5 "TABLE V ‣ IV-E1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 DF) ‣ IV-E Experiment 4: Robustness to Compression ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") compares the robustness to compression performance of $`S14`$ and the same 4 baselines used in Experiment 3 and provided in ASVspoof2021 Challenge\[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\]. Among all the baselines ($`B01`$ to $`B04`$), the method $`B04`$ has best robustness performance (EER of 22.38%). $`S14`$ has an EER of 24.37%. Comparing these results with robustness to detect synthetic speech over telephone in [Table III](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T3 "TABLE III ‣ IV-D1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 LA) ‣ IV-D Experiment 3 - Telephone Quality Robustness ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"), we observe that if one method is more robust than other method in detecting synthetic speech over telephone then it may not necessarily be more robust in detecting compressed synthetic speech. For instance, baseline methods $`B03`$ and method $`S14`$ are more robust than baseline $`B04`$ in [Table III](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T3 "TABLE III ‣ IV-D1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 LA) ‣ IV-D Experiment 3 - Telephone Quality Robustness ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"), however, their performance is lower than that of $`B04`$ in [Table V](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T5 "TABLE V ‣ IV-E1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 DF) ‣ IV-E Experiment 4: Robustness to Compression ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer"). PS3DT performs consistently better than all the baseline methods and method $`S14`$ in both [Table III](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T3 "TABLE III ‣ IV-D1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 LA) ‣ IV-D Experiment 3 - Telephone Quality Robustness ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer") and [Table V](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#S4.T5 "TABLE V ‣ IV-E1 Evaluation Dataset (ASVspoof2021 DF) ‣ IV-E Experiment 4: Robustness to Compression ‣ IV Experimental Results ‣ Compression Robust Synthetic Speech Detection Using Patched Spectrogram Transformer").

Overall, PS3DT performs significantly better than all the baselines $`B01`$ to $`B04`$ and method $`S14`$ with EER 16.61%. We also compared robustness of PS3DT with 29 methods other than the baseline methods submitted to ASVspoof2021 DeepFake Challenge \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\]. These methods do not necessarily use spectrogram but use other approaches e.g., fusion of several features to detect synthetic speech. More details about them can be found in \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\]. The least performing method has an EER of 29.75% while the best performing method has EER of 15.64%. PS3DT has third best performance with an EER of 16.61% as compared to all the 29 methods reported in \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.14205v1#bib.bib17)\]. Therefore, PS3DT has high robustness to compression and hence can detect synthetic speech shared on social platforms to spread misinformation or impersonate a person.

## V Conclusion

In this paper we proposed Patched Spectrogram Synthetic Speech Detection Transformer (PS3DT) for synthetic speech detection. PS3DT has perfect detection accuracy for 6 known and 10 out of 11 unknown speech synthesizers. PS3DT does better than previous methods which use either spectrogram or its derivatives for synthetic speech detection. We show that PS3DT generalizes better than existing methods and works better in practical scenarios such as detecting synthetic speech shared on social platforms and telephone quality synthetic speech. In future, we will work on localization of partially synthetic speech. We also plan to explore a multimodal approach that uses both spectral representation e.g., mel-spectrogram, and time-domain speech signal for detection.
