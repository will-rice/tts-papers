# SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models

Dongchao Yang, Rongjie Huang, Yuanyuan Wang, Haohan Guo, Dading Chong, Songxiang Liu,\
Xixin Wu, Helen Meng Dongchao Yang, Rongjie Huang, Yuanyuan Wang, Haohan Guo, Xixin Wu, and Helen Meng are with the Chinese University of Hong Kong.Songxiang Liu and Dading Chong are independent researcher.Helen Meng is the corresponding author.

###### Abstract

Scaling Text-to-speech (TTS) to large-scale datasets has been demonstrated as an effective method for improving the diversity and naturalness of synthesized speech. At the high level, previous large-scale TTS models can be categorized into either Auto-regressive (AR) based (e.g., VALL-E) or Non-auto-regressive (NAR) based models (e.g., NaturalSpeech 2/3). Although these works demonstrate good performance, they still have potential weaknesses. For instance, AR-based models are plagued by unstable generation quality and slow generation speed; meanwhile, some NAR-based models need phoneme-level duration alignment information, thereby increasing the complexity of data pre-processing, model design, and loss design. In this work, we build upon our previous publication by implementing a simple and efficient non-autoregressive (NAR) TTS framework, termed SimpleSpeech 2. SimpleSpeech 2 effectively combines the strengths of both autoregressive (AR) and non-autoregressive (NAR) methods, offering the following key advantages: (1) simplified data preparation; (2) straightforward model and loss design; and (3) stable, high-quality generation performance with fast inference speed. Compared to our previous publication, we present (i) a detailed analysis of the influence of speech tokenizer and noisy label for TTS performance; (ii) four distinct types of sentence duration predictors; (iii) a novel flow-based scalar latent transformer diffusion model. With these improvement, we show a significant improvement in generation performance and generation speed compared to our previous work and other state-of-the-art (SOTA) large-scale TTS models. Furthermore, we show that SimpleSpeech 2 can be seamlessly extended to multilingual TTS by training it on multilingual speech datasets. Demos are available on: https://dongchaoyang.top/SimpleSpeech2_demo/.

Text-to-speech, Audio Codec, Audio generation, diffusion model

## I Introduction

Text-to-Speech synthesis (TTS) endeavors to synthesize speech that is both intelligible and natural at a human level, a field that has witnessed significant advancements over the past few years due to the proliferation of deep learning technologies \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib1), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib2), [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib3), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib4), [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib5), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib6), [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib7)\]. These TTS models have successfully synthesized speech that is not only intelligible but also of high quality, albeit generally limited to specific styles, speakers, and languages. This limitation primarily stems from the fact that they are trained on small-scale, high-quality, labeled speech datasets. At a high level of modeling, previous TTS models are usually categorized into two types: auto-regressive (AR) and non-auto-regressive (NAR) models. Generally, NAR-based models outperform AR-based models in terms of generation speed and robustness \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib2)\]. However, AR-based models exhibit superior diversity, prosody, expressiveness, and flexibility, attributable to their implicit duration modeling and AR sampling strategy \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib1)\]. Furthermore, many NAR-based models \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib2)\] depend on fine-grained alignment information (phoneme-level duration), which complicates data pre-processing. Although numerous studies \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib5), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib4), [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib3)\] have suggested employing the Monotonic Alignment Search (MAS) strategy to learn alignment information, this approach not only increases training complexity but also results in alignments that may not always be suitable. Most critically, the rigid boundary between phoneme and speech representation can lead to unnatural prosody \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib8)\].

Recently, an increasing number of researchers have concentrated on synthesizing diverse and natural speech, particularly focusing on the diversity of speaking styles, achieving human-level naturalness, and zero-shot speaker cloning. To achieve these objectives, they propose utilizing large-scale datasets to train Text-to-Speech (TTS) models \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib9), [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib11), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib12), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib13)\]. For example, language model (LM)-based TTS systems, such as VALL-E \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib10)\], employ a pre-trained audio codec model (Encodec \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib14)\]) to convert the speech signal into a sequence of discrete tokens. Subsequently, an auto-regressive (AR) language model is trained to predict these speech tokens based on phonemes and speaker prompts. LM-based TTS models streamline data pre-processing, and their structure and training strategies draw from the successes of large language models in natural language processing (NLP) \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib15), [16](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib16)\]. Moreover, these models are capable of generating expressive and diverse speech, thanks to their implicit duration modeling and sampling strategy. However, they suffer from slow and unstable generation due to the nature of AR sampling. To mitigate these drawbacks, large-scale non-autoregressive (NAR) models \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib17), [18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18), [19](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib19), [20](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib20)\] have been proposed, such as NaturalSpeech 2/3. Although NAR-based large-scale TTS models exhibit higher inference speeds and enhanced stability, they require extra effort in data pre-processing, such as using their internal alignment tools to secure the alignment between speech and phonemes \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18), [21](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib21)\]. Additionally, the persistent issue of hard boundary alignment continues to limit the natural prosody in NAR-based models \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib8)\]. We summarize the strengths and potential weaknesses for current AR-based and NAR-based large-scale TTS models in Table [I](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S1.T1 "TABLE I ‣ I Introduction ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"). We can see that both AR-based and NAR-based large TTS models still have considerable room for improvement in terms of performance, efficiency, or data requirements. Consequently, the primary research question is whether we can develop a simple, efficient, and stable TTS system capable of synthesizing diverse, natural, and controllable speech.

However, previous models pay more attention to improving the performance of TTS systems by scaling the training data and model size. In this study, we propose to focus on two aspects of the speech tokenizer:

Then we give a detailed analysis of the influence of completeness and compactness on speech generation.

Large-scale datasets are crucial for the development of advanced Text-to-Speech (TTS) systems \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib10), [18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18), [21](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib21), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib12), [19](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib19), [22](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib22)\]. While prior studies have demonstrated that large-scale Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) datasets can be employed to train TTS systems, the details of obtaining text-speech pairs are usually neglected. In this study, we utilize publicly available ASR models (Whisper \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib23)\]), to generate the transcriptions. However, these transcriptions may be inaccurate and are thus considered noisy labels. ## II Related work and background

### II-A Speech Tokenizer

In this study, we define an audio/speech tokenizer as a model that maps complex audio data into a latent space (latent representation), which can subsequently be reconstructed from this latent space back into audio. The latent representation may take the form of either discrete tokens or continuous vectors. Within the existing literature, the concept of an audio tokenizer has been extensively investigated \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib26), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib14), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib27), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib28), [29](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib29)\]. These studies generally fall into two categories: Audio Codecs and Variance AutoEncoders (VAEs). Audio Codecs aim to quantize audio data into a set of discrete tokens, whereas VAEs are designed to model the mean and variance distributions of audio data.

Although audio codec models have been widely used in audio generation tasks, they face the problem of codebook collapse and need complex training loss combinations. Furthermore, the audio codec quantizes each speech frame into multiple tokens with RVQ, this will ensure high-quality audio reconstruction but will cause difficulty in autoregressive model generation \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib11)\] (error propagation and robust issues) due to the increased length of the token sequence. The VAE model or the continuous representations from audio codec are also can be used as the prediction target of generative models. For instance, previous works \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18), [30](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib30)\] follow the Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib31)\] to train a diffusion model to fit the distribution of continuous representations. However, developing a good VAE model also requires balancing the variance level with the reconstruction performance. A typical example is that data compressed with AE might reconstruct better than those with VAE, but the complexity retained in the compressed data distribution still poses challenges for the generative model. Thus, current VAE models in the audio and image generation fields imposes a slight Kullback-Leibler (KL) penalty towards a standard normal on the learned latent \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib31), [29](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib29)\]. In this study, we propose to use scalar quantization to replace the Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ) in previous audio codec works, named SQ-Codec. We demonstrate that SQ-Codec not only achieves good reconstruction performance but also provides a finite, compact latent space, which is highly suitable for generative models.

### II-B Large-scale text-to-speech

Large-scale text-to-speech (TTS) research has made significant strides. These methods can be divided into two categories: autoregressive (AR) TTS based on large language models (LLMs) and non-autoregressive (NAR) TTS. AR-based TTS models got great attention, for instance, AudioLM \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib9)\], VALL-E \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib10)\], SpearTTS \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib11)\], VoiceCraft \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib22)\], and UniAudio \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib12)\] use audio codec (e.g. Encodec \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib14)\] or SoundStream \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib27)\]) for mapping speech to tokens, framing text-to-speech tasks as AR language modeling tasks, thus enabling zero-shot capabilities in the speech domain. AR-based TTS models are easy to train because they only rely on speech-transcription pairs data, but they also face the problem of slow generation speed and unstable generation quality. To improve its robustness, VALLE-R \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib32)\], RALL-E \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib33)\] proposes to use more alignment information (e.g phone duration) in the training stage, but such strategy also increases the difficulty of data preparation.

The other type relies on NAR generative models, for instance, NaturalSpeech 2 \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18)\] proposes to model the continuous representation of the audio codec model by latent diffusion models. NaturalSpeech 2 is an NAR-based model, which has faster generation speed and better robustness than previous AR-based models. NaturalSpeech 3 \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib21)\] proposes to encode different attributes (e.g. content and prosody) into different codebooks, then applying discrete diffusion models \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib34), [17](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib17), [35](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib35)\] on them. One of the drawbacks in NaturalSpeech 2/3 is that the system training needs fine-grained alignment information (e.g. phone-level duration), which significantly hinders the use of large-scale in the wild data. Furthermore, the hard boundary alignment between phoneme and speech representation may result in unnatural prosody. Similarly, Voicebox \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib19)\] and Audiobox \[[36](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib36)\] propose a pre-training and fine-tuning strategy based on flow matching \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib37)\]. HierSpeech ++ \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib38)\] proposes a hierarchical variational inference method. E3-TTS proposes to generate fixed-length audio based on text using diffusion models without any duration information, which significantly simplifies the data preparation for NAR TTS training. However, the fixed length generation also limits the flexibility of the model. Our recent publication SimpleSpeech \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\] proposes to use a sentence duration to control the length of audio, and we demonstrate that the sentence duration is a effective way for NAR TTS. In this study, we follow the line of our previous publication SimpleSpeech uses sentence duration to control the speech length. In addition, we investigate more strategies to obtain the sentence duration, refer to Section [III-F](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.SS6 "III-F Sentence Duration ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") to find the details.

We notice that some concurrent works, SeedTTS \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib8)\], DiTTo-TTS \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib30)\], and E2TTS \[[39](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib39)\] also adopt a similar idea to use the sentence duration to control the generated speech length in an NAR TTS framework. We claim that our study is one of the earliest works to adopt this idea, and our proposed method is different from theirs.

### II-C Diffusion Models

Diffusion models \[[40](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib40), [41](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib41)\] have demonstrated great performance on a variety of generation tasks, including image \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib31)\] and audio generation \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib34), [29](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib29), [42](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib42)\]. Many previous TTS systems that use diffusion models have been shown to produce high-fidelity speech that is comparable with state-of-the-art systems \[[43](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib43), [44](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib44), [18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18), [45](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib45), [46](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib46)\]. However, most of the previous diffusion-based TTS models rely on an additional duration modeling unit \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18), [45](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib45)\] or carefully designed architecture \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib46)\]. In this study, we follow our previous publication, SimpleSpeech \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\], to design a simple transformer-based diffusion model to generate speech without any alignment module.

## III Proposed Method

In this part, we present the details of the proposed SimpleSpeech 2. The overall architecture of the SimpleSpeech 2 framework is demonstrated in Figure 1, which includes three parts: (1) off-the-shelf pre-trained text and speaker encoders to encode the text and speaker prompt without relying on speech domain-specific modeling, such as phoneme and duration; (2) A pre-trained high-quality audio tokenizer; (3) A LLAMA-style flow-based diffusion transformer. In the following, we first introduce the text encoder, speaker encoder, and SQ-Codec, and then give the details of flow-based scalar latent transformer diffusion models.

### III-A Text Encoder

This work explores to use the large-scale speech-only datasets to train a TTS system. Previous works use high-quality text-speech pair or their internal annotation tools to obtain text transcriptions, making TTS more complicated to implement. In this study, we propose to use the open available ASR ( Whisper-base model \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib23)\]) model to get transcripts. Following SimpleSpeech \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\], we use a pre-trained language model to directly extract the textual representations (e.g. ByT5 \[[47](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib47)\]) from text input without relying on speech domain-specific knowledge, such as phoneme.\

### III-B Speech Attribute Disentanglement Based Speaker Encoder

Previous works directly extract speaker prompt representation from audio codec \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib10)\], but such representation includes not only speaker timbre but also other acoustic information, such as recording condition, noise. Thus the synthesized quality is significantly influenced by the speaker prompt. For example, if the speaker prompt includes noise, the quality of synthesized speech will be poor. Inspired by the FACodec \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib21)\], which proposes to disentangle speech waveform into subspaces of content, prosody, timbre, and acoustic details, we use the pre-trained FACodec to extract the timbre representation. Specifically, we first use the timbre encoder of FACodec to extract a global embedding. Then we extract a fixed number of style vectors from the prosody encoder by using segment-based average pooling. To better show the prosody variation over time, we first split the prompt speech into three segments, and then we extract 3 style vectors from the prompt. Lastly, we concatenate the timbre and style vectors.

### III-C SQ-Codec

Recently, residual vector quantization (RVQ) based audio codec models have shown their advantages in audio compression and audio generation. Many audio generation models use an RVQ-based audio codec as the audio tokenizer (e.g. extracting discrete tokens), then use a transformer model to predict the discrete tokens. Furthermore, the continuous representations extracted from the encoder of the audio codec can also be used as the training target of latent diffusion models, e.g. NaturalSpeech 2 \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18)\]. However, current RVQ-based codec models have drawbacks: (1) training a good RVQ-based codec model needs a lot of tricks and complicated loss design \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib28)\]; (2) the reconstruction performance is positively correlated with codebook number, but too many codebooks also bring burden for LM-based model; (3) The distribution of continuous representations extracted from the encoder of audio codec is complex and infinite, which also increases the modeling difficulty of diffusion models. In this study, we follow SimpleSpeech \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\] to use scalar quantization \[[48](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib48), [49](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib49)\] to replace the residual vector quantization in audio codec models, which can be trained with the reconstruction loss and adversarial loss without any training tricks. We show that the scalar quantization effectively maps the complex speech signal into a finite and compact latent space, which is suitable for the diffusion model (refer to Section [V-C](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S5.SS3 "V-C Discussion: what is a good audio tokenizer? ‣ V Experiments ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") for more details).

Similar to RVQ-based audio codec, our SQ-Codec also consists of an encoder, a decoder, and a scalar quantization. For any input speech $`\mathbf{x}`$, the encoder first transfers it as hidden representation, $`{\mathbf{h}} \in \mathcal{R}^{T \ast d}`$, where $`T`$ and $`d`$ denote the number of frames and the dimension of each vector. Then for any vector $`{\mathbf{h}}_{i}`$, we use a parameter-free scalar quantization module to quantize $`{\mathbf{h}}_{i}`$ into a fixed scalar space, this operation can be easily completed by following the formula:

``` math
{{{\mathbf{h}}_{i} = {\text{Tanh}{({\mathbf{h}}_{i})}}},{{\mathbf{s}}_{i} = {{\text{Round}{({{\mathbf{h}}_{i} \ast S})}}/S}}},
```

where $`S`$ is a hyper-parameter that determines the scope of scalar space. We discuss the influence of hyper-parameter $`S`$ for the reconstruction performance and generation performance in Section [V-C](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S5.SS3 "V-C Discussion: what is a good audio tokenizer? ‣ V Experiments ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"). To get gradients through the rounding operation, we follow a straight-through estimator from VQ-VAE \[[50](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib50)\]. From formula [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.E1 "In III-C SQ-Codec ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"), we can see that the SQ operation first uses a tanh activation function to map the value of features into $`\lbrack{- 1},1\rbrack`$, then it uses a round operation to further scale the value of range into 2\*S+1 different numbers. We named such value domain as scalar latent space. We find that previous works \[[49](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib49), [51](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib51)\] also adopt scalar quantization for the image tokenizer. We claim that our implementation is different from theirs and we have different targets to build the codec: previous work tries to quantize image data into discrete representation, but we want to compress the search space of diffusion models.\

### III-D Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models

Previous diffusion-based text-to-speech (TTS) methods typically model the speech data in a continuous space, e.g. mel-spectrogram \[[43](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib43)\], VAE \[[45](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib45)\], or the encoder output of audio codec \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18)\]. With the help of the powerful generative modeling ability of diffusion models, these works obtain good performance. In this study, we propose a new perspective on the modeling target of diffusion models: a compact, complete, and finite space is easier and more suitable for diffusion models. Specifically, we first pre-training a scalar quantization-based audio codec (SQ-Codec), as section [III-C](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.SS3 "III-C SQ-Codec ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") introduced. Then we train a flow-based diffusion model that transfers the noise distribution into the scalar latent space. In the following, we first give the preliminaries of flow-based diffusion models, and then describe how to use flow-based diffusion models to parameterize the problem.

#### III-D1 Flow-based Diffusion models

In this study, we adopt a flow-based diffusion model \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib37), [52](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib52)\] for text-to-speech, which has been shown powerful generative ability in the image and video domain \[[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib53), [52](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib52)\]. Following previous work \[[52](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib52)\], we will refer to it as flow matching in the following discussion. flow matching uses a simple strategy to corrupt data: it linearly interpolates between noise and data in a straight line. More specifically, given the representation of speech data $`\mathbf{x} \sim {p{(\mathbf{x})}}`$ and Gaussian noise $`\varepsilon \sim {\mathcal{N}{(0,\mathbf{I})}}`$, an interpolation-based forward process can be defined as:

``` math
{\mathbf{x}}_{t} = {{\alpha_{t}{\mathbf{x}}} + {\beta_{t}\mathbf{\epsilon}}}
```

where $`\alpha_{0} = 0`$, $`\beta_{0} = 1`$, $`\alpha_{1} = 1`$, $`\beta_{1} = 0`$, and $`t \in {\lbrack 0,1\rbrack}`$. Similar to the diffusion schedule, we can set $`\alpha_{t}`$ and $`\beta_{t}`$ to follow the cosine schedule $`{\alpha_{t} = {sin{(\frac{\pi t}{2})}}},{\beta_{t} = {cos{(\frac{\pi t}{2})}}}`$ or linear interpolation schedule $`{\alpha_{t} = t},{\beta_{t} = {({1 - t})}}`$. In this study, we adopt the linear interpolation schedule, so formula [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.E2 "In III-D1 Flow-based Diffusion models ‣ III-D Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") can be written as:

``` math
{\mathbf{x}}_{t} = {{t{\mathbf{x}}} + {{({1 - t})}\mathbf{\epsilon}}}
```

where $`t \in {\lbrack 0,1\rbrack}`$. This formulation shows a uniform transformation with constant velocity between data and noise. The corresponding time-dependent velocity field can be written as

$`v_{t}{(x_{t})}`$$`= {{{\hat{\alpha}}_{t}{\mathbf{x}}} + {{\hat{\beta}}_{t}\mathbf{\epsilon}}}`$$`= {{\mathbf{x}} - \mathbf{\epsilon}}`$

where $`{\hat{\alpha}}_{t}`$ and $`{\hat{\beta}}_{t}`$ denote time derivative of $`\alpha`$ and $`\beta`$. Following \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib37), [52](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib52)\], we consider the probability flow ordinary differential equation (ODE) with a velocity field:

``` math
{{d\mathbf{x}_{t}} = {\mathbf{v}_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}dt}},
```

where the velocity $`\mathbf{v}`$ is parameterized by a neural network $`\theta`$. By solving the probability flow ODE backward in time from $`\varepsilon \sim {\mathcal{N}{(0,\mathbf{I})}}`$, we can generate samples and approximate the ground-truth data distribution $`p{(x)}`$. We refer to Eq. [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.E5 "In III-D1 Flow-based Diffusion models ‣ III-D Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") as a flow-based generative model. However, parameterizing $`\mathbf{v}_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}`$ is computationally expensive. Previous works \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib37)\] have shown that estimating a vector field $`\mathbf{u}_{t}`$ that generates a probability path between $`p_{0}`$ and $`p_{1}`$ is equivalent. By solving this flow-based generative model from $`t = 0`$ to $`t = 1`$, we can transform noise into a data sample using the approximated velocity fields $`\mathbf{v}_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}`$. During training, the flow-matching objective directly regresses the target velocity

``` math
{\mathcal{L}_{FM} = {\min\limits_{\theta}{{\mathbb{E}}_{t,{p_{t}{(\mathbf{x})}}}{\|{{\mathbf{v}_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x},t)}} - {{\hat{\alpha}}_{t}{\mathbf{x}}} - {{\hat{\beta}}_{t}\mathbf{\epsilon}}}\|}^{2}}}},
```

The formula [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.E6 "In III-D1 Flow-based Diffusion models ‣ III-D Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") is called conditional flow matching loss \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib37)\], it is similar to the noise prediction or score prediction losses in diffusion models. These properties minimize the trajectory curvature and connect the target data and noise on a straight line. It has been demonstrated that flow probabilistic models \[[54](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib54), [55](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib55), [56](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib56)\] can learn diverse data distribution in multiple domains, such as images \[[52](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib52), [53](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib53)\]. In this work, we compare the flow formulation to existing DDPM \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\] in text-to-speech and demonstrate its benefits.

Furthermore, to make sure the final output belongs to the scalar latent space, we propose to use the scalar quantization (SQ) regularization to scale the final prediction.

``` math
\hat{\mathbf{x}} = {SQ{({\theta{(\mathbf{\epsilon},T,{\mathbf{c}})}})}}
```

where $`\theta`$ denotes the flow-based transformer, $`SQ`$ denotes the scalar quantization operation in formula ([1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.E1 "In III-C SQ-Codec ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models")).

#### III-D2 Architecture

As illustrated in figure [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"), we describe the transformer architecture components in the following part. In previous work \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\], we use a GPT-2 style \[[57](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib57)\] backbone. Inspired by the advanced LLMs architecture \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib15)\], we propose to build a more advanced transformer backbone for the text-to-speech task. The difference from the following parts:\
(1) Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE): RoPE \[[58](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib58)\] is used as the positional encoding. RoPE can encode relative positions, which is more suitable for TTS tasks than Absolute position encoding, e.g. generating long speech during the inference.\
(2) RMSNorm: we substitutes all LayerNorm with RMSNorm to improve training stability \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib15)\]. Moreover, it incorporates key-query normalization (KQ-Norm) before key-query dot product attention computation. The introduction of KQ-Norm aims to prevent loss divergence by eliminating extremely large values within attention logits.\
(3) Condition strategy: In SimpleSpeech \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\], we find that cross attention condition style \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib31)\], and in context condition (concatenate the conditional sequence and noisy latent sequence) are both effective. In this study, we follow the in-context condition strategy that join the sequences of the two modalities (condition features and noisy latent sequence) for the self-attention operation, such that both representations can work in their own space yet take the other one into account.\
(4) Time Mixture-Of-Experts As the previous part introduced, our flow-based diffusion models solve ODE backward in $`t \in {\lbrack 0,1\rbrack}`$ and generate samples approximating the scalar latent distribution. We can find that 1) if the timesteps near $`0`$, the input $`\mathbf{x}`$ is highly noised, and the network of these steps mainly intends to capture the semantic information from conditional information (i.e., text and timbre information); 2) instead, when timesteps near $`1`$, the input $`{\mathbf{x}}_{t}`$ includes most of semantic information, where the model focus more on refining the details ( e.g. improving the speech quality). To enhance the capability of flow-based generative models, following \[[59](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib59)\], we divide all the timesteps uniformly into $`4`$ blocks, in which each block consists of consecutive timesteps and is assigned to one denoising expert. Intuitively, when more experts are used, each block contains fewer timesteps, allowing each expert to better focus on learning the characteristics of the specific denoising steps assigned to it.

### III-E Classifier-Free Guidance

Classifier-free guidance (CFG) \[[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib24)\] has been demonstrated as an effective way to enhance the generation quality in both image and audio domains \[[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib53), [35](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib35)\]. CFG can be formulated as:

``` math
{\hat{p}{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}} = {{p{({\mathbf{x}})}} + {\gamma{({{p{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}} - {p{({\mathbf{x}})}}})}}}
```

where $`\lambda`$ denotes the guidance scale. CFC tries to model conditional distribution $`p{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}`$ and unconditional distribution $`p{({\mathbf{x}})}`$ in the training. In the inference stage, $`\lambda = 1`$ denotes that we do not use classifier-free guidance, when $`\lambda > 1`$ the model decreases the unconditional likelihood of the sample while increasing the conditional likelihood. In other words, classifier-free guidance conducts this by decreasing the unconditional likelihood with a negative score term. During the training stage, previous works try to mask the condition information of some samples (e.g. set the training sample’s text as empty with 10% probability), so that these samples can be used to optimize unconditional distribution $`p{({\mathbf{x}})}`$.

Many prior studies \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib10), [22](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib22)\] demonstrate that text transcriptions generated by an ASR system can be utilized to train a TTS system. Inevitably, the text transcription from the ASR system is not flawless, referred to as noisy or weak labels. Despite this, existing literature does not thoroughly explain why TTS systems can be effectively trained using such dataset. In this study, we show that including a small part of the noisy label in the training set is equivalent to introducing the CFC training strategy, thus we do not need to deliberately construct masked samples during training stage. In the following, we give the proof.

Assume the condition is $`\mathbf{y}`$, the target is $`\mathbf{x}`$, and the generative model tries to model $`p{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}`$. Consider the text $`\mathbf{y}`$ includes $`n`$ words, and $`\mathbf{y}`$ can be split into $`n`$ parts, and we have correspondence ($`{\mathbf{y}}_{i},{\mathbf{x}}_{i}`$). Given that the TTS task tries to learn the mapping between $`{\mathbf{y}}_{i}and{\mathbf{x}}_{i}`$, $`{\mathbf{y}}_{i}`$. In general, we assume $`{\mathbf{x}}_{j}`$ are mutually independent if $`i \neq j`$. The target of the generative model is to learn the distribution $`p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}`$. Based on Bayes formula, we have:

``` math
{{\log p}{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}} = {{{{\log p}{(\left. {\mathbf{y}} \middle| {\mathbf{x}} \right.)}} + {{\log p}{({\mathbf{x}})}}} - {{\log p}{({\mathbf{y}})}}}
```

We can discard the last term when we calculate the derivative of $`x`$.

``` math
{{{\nabla_{x}\log}p}{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}} = {{{{\nabla_{x}\log}p}{(\left. {\mathbf{y}} \middle| {\mathbf{x}} \right.)}} + {{{\nabla_{x}\log}p}{({\mathbf{x}})}}}
```

Based on the independence assumption, we can re-write $`p{(\left. {\mathbf{y}} \middle| {\mathbf{x}} \right.)}`$ as:

$`p{(\left. {\mathbf{y}} \middle| {\mathbf{x}} \right.)}`$$`= {p{(y_{1},y_{2},\left. {\ldots y_{n}} \middle| {x_{1},x_{2},{\ldots x_{n}}} \right.)}}`$$`= {\prod\limits_{i = 1}^{i = n}{p{(\left. y_{i} \middle| x_{i} \right.)}}}`$

By combining equation [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.E11 "In III-E Classifier-Free Guidance ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") and [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.E10 "In III-E Classifier-Free Guidance ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"), we have:

``` math
{{{\nabla_{x}\log}p}{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}} = {{\sum\limits_{i}{{{\nabla_{x}\log}p}{(\left. y_{i} \middle| x_{i} \right.)}}} + {\sum\limits_{i}{{{\nabla_{x}\log}p}{(x_{i})}}}}
```

In a large-scale speech dataset, most of the words will occur many times. In some cases, the given input $`y_{i}`$ is not the ground truth label, we have $`{p{(\left. y_{i} \middle| x_{i} \right.)}}\rightarrow 0`$, thus the first term in formula ([12](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.E12 "In III-E Classifier-Free Guidance ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models")) cannot contribute a positive gradient for our desired optimization process. In an extreme case, we set the text input as empty, so that each term of $`{p{(\left. y_{i} \middle| x_{i} \right.)}} = 0`$. It becomes an unconditional optimization problem:

``` math
{{{\nabla_{x}\log}p}{(\left. {\mathbf{x}} \middle| {\mathbf{y}} \right.)}} = {\sum\limits_{i}{{{\nabla_{x}\log}p}{(x_{i})}}}
```

Note that the traditional CFC training strategy is to randomly choose mask samples during the training process, which means that each correct sample will be used to train the model. Instead, in our noisy label scene, these noisy labels are always wrong. Thus, the model just learns the unconditional distribution when noise labels are in the majority.

### III-F Sentence Duration

In this study, we propose to use sentence duration to control the generated speech length in the inference stage. In our previous study, we use a sentence duration encoder \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\] to encode the duration information into the model. In this study, we further simply this module, we find that it is not necessary to encode the duration information. The sentence duration is only used to provide a coarse length guidance for the initialization of noisy latent. To obtain the sentence duration, we propose the following potential strategies:\
ByT5 based sentence duration predictor We propose to train a sentence duration prediction model with the help of a pre-trained large language model (ByT5 is used). As Figure [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ III-D2 Architecture ‣ III-D Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") (a) shows, we add several learnable attention and linear layers with the fixed ByT5 models, this model is asked to predict the duration. We train the model with MSE loss.\
Get sentence duration by using the context learning ability of ChatGPT Following SimpleSpeech \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\], we can use the in-context learning of ChatGPT to obtain the sentence duration. Specifically, we write a simple prompt for ChatGPT, and ask ChatGPT to predict a coarse sentence duration based on the length and the pronunciation of each words. Considering the ChatGPT is not stable, we call the ChatGPT 5 times for each text, and then get the average duration time.\
Obtaining sentence duration from teacher models Previous NAR TTS models \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib18), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib2)\] has a phoneme duration predictor module, we can easily obtain the duration of each phoneme, and then we can sum them to get the final sentence duration. We use the phoneme duration predictor from open sourced FastSpeech 2 model ²²2https://github.com/ming024/FastSpeech2. To increase the diversity, we add a scale factor for each phoneme duration, the scale factor is a random value, which belongs to $`\lbrack 0.9,1.3\rbrack`$. Note that we only use this module to obtain a coarse sentence duration, which does not introduce additional complexity for our SimpleSpeech 2.\
Training a AR-based duration model We can also train a shallow AR-based duration predictor, that predicts the phoneme or word duration in an autoregressive style. In this study, we use the open-sourced phoneme predictor ³³3https://github.com/ex3ndr/supervoice-gpt as Fig. [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ III-D2 Architecture ‣ III-D Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") shows. Similarly, such module is used to obtain a coarse sentence duration, it does not introduce additional complexity for SimpleSpeech 2.\
In Section [V-E](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S5.SS5 "V-E Ablation study: the influence of sentence duration predictor ‣ V Experiments ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"), we give detailed experimental analysis for each way and find that the ByT5-based sentence duration predictor shows the best performance.

## IV Experimental Setup

### IV-A Dataset

Training data In this study, we train two distinct models, SimpleSpeech 2-EN and SimpleSpeech 2-MUL. For SimpleSpeech 2-EN, we create a dataset with only 7k hours of unlabeled English speech data sampled from the Multilingual LibriSpeech (MLS) dataset \[[60](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib60)\]. For each original audio, we randomly crop 2-10 seconds if the length is larger than 10 seconds. Although the MLS dataset includes text labels, we opt not to use these in order to explore the potential of a speech-only dataset for TTS development. For all of the speech samples, we re-sampling it into 16k Hz. Given the predominant focus of previous research on English datasets, we designate SimpleSpeech 2-EN as our primary experiment and benchmark its performance against existing methods.\
For SimpleSpeech 2-MUL, we enrich the dataset with an additional 4k hours Chinese dataset from WenetSpeech \[[61](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib61)\], which we integrate with the 7k hours English data from MLS. Leveraging the multilingual capabilities of the ByT5 model, we concurrently train on both English and Chinese speech.\
Evaluation data Most prior studies train their models on the LibriLight \[[62](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib62)\] or MLS \[[60](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib60)\] datasets, and then evaluate performance using the LibriSpeech test set \[[63](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib63)\]. One potential drawback is that both LibriSpeech and LibriLight are sourced from the same LibriVox \[[64](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib64)\] audiobooks dataset, which may not fully represent the diversity of real-world audio environments. In this study, we propose to evaluate the model using test sets derived from various other sources. We have constructed a comprehensive test set that includes LibriTTS \[[63](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib63)\], VCTK, CommonVoice \[[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib65)\], RAVDESS \[[66](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib66)\], and SwitchBoard \[[67](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib67)\]. Both LibriTTS and VCTK are widely used in academic research and offer high-quality speech data. SwitchBoard, being a conversational test set, and RAVDESS, which provides a range of emotional speech samples, can validate the model’s ability to handle conversational dynamics and emotional variance, respectively.\

### IV-B Model training and inference

SQ-Codec training For the SQ-Codec model, we train it on the LibriTTS \[[68](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib68)\] dataset. The sample rate is set as 16k Hz. Following \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib25)\], we default use $`S = 9`$ and $`d = 32`$ for all of the experiments. We also train different SQ-Codec models by changing the hyper-parameter $`S`$ and $`d`$, refer to Section [V-C](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S5.SS3 "V-C Discussion: what is a good audio tokenizer? ‣ V Experiments ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") for more details. During the training, we set the learning rate as 2e-3. We used Adam optimizer for all of the experiments. We train the SQ-Codec models with 200k steps.\
SimpleSpeech 2 training For the SimpleSpeech 2 model training, we freeze the SQ-Codec, text encoder, and speaker encoder. We only update the parameters of the transformer diffusion part. We train it by using Adam optimizer with the learning rate of $`{1e} - 4`$. We use a cosine learning rate scheduler with a warmup of 1K steps. The model is trained with 400k steps. For the transformer, we set 16 attention layers, the hidden size is 768, and the transformer head is 32.\
Inference To synthesize speech, we first input the text into the proposed sentence duration module, which can be used to determine the total length of the predicted speech by controlling the length of the noisy latent sequence. Then we input the text and speaker prompt into the SimpleSpeech 2 to generate the predicted latent of SQ-codec. Lastly, we get the predicted waveform by inputting the predicted latent representation into the SQ-Codec decoder.\

### IV-D Evaluation metrics

#### IV-D1 Speech Tokenizer Evaluation

For audio tokenizer models, we use the following metrics: Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality (PESQ), Short-Time Objective Intelligibility (STOI), and structural similarity index measure (SSIM).\

#### IV-D2 TTS Evaluation

For the TTS task, we assess the performance from 4 aspects: intelligibility, speaker similarity, speech quality, and generation speed. Furthermore, we also conduct subjective evaluations for TTS.\
Intelligibility Word Error Rate (WER) is widely used to assess the intelligibility of generated speech. However, previous work \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib8)\] suggests that WER may not serve as a definitive criterion; a lower WER often indicates that the model produces more ‘standardized’ speech, which is easier for ASR systems to recognize. To provide a more comprehensive evaluation, we propose using multiple ASR systems to assess the generated speech, employing the mean WER as the metric for comparison. In this study, we utilize the Whisper-large model and the Hubert-large-based ASR system to evaluate the robustness.\
Speaker Similarity Similarly, previous works use a pre-trained speaker model to extract speaker embedding, and then calculate the speaker similarity score (SIM). However, we empirically observe that using different speaker embedding models results in different SIM scores. Thus, we propose to use multiple speaker embedding models as the ’referee’, and use the average score as the final metric. More specifically, we use the pre-trained WavLM-TDNN model and the 1st layer of XLSR-53 \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib72)\] to extract speaker embedding.\
Speech quality (Perception score) DNSMOS \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib73)\] and Mel-Cepstral Distortion (MCD) are used to measure speech quality.\
Generation speed Following previous work, we use RTF real-time factor (RTF) to measure the efficiency:

``` math
{RTF} = \frac{Cost\_ Time}{Duration}
```

Subjective Evaluation For subjective metrics, we hire 15 listeners to conduct speech naturalness/quality (MOS) and speaker similarity (SMOS) tests. For the MOS test, we ask listeners to focus on whether the speech is realistic, the speech’s naturalness, clearness, and prosody. For the SMOS test, we ask listeners to judge whether the generated speech is similar to the reference speech in terms of timbre, emotion, and prosody.\

### V-C Discussion: what is a good audio tokenizer?

In this section, we explore the influence of audio tokenizers on large-scale TTS systems. Initially, we replace our proposed SQ-Codec with other audio tokenizers, such as VAE and SoundStream. The results are presented in Table [V](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S5.T5 "TABLE V ‣ V-B Comparison with Previous Large-scale TTS Baselines ‣ V Experiments ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"). Subsequently, we investigate the impact of the search space of SQ-Codec on speech generation. Specifically, we set different hyper-parameters, $`S`$ and $`d`$ , to train new SQ-Codec models. These models are then utilized as audio tokenizers for speech synthesis. Our findings are as follows:\
(1) The reconstruction performance of the audio tokenizer determines the upper limit of generated speech quality.For example, Table [II](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S4.T2 "TABLE II ‣ IV-D2 TTS Evaluation ‣ IV-D Evaluation metrics ‣ IV Experimental Setup ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") shows that our SQ-Codec achieves the best reconstruction performance, while our reproduced version of SoundStream performs less effectively. Consequently, using SQ-Codec as the audio tokenizer results in the highest DNSMOS score for the generation model, whereas using SoundStream yields inferior outcomes.\
(2) The proposed scalar latent space is more readily modeled by generative model. In Table [V](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S5.T5 "TABLE V ‣ V-B Comparison with Previous Large-scale TTS Baselines ‣ V Experiments ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"), we set the latent dimension of VAE, SoundStream, and SQ-Codec as 32. The difference is that SoundStream and VAE tried to compress the speech data into a complex continuous distribution, and SQ-Codec tried to compress the speech data into a finite distribution: the value range of each pixel in the scalar latent space belongs to a discrete uniform distribution. Instead, the range of SoundStream and VAE is infinite. Consequently, using SQ-Codec as the speech tokenizer results in superior performance.\
(3) The Compactness of audio tokenizers significantly influences the performance of generative models. As Table [VI](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S5.T6 "TABLE VI ‣ V-C Discussion: what is a good audio tokenizer? ‣ V Experiments ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models") shows, when we increase the latent dimension of SQ-Codec to 50, its reconstruction performance obtains improvement, but the performance of the generation model significantly decreases. Similarly, if we decrease the latent dimension and scalar value, the reconstruction performance will drop, and a poor audio tokenizer also influences the generation performance. In our study, to balance the completeness and compactness, we suggest to use $`S = 9`$ and $`d = 32`$.\
In summary, an effective tokenizer should compress speech data into a latent space that is both compact and complete, ensuring good reconstruction performance and ease of modeling by generative models.

### V-D Discussion: why ASR transcription can be used as a label to train a good TTS system?

Many previous studies \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib10), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib12)\] have demonstrated that text transcriptions from ASR systems can be effectively used to train TTS systems. However, these transcriptions are inevitably imperfect and are often referred to as noisy or weak labels. In Section [III-E](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#S3.SS5 "III-E Classifier-Free Guidance ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ SimpleSpeech 2: Towards Simple and Efficient Text-to-Speech with Flow-based Scalar Latent Transformer Diffusion Models"), we provide a theoretical analysis showing that including a small number of noisy labels in a large-scale dataset is analogous to employing classifier-free guidance training for model optimization. Generally, most ASR systems perform well, achieving a WER of less than 10%. Therefore, we can utilize these ASR transcriptions to train our TTS system. In this study, we employ a state-of-the-art (SOTA) open-sourced ASR model, Whisper \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.13893v2#bib.bib23)\], which has been widely validated.

However, we recognize that there is still room for improvement in the robustness of our model on the Chinese dataset. One potential factor is the direct input of Chinese words into the ByT5 model, which complicates modeling due to the prevalence of polyphones in Chinese. We hypothesize that expanding to a larger dataset could enhance pronunciation accuracy. Additionally, our model demonstrates the ability to produce natural code-switched speech; for more details, please visit our demo page.

## VI Conclusion

In this study, we introduce SimpleSpeech 2, an enhanced TTS system building upon our previous work. SimpleSpeech 2 incorporates advanced model structures, such as Time Mixture-of-Experts, and employs flow-based scalar diffusion techniques. We demonstrate the effectiveness of these improvements through rigorous experiments. Additionally, we conduct extensive experimental and theoretical analyses to address two pivotal research questions in large-scale TTS: (1) identifying the characteristics of an effective speech tokenizer, and (2) understanding why ASR transcriptions are suitable for training TTS systems. Our findings confirm that SimpleSpeech 2 is not only simple and efficient but also stable, capable of synthesizing speech that is diverse, natural, and controllable.
