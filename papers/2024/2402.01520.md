# Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations

###### Abstract

In this paper, we propose a singing voice synthesis model, Karaoker-SSL, that is trained only on text and speech data as a typical multi-speaker acoustic model. It is a low-resource pipeline that does not utilize any singing data end-to-end, since its vocoder is also trained on speech data. Karaoker-SSL is conditioned by self-supervised speech representations in an unsupervised manner. We preprocess these representations by selecting only a subset of their task-correlated dimensions. The conditioning module is indirectly guided to capture style information during training by multi-tasking. This is achieved with a Conformer-based module, which predicts the pitch from the acoustic modelÔÇÖs output. Thus, Karaoker-SSL allows singing voice synthesis without reliance on hand-crafted and domain-specific features. There are also no requirements for text alignments or lyrics timestamps. To refine the voice quality, we employ a U-Net discriminator that is conditioned on the target speaker and follows a Diffusion GAN training scheme.

Index TermsÔÇöÔÇë singing voice synthesis, low-resource, self-supervised, multi-tasking, cross-domain

## 1 Introduction

Despite the recent advancements in speech synthesis with deep learning, modeling a melodic human voice remains an open challenge \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib1)\]. Singing voice synthesis (SVS) is the task of generating vocals for a target speaker that adhere to a template audio or a music score. Predicting how a human sounds beyond the standard operation of the vocal tract is a complex objective. Singing is a controlled and structured voice production, which is characterized by high pitch variation and other vocal manipulations, altering the speakerÔÇÖs identity. For this reason, SVS is an edge and complex case of human voice synthesis and it follows the progress of normal speech synthesis research.

### 1.1 Related Work

The current state of the art in text-to-speech (TTS) involves transformer-based neural networks with flow-based decoders for acoustic modeling \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib2)\]. Some variations either incorporate modules that separately model vocal characteristics such as pitch and energy, to enrich the synthesized voice \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib3)\] or utilize self-supervised learning (SSL) speech representations \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib4)\]. Explicit duration modeling has been established, replacing attention-based alignments. Expressive TTS and voice conversion (VC) extend these approaches \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib5), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib6)\]. Recently, a new generation of speech synthesis models have emerged, which leverage neural codecs and the diffusion-based training scheme \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib7), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib8)\].

The latest efforts in singing voice synthesis (SVS) build upon this progress, introducing adapted models for this task that process additional music information. These solutions are either two-way \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib9), [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib11)\] or end-to-end \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib7), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib12), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib13)\]. They rely on domain-specific data such as singing recordings, music scores or/and lyric alignments. However, these resources require time-consuming and expensive labor with error-prone results, complicating the deployment and scaling of dependent systems in a production setting.

### 1.2 Proposed method

In our previous work, we developed Karaoker \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\], a conditioned sequence-to-sequence acoustic model. It is designed according to a constrained task of SVS, where only unaligned text and speech audios are available. Also, its design aims to produce a lightweight but robust final subsystem by incorporating multiple modules that are active only in training. The vocoder that is used in the pipeline is similarly trained only on speech data.

We present Karaoker-SSL, which is based on the previous Karaoker principles, incorporating some of the recent advancements discussed above. The contributions of this work are listed below:

- ÔÇó
  We utilize a parallel speech-singing dataset to select the most relevant dimensions of SSL speech representations for the SVS task.
- ÔÇó
  We condition an acoustic model in an unsupervised manner with the reduced SSL speech representations.
- ÔÇó
  The acoustic model predicts the pitch from its decoder outputs, performing multi-tasking.
- ÔÇó
  The predicted pitch is evaluated with a custom loss of multiple comparisons.
- ÔÇó
  The generated mel spectrogram is evaluated by a U-Net discriminator using differentiable augmentations.

## 2 METHOD

### 2.1 SSL Dimension selection

Managing large representations is a challenge in terms of resources for the training and deployment of a model. We propose a task-specific strategy that effectively reduces the dimensions of a speech-based SSL representation by $`\sim`$88%. This reduction also aims to keep the style information and minimize the linguistic content. At first, we extract SSL embeddings (Figure [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#S2.F1 "Figure 1 ÔÇú 2.1 SSL Dimension selection ÔÇú 2 METHOD ÔÇú Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations")) from an internal multi-speaker parallel dataset, which includes 985 double takes (speech/singing) of one well-known verse. Then, we compute the absolute differences between the embeddings of the parallel audios of each speaker, averaged on the time dimension. We choose the dimensions that have median absolute differences across the dataset of z-score $`\geq 1`$. This strategy is applied on Wav2Vec 2.0 \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib15)\] and WavLM \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib16)\] embeddings and we choose the latter as it leads to better quality.

### 2.2 Acoustic modeling

The acoustic model is a non-attentive version of the one used in \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\] (Figure [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ÔÇú 2.3 SSL Consumer ÔÇú 2 METHOD ÔÇú Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations")). The encoder outputs are firstly concatenated with speaker and global SSL embeddings. Then, they are summed with rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib17)\] and expanded to the maximum mel length by Nearest Neighbours interpolation. Local SSL embeddings, that undergo a similar process of length interpolation and RoPE addition, are added to the expanded encoder outputs and then passed altogether to the decoder. The SSL embeddings are produced by SSL Consumer that conditions the acoustic model.

### 2.3 SSL Consumer

At first, the reduced WavLM embeddings, rWavLM, are projected to 8 dimensions with a linear layer. The projection is followed by instance normalization \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib18)\], Mish activation \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib19)\] and linear interpolation to mel length. Then, they are provided to SSL Consumer, which consists of 14 convolutional blocks as the ones used in the feature encoder of \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\] but with Mish as the activation function. The first 6 blocks do not have an activation in their final layer and have a kernel size of 3. The outputs are normalized with Conditional Layer Normalization \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib20)\] on speaker embeddings. An 1D convolutional layer (1x1) is used as a bottleneck on the SSL ConsumerÔÇÖs outputs for the learning of global SSL embeddings. The output of this layer is averaged on the time dimension to facilitate the training of SSL Consumer as an InfoVAE \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib21)\]. The global embeddings are also trained to exhibit minimum mutual information with the encoder outputs.

### 2.4 Multi-tasking

Except for the main objective of mel spectrogram generation, Karaoker-SSL has an auxiliary task of predicting the pitch from the decoderÔÇÖs outputs. A four-layered Conformer \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib22)\] was employed for these predictions with a 128-dimensional feedforward layer, 8 multi-attention heads and kernel size of 15 for its depthwise convolution layers. The mel spectrogram is projected to 64 dimensions with a linear layer before being sent to the Conformer. The pitch predictorÔÇÖs outputs are projected to a single dimension and they are compared with the ground truth pitch.

In our early experiments, we observe that known regression or alignment metrics, such as mean squared error (MSE) and differentiable dynamic time warping (DTW), are proved to be less effective for the task. We suggest the following loss function for pitch comparison:

```math
\begin{matrix}
{L_{pitch} = ln\left( \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N}\left( \parallel DFT{(means_{w}{(F0_{gt},div_{i})})} - .. \right. \right.} \\
\left. \left. ..DFT{(means_{w}{(F0_{gen},div_{i})})} \parallel_{1} \right) \right)
\end{matrix}
```

Where $`N`$ are the number of mel lengthÔÇÖs proper divisors $`div_{i}`$ ($`{{div_{i}} > 1},{{\forall\,\, 1} \leq i \leq N}`$), $`DFT`$ is the Discrete Fourier Transform, $`means_{w}`$ is the series of the windowed means by a window size of $`div_{i}`$, $`F0_{gt}`$ is the ground truth pitch and $`F0_{gen}`$ is the predicted pitch. This function produces and compares multiple smoothed versions of the two pitch contours with minimal overhead.

### 2.5 U-Net Discriminator

We implemented a U-Net \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib23)\] with convolutional blocks of two 1D convolutional layers (kernel size=3, padding=1) which are accompanied with spectral normalization \[[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib24)\] and GeLU \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib25)\] activation. The downsampling path (64 to 512 dimensions) consists of 4 blocks with 20% dropout and max pooling (kernel size=2, stride=2). Its symmetric upsampling path (512 to 64 dimensions) includes 4 blocks, each one preceded by a transposed convolutional layer (kernel size=2, stride=2).

The idea of using a U-Net as a discriminator has been mentioned recently for wave signals \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib26)\]. Here, we employ U-Net to discriminate random slices of mel spectrograms \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\] by the output of the last downsampling block. The discriminator is trained as in \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\] but only with combined augmentations of SpecAugment \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib27)\], Diffusion GAN \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib28)\] and real-as-fake \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib29)\]. Thus, the U-Net is not trained on clean ground truth data. Also, mel reconstruction ($`L_{1} + L_{2}`$ losses) has been added in the dicriminatorÔÇÖs training objective.

### 2.6 Losses

The training objective of the Karaoker-SSL consists of the following losses:

```math
\begin{array}{r}
{L = {L_{mel} + {\beta L_{mmd}} + {\kappa L_{mi}} + L_{pitch}}} \\
{{{+ {\lambda L_{pitch\_ g}}} + L_{pitch\_ repr}} - L_{D}}
\end{array}
```

where $`L_{mel}`$ is the sum of $`L_{1}`$, $`L_{2}`$ losses between ground truth and decoder/post-net outputs, $`L_{mmd}`$ is Maximum Mean Discrepancy for InfoVAEÔÇÖs training, $`L_{mi}`$ is the Mutual Information minimization loss, $`L_{pitch}`$ and $`L_{pitch\_ g}`$ refer to pitch prediction from ground truth and generated mels, $`L_{pitch\_ repr}`$ is the application of the custom pitch loss function between the intermediate 64-dim. pitch learning representations of ground truth and generated mels and $`L_{D}`$ is the discriminatorÔÇÖs loss that should be maximized.

## 3 Experiments

### 3.1 Experimental setup

We perform pitch extraction in the same manner as in \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\] from the female voices in LibriTTS \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib30)\] and VCTK \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib31)\] (VCTK+Libri-f). The pitch is divided by 100 and random noise is applied ($`\pm`$ 0.01). In this setup, we process audio files with 24KHz sampling rate as accepted by the efficient LPCNet \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib32)\], which is trained on an internal speech dataset.

The whole model has 12M parameters and it is trained for 600k iterations with batch size of 16 samples, $`\beta`$=0.1 and $`\kappa`$=10000. The pitch predictor infers the pitch of the generated mel spectrograms with $`\lambda`$=100 after 100k iterations of training. The discriminator starts learning at 150k iterations and discriminates at 250k iterations. Two separate Adam optimizers \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib33)\] are used for the generator (acoustic model) and the discriminator with a starting learning rate of 0.001. The rate of the first optimizer drops in half every 100k iterations, while the second optimizer follows a step learning scheduler (gamma=0.1). Concerning inference, only the unaligned text and a rWavLM representation are required, which form the template for the SVS.

### 3.2 Subjective evaluation

We conduct three types of listening tests in order to evaluate the proposed model. We survey native speakers in by Mean Opinion Score (MOS) (37 testers), Speaker Similarity (spk-sim) (41 testers) and Song Similarity (song-sim) (29 testers) as described in \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\]. For reproducibility, we evaluate Karaoker-SSL on the combined public dataset VCTK+Libri-f and we investigate the suitability of the reduced Wav2Vec 2.0 (rWav2Vec) and rWavLM embeddings for cross-domain SVS. The listening tests are applied on 10 source tracks of different length, singerÔÇÖs gender, quality and melodic complexity.

We notice that the model performs better with rWavLM embeddings (Table [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#S3.T1 "Table 1 ÔÇú 3.2 Subjective evaluation ÔÇú 3 Experiments ÔÇú Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations")) and is robust enough to process noisy vocals from source separation predictions. Also, we observe that the model attempts to reproduce the vocal manipulations of the source, distorting speaker identity (Tables [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#S3.T1 "Table 1 ÔÇú 3.2 Subjective evaluation ÔÇú 3 Experiments ÔÇú Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations"),[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#S3.T2 "Table 2 ÔÇú 3.2 Subjective evaluation ÔÇú 3 Experiments ÔÇú Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations")). Except for potential technical shortcomings such as lack of data coverage, the speaker similarity can be affected from the fact that a personÔÇÖs singing style deviates from her normal speaking style. From the breakdown of the scores per speaker, we confirm that the modelÔÇÖs outputs are consistent even for target speakers with different data coverage (Table [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#S3.T2 "Table 2 ÔÇú 3.2 Subjective evaluation ÔÇú 3 Experiments ÔÇú Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations")). The high standard deviation values emphasize the difficulty of testing SVS as we mention in \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\].

### 3.3 Objective evaluation

Our objective evaluation is conducted as in \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\] using the template data from the listening tests. It yields that the synthesized voice has a high median speaker embedding similarity of 70.2% and 0.26 mF0 RMSE (median-normalized pitch Root Mean Square Error). We also compute mF0 NRMSD (Normalized Root Mean Square Deviation) which is 18.2%. Although the latter value is far from perfect, it describes a fair accuracy in terms of the objective evaluation, given the difficulty of the task and since there is no other model available for direct comparison. Equivalent claims have been made in our previous work \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\].

### 3.4 Ablations

We investigate how each auxiliary module contributes to the acoustic model, thus we prepare some ablations. The baseline (ablation{1}) is only the conditioned acoustic model and the ablations are ablation{2} (ÔÇÖnopÔÇÖ), which lacks a pitch predictor, and the U-Net-missing ablation{3} (ÔÇÖnodÔÇÖ). They are jointly tested with the other systems in the listening test of [3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#S3.SS2 "3.2 Subjective evaluation ÔÇú 3 Experiments ÔÇú Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations"). From the results in Table [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#S3.T3 "Table 3 ÔÇú 3.4 Ablations ÔÇú 3 Experiments ÔÇú Low-resource cross-domain singing voice synthesis via reduced self-supervised speech representations"), it is evident that the pitch predictor and the discriminator are critical for the task (ablation {4}).

Both components elevate the capabilities of the acoustic model for SVS. The discriminator contributes to song similarity as it might aid the auxilliary task of pitch prediction, apart from the main task of mel spectrogram generation. Essentially, the acoustic model is the only active network during inference, thus the final production model for each ablation and Karaoker-SSL is the same. However, Karaoker-SSLÔÇÖs acoustic model performs better due to its enriched representations as a result of its multi-objective training.

### 3.5 Comparison with Karaoker

Although the scores in \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.01520v1#bib.bib14)\] are not directly comparable due to the different conditioning, template data, sampling rate, vocoder and target speakers, we could assume that Karaoker-SSL produces a more natural sounding result. The song similarities of the two models are comparable, even though Karaoker-SSL uses a fraction of the hand-crafted features during training and none during inference. We fail to detect any recent solution that approaches the SVS task by using only speech data end-to-end for its training. Therefore, we encourage the reader to listen to the demo samples of Karaoker-SSL┬╣┬╣1 https://innoetics.github.io/publications/karaoker-ssl/index.html and make his/her own judgement.

## 4 Conclusion

We have presented Karaoker-SSL, a model that can synthesize singing voice without having seen any singing data in its own training. We selected the dimensions of SSL speech representations according to the SVS task by analyzing a parallel speech-singing dataset. The model leverages these reduced reprentations to condition acoustic modeling without direct supervision. Unsupervised conditioning serves the scenario of an easily scalable and maintainable production model with low demand on resources. We observe that the acoustic modelÔÇÖs co-training with auxilliary modules greatly enhances the overall performance towards the SVS task, without any increase in the number of the production modelÔÇÖs parameters. Also, a level of disentaglement is achieved between linguistic and acoustic information. Yet, it is not enough for achieving full controllability on the generated voice by the input text and it will be prioritized in our future focus.
