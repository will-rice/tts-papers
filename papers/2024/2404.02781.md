# CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech

Jaehyeon Kim, Keon Lee, Seungjun Chung, Jaewoong Cho\
KRAFTON\
{jay.310,keonlee,s.j.chung,jwcho}@krafton.com\

###### Abstract

With the emergence of neural audio codecs, which encode multiple streams of discrete tokens from audio, large language models have recently gained attention as a promising approach for zero-shot Text-to-Speech (TTS) synthesis. Despite the ongoing rush towards scaling paradigms, audio tokenization ironically amplifies the scalability challenge, stemming from its long sequence length and the complexity of modelling the multiple sequences. To mitigate these issues, we present CLaM-TTS that employs a probabilistic residual vector quantization to (1) achieve superior compression in the token length, and (2) allow a language model to generate multiple tokens at once, thereby eliminating the need for cascaded modeling to handle the number of token streams. Our experimental results demonstrate that CLaM-TTS is better than or comparable to state-of-the-art neural codec-based TTS models regarding naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity, and inference speed. In addition, we examine the impact of the pretraining extent of the language models and their text tokenization strategies on performances.

## 1 Introduction

Large language models (LLMs), characterized by a considerable number of model parameters and trained on massive text data, have demonstrated remarkable zero-shot learning capabilities (Brown et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib9); Chung et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib15); Kaplan et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib33)). While scaling paradigm affects not only the natural language processing domain but also other fields such as image generation (Ramesh et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib63); Saharia et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib71)), image recognition (Radford et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib60)), and speech recognition (Baevski et al., [2020b](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib2); Radford et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib61)), significant challenges in their efficient training and inference simultaneously arise. In the realm of image processing, discretizing image representation (Razavi et al., [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib64); Ramesh et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib63); Esser et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib19)) has been shown to mitigate these issues by effectively reducing the input length to a manageable size.

Language modeling in the speech domain has become feasible with the emergence of neural audio codecs (Zeghidour et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib84); Défossez et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib18)) that enable high-fidelity audio tokenization. For Text-to-Speech (TTS) synthesis, there have been several attempts to adopt the LLMs for zero-shot TTS, which namely synthesize the diverse speech of any human voice (Zhang et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib88); Wang et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib79); Kharitonov et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib34); Rubenstein et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib70)). These attempts move away from the previous research direction to train models on curated high-quality recording datasets and produce human-like voices on benchmark datasets (Li et al., [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib46); Kim et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib36); Tan et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib75); Casanova et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib10)). It is demonstrated that, by training LLMs on tens of thousands of hours of diverse audio data, zero-shot adaptation can be accomplished with just a few seconds of audio input.

Despite the significant advancements in TTS at scale, it still poses challenges to further scale up the models. Neural audio codecs typically generate multiple sequences of audio tokens. For instance, Encodec (Défossez et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib18)) encodes a 5-second speech into 8 sequences of 375 audio tokens. Several work (Kharitonov et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib34); Borsos et al., [2023b](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib8)) employ the semantic tokens from self-supervised speech representation learning (Chung et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib16)) as an intermediary between text and audio tokens. Although semantic tokens compress information more concisely than audio tokens, a 5-second speech segment still demands 125 semantic tokens, presenting a hurdle even setting aside the further complexities of audio token modeling from them.

In this work, we aim to bring the capability of efficient training and inference of large-language models within the TTS domain. To this end, we propose an improved Codec Language Model-based TTS (CLaM-TTS) system that encodes speech into multiple token sequences similar to existing methods but in a more concise way. With CLaM-TTS, all multiple tokens at each timestep in these sequences are generated through a single autoregressive step of a language model, eliminating the need for iterative generative modeling along the number of sequences. The core of our method lies in the probabilistic discrete representation learning, ensuring that all discrete latent codes participate in the training process, resulting in a high-quality autoencoder for speech. Furthermore, we provide a principled framework enabling a latent language model to efficiently generate a stack of tokens at once; the latent language model produces a continuous latent audio representation and converts it to a discrete representation with the proposed probabilistic quantization method. We scale up the training dataset to 100K hours. Our experimental findings indicate that CLaM-TTS either surpasses or is on par with leading zero-shot neural codec-based TTS models in aspects such as naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity, and inference speed. Furthermore, we investigate how the depth of pretraining in the language models and their methods of text tokenization influence TTS outcomes. Our generated samples are available on our demo page¹¹1[https://clam-tts.github.io](https://clam-tts.github.io).

## 2 Related Work

#### Neural audio codec

The neural discrete representation learning within a variational autoencoder (VAE) framework, called the vector-quantized VAE (VQ-VAE), has been proven effective in encoding raw-waveforms into discrete tokens (Baevski et al., [2020a](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib1)), employed as a speech codec (Oord et al., [2017](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib56); Gârbacea et al., [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib21)). Similar to VQ-VAE, the neural audio codec methods usually use a framework that jointly trains an encoder, a decoder, and a quantizer ([Li et al.,](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib47) ; Zeghidour et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib84); Jiang et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib29); Jayashankar et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib28); Défossez et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib18); Kumar et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib41); Wu et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib80)). Zeghidour et al. ([2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib84)) pioneers using residual vector quantization (RVQ) (Gray, [1984](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib22); Vasuki & Vanathi, [2006](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib77); Lee et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib44)) in a neural audio codec model. It operates efficiently on clean and noisy speech and music, even at low bitrates. EnCodec (Défossez et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib18)) employs a similar model structure with improved training efficiency and stability to achieve a downsampling rate of 320 for input waveforms. Kumar et al. ([2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib41)) identify the issue of codebook under-utilization in EnCodec and improve the codebook utilization with the techniques introduced in Yu et al. ([2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib83)) resulting in state-of-the-art performance as a neural audio codec.

Building on these advancements, we focus more on the discrete representation learning of speech rather than general audio and optimize the compression level to be suitable for the TTS task. In other words, we compress mel-spectrograms rather than raw waveforms, delegating the task of converting mel-spectrograms back into raw waveforms to standard vocoders.

#### Large-scale TTS

AudioLM (Borsos et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib7)) is a language model directly trained on audio tokens. In AudioLM, semantic tokens are first generated. These tokens originate from self-supervised discrete speech representation methods (Hsu et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib25); Chung et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib16)) that have previously been utilized for speech resynthesis or generation without text (Lakhotia et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib42); Polyak et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib58); Nguyen et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib55)). Following this, the model produces acoustic tokens of neural audio codes given the semantic tokens. Wang et al. ([2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib79)) propose the first neural codec language model, Vall-E, for text-to-speech that utilizes a pre-trained neural audio codec, EnCodec (Défossez et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib18)). In a different approach, following AudioLM, text-to-speech has been realized by applying language modeling to generate the semantic tokens from text, as demonstrated by  Kharitonov et al. ([2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib34)). A shared characteristic among these neural codec language models is their two-stage pipeline; they autoregressively generate coarse-grained audio tokens and decode them into fine-grained representations. Recent work in music generation hints at an efficient way to eliminate the second-stage modeling by interleaving audio tokens in a delayed pattern (Copet et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib17)), but its application in TTS remains unexplored.

Given the complexities in modeling long audio sequences, several studies have incorporated phonemes and durations to alleviate the need for speech synthesizers to predict speech rates (Shen et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib72); Le et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib43); Jiang et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib30)). Some work shows that non-autoregressive generative models, such as a diffusion model and flow-matching (Ho et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib23); Lipman et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib48)), can produce diverse and natural-sounding speech with large-scale training. A hybrid method is utilized in another approach, employing non-autoregressive architecture except prosody modeling (Jiang et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib30)). This method aligns with previous work that applies VQ-VAEs to capture fine-grained speech features so that the prosody is controllable by them (Sun et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib74); Ren et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib65)).

To address the challenges associated with neural codec language models while not relying on the phoneme and its duration that requires significant domain expertise, we design a language model that generates from coarse to fine-grained tokens without needing a two-stage pipeline. Our approach is similar to recent work that utilizes pre-trained language models, Spectron (Nachmani et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib54)) and SoundStorm (Borsos et al., [2023b](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib8)). While Spectron employs pre-trained transformer decoders to directly model the mel-spectrogram and then fine-tunes it, our method preserves the pre-trained text encoder and decodes speech tokens that are shorter than the mel-spectrogram using a latent transformer decoder. SoundStorm freezes a pre-trained text encoder similar to ours, but it generates semantic tokens and subsequently decodes acoustic tokens using an iterative generative model.

## 3 Preliminaries

Building upon the approach proposed by Wang et al. ([2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib79)), we explore zero-shot TTS through the lens of neural codec language modeling task. We consider a setting that includes two types of data: (i) text data and (ii) mel-spectrogram representation of its corresponding speech data, denoted by $`\mathbf{x}`$ and $`\mathbf{y}`$, respectively. We model a sequence of $`T`$ discrete codes $`{\mathbf{c}}_{1:T}:={\{{\mathbf{c}}_{1},\ldots,{\mathbf{c}}_{T}\}}`$ from latent representations $`{\mathbf{z}}_{1:T}`$ of a mel-spectrogram $`\mathbf{y}`$, using the framework of a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) with Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ). Here, $`{\mathbf{c}}_{t}`$ represents the $`D`$-depth of quantized, discrete codes. We interchangeably use $`{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D}`$ with $`{\mathbf{c}}_{t}`$. Subsequently, a neural language model $`p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{1:T} \middle| {\mathbf{x}} \right.)}`$ is employed, aiming to predict $`{\mathbf{c}}_{1:T}`$ from the text transcript $`\mathbf{x}`$. During the inference phase, the language model generates $`{\mathbf{c}}_{1:T}`$ for a given text $`\mathbf{x}`$, which is subsequently transformed into speech through the VAE decoder and a pre-trained vocoder.

### 3.1 Residual-quantized Variational Autoencoder (RQ-VAE)

An RQ-VAE (Lee et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib44)) is a neural network architecture representing data as discrete codes using residual vector quantization. It comprises of three components: (1) an encoder parameterized by $`\phi`$ that maps data $`\mathbf{y}`$ into a sequence of latent representations $`{\mathbf{z}}_{1:T}`$; (2) a residual vector quantizer $`\text{RQ}_{\psi}{( \cdot )}`$, converting the latent vector $`{\mathbf{z}}_{t}`$ at each time $`t`$ into the discrete code representation $`{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D} = {\text{RQ}_{\psi}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t})}}`$, or the corresponding quantized embedding $`{\hat{\mathbf{z}}}_{t}`$; and (3) a decoder parameterized by $`\omega`$ that reconstructs the data $`\hat{\mathbf{y}}`$ from a sequence of the quantized latent representations $`{\hat{\mathbf{z}}}_{1:T}`$.

Here $`{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D}`$ represents the set $`\{ c_{t,1},\ldots,c_{t,D}\}`$ with $`D`$ indicating the total depth of the quantizer. The latent representation from the encoder is quantized through the multi-stage nearest-neighbour lookup over the codebook embeddings, of which the vocab size is $`V`$. The process is defined as finding the optimal code from the codebook, which minimizes the residual error at each depth $`d`$:

```math
\begin{matrix}
{{{c_{t,d} = {\underset{c' \in {\{ 1,\ldots,V\}}}{\arg\min}{\|{{\mathbf{r}}_{t,{d - 1}} - {e_{\psi}{(c';d)}}}\|}^{2}}},{{{\mathbf{r}}_{t,d} = {{\mathbf{r}}_{t,{d - 1}} - {e_{\psi}{(c_{t,d};d)}}}}\quad{{\text{for all~}d} \in {\lbrack 1,D\rbrack}}}},}
\end{matrix}
```

where $`{\mathbf{r}}_{t,0}`$ = $`{\mathbf{z}}_{t}`$ and $`e_{\psi}{(c;d)}`$ corresponds to the $`c`$-th embedding vector in the codebook at depth $`d`$. The sum of embeddings $`\sum_{d = 1}^{D}{e_{\psi}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d};d)}}`$ becomes the quantized latent representation $`{\hat{\mathbf{z}}}_{t}`$, which is converted back to the input space through the decoder. The codebook embeddings are updated with the clustered latents by the exponential moving average updates (Razavi et al., [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib64)).

### 3.2 Mean-field Variational Inference

Consider a latent variable model characterized by a joint distribution $`p_{\psi}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t},{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D})}`$ parameterized by $`\psi`$. Here, $`{\mathbf{z}}_{t}`$ denotes an observed random variable, $`{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D}`$ indicates a set of latent random variables $`\{{\mathbf{c}}_{t,1},\ldots,{\mathbf{c}}_{t,D}\}`$. In this model, variational inference is a method to approximate the intractable distribution $`p_{\psi}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}`$ by solving an optimization problem with respect to parameters of approximate distribution $`q{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}`$. We can derive a lower bound on the marginal log-likelihood $`p_{\psi}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t})}`$, known as the evidence lower bound (ELBO) (Blei et al., [2017](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib6)):

$`{\log p_{\psi}}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t})}`$$`{= {\log{\sum\limits_{{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D}}{p_{\psi}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D} \right.)}p{({\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D})}}}} \geq {{\mathbb{E}}_{q{({{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D}|{\mathbf{z}}_{t}})}}\left\lbrack {\log\frac{p_{\psi}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D} \right.)}p{({\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D})}}{q{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}}} \right\rbrack}}.`$

Mean-field variational inference (Koller & Friedman, [2009](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib40); Blei et al., [2017](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib6)) is a specific approach of variational inference that assumes the independence among the latent variables conditioned on the observed variable: $`{q{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}} = {\prod_{d = 1}^{D}{q{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t,d} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}}}`$. We can show that each optimal variational posterior distribution $`q^{\ast}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t,d} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}`$, which maximizes the ELBO, satisfies:

$`{{q^{\ast}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t,d} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}} \propto {\exp\left( {{\mathbb{E}}_{q{({{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}|{\mathbf{z}}_{t}})}}\left\lbrack {{\log p_{\psi}}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{z}}_{t,d},{\mathbf{z}}_{t,{- d}}} \right.)}p{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}})}} \right\rbrack} \right)}},`$

where $`{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}`$ denotes the latent variables of all depth $`{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D}`$ except $`{\mathbf{c}}_{t,d}`$. An iterative coordinate ascent algorithm based on Eq. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#S3.E2 "In 3.2 Mean-field Variational Inference ‣ 3 Preliminaries ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech") can be used to update the distribution $`q`$ (Bishop & Nasrabadi, [2006](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib5)), and the complexity of the algorithm mainly lies on the computation of the expectation over $`q{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}`$.

## 4 Method

### 4.1 Mel-VAE

We aim to develop a neural codec that can generate discrete speech codes within a short sequence length to make speech audios suitable for language model utilization. To achieve this, we employ a RQ-VAE that compresses mel-spectrograms of speech audios (see Fig. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech")a). We introduce a variational inference based method for learning residual codewords to address the codeword collapse issue found in conventional vector quantization methods (Kaiser et al., [2018](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib32); Roy et al., [2018](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib69); Zeghidour et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib84); Kumar et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib41)).

We illustrate Mel-VAE similar to RQ-VAE following most of notations from Sec. [3.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#S3.SS1 "3.1 Residual-quantized Variational Autoencoder (RQ-VAE) ‣ 3 Preliminaries ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech"). The encoder maps a mel-spectrogram $`\mathbf{y}`$ into a sequence of latent representations $`{\mathbf{z}}_{1:T}`$, and a residual vector quantizer $`{\mathsf{R}\mathsf{Q}}_{\psi}{( \cdot )}`$, converting the latent vector $`{\mathbf{z}}_{t}`$ at each time $`t`$ into the discrete code representation $`{\mathbf{c}}_{t}`$, or its corresponding quantized embedding $`{\hat{\mathbf{z}}}_{t} = {\sum_{d = 1}^{D}{e_{\psi}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d};d)}}}`$. The decoder reconstructs the mel-spectrogram $`\hat{\mathbf{y}}`$ from a sequence of quantized latent representations $`{\hat{\mathbf{z}}}_{1:T}`$.

With the assumptions that $`{q{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}} = {\prod_{d = 1}^{D}{q{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t,d} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}}}`$ and $`p{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}})}`$ is uniformly distributed, mean-field variational inference yields the condition of such distribution as the following (see Eq. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#S3.E2 "In 3.2 Mean-field Variational Inference ‣ 3 Preliminaries ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech")):

$`{{q^{\ast}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t,d} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}} \propto {\exp{({{\mathbb{E}}_{q{({{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}|{\mathbf{z}}_{t}})}}\left\lbrack {{\log p_{\psi}}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{c}}_{t,d},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}} \right.)}} \right\rbrack})}}},`$

where the latents follows a normal distribution: $`{p_{\psi}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {\mathbf{c}}_{t} \right.)}} = {\mathcal{N}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t};{\sum_{d}{e_{\psi}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d};d)}}},{\sigma_{\psi}^{2}I})}}`$.

However, the mutual interdependence of codes at every depth in the latter equation makes it difficult to solve it without an iterative approach. Instead of using an iterative coordinate update approach, we approximate $`{\mathbb{E}}_{q{({{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}|{\mathbf{z}}_{t}})}}\left\lbrack {{\log p_{\psi}}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{c}}_{t,d},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}} \right.)}} \right\rbrack`$ pointwisely as $`{\log p_{\psi}}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{c}}_{t,d},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}^{\ast}} \right.)}`$ for all $`d`$, where $`{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D}^{\ast} = {\text{RQ}_{\psi}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t})}}`$. The posterior then has a form: $`{q^{\ast}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t,d} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}} \propto {p_{\psi}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{c}}_{t,d},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}^{\ast}} \right.)}}`$.

We independently optimize the codebook embeddings at each depth $`d`$, in a variational inference framework:

$`\mathcal{L}{(\psi_{d};{\mathbf{z}}_{t},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}^{\ast})}`$$`{= {{\mathbb{E}}_{q^{\ast}{({{\mathbf{c}}_{t,d}|{\mathbf{z}}_{t}})}}\left\lbrack {- {{\log p_{\psi}}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{c}}_{t,d},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}^{\ast}} \right.)}}} \right\rbrack}},`$$`\mathcal{L}{(\psi;{\mathbf{z}}_{t},{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D}^{\ast})}`$$`{= {\sum_{d = 1}^{D}{\mathcal{L}{(\psi_{d};{\mathbf{z}}_{t},{\mathbf{c}}_{t,{- d}}^{\ast})}}}}.`$

The other modules of Mel-VAE, including the encoder parameters $`\phi`$ and the decoder parameters $`\omega`$, are trained with commitment loss, reconstruction loss, and adversarial losses.

$`\mathcal{L}{(\omega,\phi;{\mathbf{y}},{\mathbf{c}}_{{t,1}:D})}`$$`{= {{\lambda_{r}{|{{\mathbf{y}} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}}|}} + {\lambda_{c}{\|{{\mathbf{z}} - {\sum_{d}{e_{\psi}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d};d)}}}}\|}^{2}} + {\lambda_{a}\mathcal{L}_{adv}}}},`$

where $`\lambda_{r}`$, $`\lambda_{c}`$, and $`\lambda_{a}`$ corresponds to coefficients of the reconstruction loss, commitment loss, and adversarial losses, respectively. For adversarial training, we adopt the multi-length discriminator (Chen et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib12)) that distinguishes mel-spectrograms at different lengths and a modified multi-resolution spectrogram discriminator (Lee et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib45)) that accepts mel-spectrogram rather than linear spectrogram. We combine the least squares GAN objective (Mao et al., [2017](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib51)) and the $`L1`$ feature matching loss (Kumar et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib41)) into a loss function denoted by $`\mathcal{L}_{adv}`$.

### 4.2 Latent Language Modeling

We propose a conditional speech code language model given text $`\mathbf{x}`$ aimed at enhancing the efficiency of the model. This improvement stems from the insight that speech codes are generated through vector quantization. Our approach leverages this by predicting the vector itself, which can then be converted into multiple tokens at each layer via residual vector quantization. This is a departure from previous methods that predict speech code tokens sequentially.

Specifically, rather than directly predicting tokens from text, we consider a continuous latent representation $`{\mathbf{z}}_{t}`$ of speech that can be converted into a speech code using residual vector quantization:

$`{p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{1:T} \middle| {\mathbf{x}} \right.)}} = {\prod\limits_{t = 1}^{T}{p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}}}`$$`{= {\prod\limits_{t = 1}^{T}{\int{p_{\theta}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t},\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}{d{\mathbf{z}}_{t}}}}} = {\prod\limits_{t = 1}^{T}{\int{p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}p_{\psi}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}{d{\mathbf{z}}_{t}}}}}},`$

where $`{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}`$ indicate $`{\mathbf{c}}_{1:{t - 1}}`$, and we employ $`p_{\psi}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t} \middle| {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \right.)}`$, the probabilistic quantizer distribution learned together with the Mel-VAE model (see Sec. [4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#S4.SS1 "4.1 Mel-VAE ‣ 4 Method ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech")), as a replacement of $`p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{z}}_{t},{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}`$. Here, we define the conditional distribution $`p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}`$ as a Gaussian mixture model:

```math
{{p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}} = {\sum_{k = 1}^{K}{p_{\theta}{(\left. k \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}\mathcal{N}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t};{\mu_{\theta}{(k,{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t})}},{\sigma_{\psi}^{2}I})}}}}.
```

In this model, we can derive the following variational lower bound on the log-likelihood:

$`{\log p_{\theta}}{(\left. {\mathbf{c}} \middle| {\mathbf{x}} \right.)}`$$`\geq \sum\limits_{t = 1}^{T}{\mathbb{E}}_{q{({k|{{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{\leq t}}})}}\left\lbrack - D_{KL}{(p_{\psi}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t}|{\mathbf{c}}_{t})}||p_{\theta}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t}|{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t},k)})} + \log p_{\theta}{(k|{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t})} + \mathcal{B}{(\psi,{\mathbf{c}}_{t})} \right\rbrack`$$`{= {{- {\mathcal{L}_{\mathsf{V}\mathsf{B}}{(\theta)}}} + {\mathcal{B}{(\psi,{\mathbf{c}}_{t})}}}},`$

for any $`q{(\left. k \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{\leq t}} \right.)}`$. The derivation of the lower bound and the definition of $`\mathcal{B}{(\psi,{\mathbf{c}}_{t})}`$ are provided in Appendix [A](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#A1 "Appendix A Variational Lower Bound ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech"). Here, we set $`q{(k|{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{\leq t})} \propto \exp{( - D_{KL}{(p_{\psi}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t}|{\mathbf{c}}_{t})}||p_{\theta}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t}|{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t},k)})})}`$.

With the second loss $`\mathcal{L}_{\mathsf{E}\mathsf{O}\mathsf{S}}{(\theta)}`$, which is associated with training a binary classifier to identify the end of speech (EOS), the total loss for training the latent language model is the sum of the two losses above: $`{{\mathcal{L}{(\theta)}} = {{\mathcal{L}_{\mathsf{V}\mathsf{B}}{(\theta)}} + {\mathcal{L}_{\mathsf{E}\mathsf{O}\mathsf{S}}{(\theta)}}}}.`$

As shown in Fig. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech"), we implement an autoregressive latent model that yields three distinctive outputs: the mixture weights and the means of the Gaussian mixture distribution as well as the probability of concluding the generation. Specifically, it incorporates a transformer decoder followed three parallel modules, comprising (1) a prediction layer with softmax activation for $`p_{\theta}{(\left. k \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}`$; (2) a prediction layer for $`\mu_{\theta}{(k,{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t})}`$; (3) a binary classifier layer for EOS prediction. Additionally, we use the pre-trained quantizer $`{\mathsf{R}\mathsf{Q}}_{\psi}{( \cdot )}`$ of Mel-VAE.

### 4.3 Model Architecture and Inference

#### Model Architecture

For the Mel-VAE, we adopt a causal 1d convolutional U-Net, a variant of the model used in Ho et al. ([2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib23)). We remove the skip connections and attention layers and append 1-d ConvNeXt (Liu et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib49)) blocks used in  Siuzdak ([2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib73)) to the final layer of the decoder. We employ 32-stage residual vector quantization with a codebook size of 1,024 for each depth. For the text-to-code latent language model, we adopt a transformer-based encoder-decoder LM, especially a pre-trained ByT5-large²²2[https://huggingface.co/google/byt5-large](https://huggingface.co/google/byt5-large) (Xue et al., [2021a](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib81)) similar to Borsos et al. ([2023b](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib8)). We keep the text encoder frozen. Please refer to Appendix [C](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#A3 "Appendix C Model Configurations and Implementation Details ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech") for more detailed model configuration.

#### Inference

The text-to-code generation unfolds in three steps: (1) at time step $`t`$, we randomly select $`k`$ from the distribution $`p_{\theta}{(\left. k \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t}} \right.)}`$; (2) following this, randomly sample the latent vector $`{\mathbf{z}}_{t}`$ from $`p_{\theta}{(\left. {\mathbf{z}}_{t} \middle| {{\mathbf{x}},{\mathbf{c}}_{< t},k} \right.)}`$. Consequently, at time step $`t`$, the discrete code is obtained through the learned quantizer, $`{\mathbf{c}}_{t} = {\text{RQ}_{\psi}{({\mathbf{z}}_{t})}}`$; (3) if the probability of EOS exceeeds 0.5, conclude the generation, or proceed to step, otherwise. Subsequently, the generated codes are decoded to mel-spectrograms using the decoder of Mel-VAE, then converted to raw-waveforms through an off-the-shelf pre-trained vocoder, BigVGAN (Lee et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib45)).

## 5 Experimental Setup

#### Training Dataset

We employ 100K hours of over 12K distinct speakers’ speech-transcript dataset spanning 11 languages: English, Korean, Chinese, Japanese, German, Dutch, French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, and Polish. We train two models: (1) CLaM-en: an English-only model on 55K-hour English dataset and (2) CLaM-multi: a multilingual model trained on 11-language dataset. We provide details of dataset for each language in Appendix [B.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#A2.SS1 "B.1 Dataset Statistics ‣ Appendix B Additional Details of Experiment ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech"), and data pre-processing in Appendix [B.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#A2.SS2 "B.2 Data Pre-Processing ‣ Appendix B Additional Details of Experiment ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech") and  [B.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#A2.SS3 "B.3 Approximation for audio resampling ‣ Appendix B Additional Details of Experiment ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech").

#### Training

\(1\) Mel-VAE: We train the model on 4 NVIDIA A100 40GB GPUs for around 2M steps. Each GPU processes a size one minibatch containing concatenated mel-spectrograms of several audios. We trim the trailing end to have it precisely 32,768 frames long. We use Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, [2015](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib37)) with a constant learning rate of 0.0002 throughout the training. (2) Text-to-code: We train only the decoder and use a learned codebook from Mel-VAE. The model is trained on 4 NVIDIA A100 40GB GPUs for around 4M steps with dynamic batching while keeping a maximum code size of 2,560. We use AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib50)), and the learning rate is fixed to 0.0002 throughout the training. Throughout all our experiments, during the model inference, we sample $`k`$ using top-$`p`$ sampling (Holtzman et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib24)) with 0.5 and $`z`$ is sampled with temperature (Kingma & Dhariwal, [2018](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib38)) of 2.6, which matches the empirical standard deviation in our validation dataset.

#### Baselines

We compare the proposed model with the following four baselines: (1) YourTTS (Casanova et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib10)), a zero-shot TTS built on VITS (Kim et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib36)) which is flow-based end-to-end TTS (representing Conventional TTS), (2) Vall-E (Wang et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib79)) and (3) SPEAR-TTS (Kharitonov et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib34)) (representing Neural Codec LM), and (4) VoiceBox (Le et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib43)), a flow-matching-based TTS model trained on large-scale training data (representing Non-Autoregressive Model with Phoneme Input and Duration).

#### Metrics

\(1\) Intelligibility and Robustness: We measure these attributes by character error rate (CER) and word error rate (WER) of the synthesized transcription from generated speech concerning the input text. We follow the procedure in Wang et al. ([2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib79)). In English-only Evaluation, we synthesize the transcription by using the automatic speech recognition (ASR) model, the CTC-based HuBERT-Large³³3[https://huggingface.co/facebook/hubert-large-ls960-ft](https://huggingface.co/facebook/hubert-large-ls960-ft) (Hsu et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib25)) model pre-trained on LibriLight (Kahn et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib31)) and then fine-tuned on LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., [2015](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib57)). In the Multilingual Evaluation, we use OpenAI’s Whisper (Radford et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib61)) model⁴⁴4[https://github.com/openai/whisper/blob/main/model-card.md](https://github.com/openai/whisper/blob/main/model-card.md): ”large-v2”. We adopt NVIDIA’s NeMo-text-processing⁵⁵5[https://github.com/NVIDIA/NeMo-text-processing](https://github.com/NVIDIA/NeMo-text-processing) (Zhang et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib87); Bakhturina et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib3)) for text normalization; (2) Speak Similarity: We assess the speaker similarity of two separate speech audio clips by following the same procedure outlined in Wang et al. ([2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib79)). We employ WavLM-TDCNN⁶⁶6[https://github.com/microsoft/UniSpeech/tree/main/downstreams/speaker_verification](https://github.com/microsoft/UniSpeech/tree/main/downstreams/speaker_verification) (Chen et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib13)) which outputs the embedding vector representing the speaker’s voice attribute. We measure the cosine similarity between the two embedding vectors to get a score in $`\lbrack{- 1},1\rbrack`$, where a higher score indicates a higher speaker similarity of the audios. We borrow the definition of SIM-o and SIM-r from Le et al. ([2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib43)). SIM-o measures the similarity between the generated and the original target speeches, while SIM-r measures the similarity concerning the target speech reconstructed from the original speech by Mel-VAE and the pre-trained vocoder; (3) Subjective Speech Quality: We measure the quality of the generated speech from human evaluations via three types of Mean Opinion Score (MOS) (Ribeiro et al., [2011](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib66)): i) Quality MOS (QMOS) for an overall audio assessment, ii) Similarity MOS (SMOS) to measure speaker similarity between the prompt and the generated speech, and iii) Comparative MOS (CMOS) to compare our model with available baselines. Detailed settings of subjective tests are described in Sec. [B.5](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#A2.SS5 "B.5 Subjective Evaluation ‣ Appendix B Additional Details of Experiment ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech").

#### Tasks

We measure the performances of the proposed model under two different tasks: 1) _continuation_: Given a text and corresponding initial 3 seconds of the Ground Truth speech as a prompt, the task is to seamlessly synthesize the subsequent portion of the speech, and 2) _cross-sentence_: The model is given a text, a 3-second speech segment, and its corresponding transcript (the transcript is different from the text). The task is to synthesize a speech reading the text in the style of the provided 3-second speech. We include our samples across the tasks discussed above, covering speaker diversity, text prompting, and other aspects, on our demo page.

### 6.3 Ablation Study

#### Effectiveness of Proposed RVQ

To demonstrate the effect of the proposed RVQ on Mel-VAE, we conduct an ablation study by assessing speech reconstruction capability. We train two Mel-VAEs: one with the proposed RVQ and the other with the baseline RVQ (Défossez et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib18)). We train both for 500k steps on the same training dataset described in Sec. [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#S5 "5 Experimental Setup ‣ CLaM-TTS: Improving Neural Codec Language Modeling for Zero-shot Text-to-Speech"). The generated speech is compared to the Ground Truth speech using two metrics: Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality (PESQ) (Rix et al., [2001](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib67)) and Virtual Speech Quality Objective Listener (ViSQOL) (Chinen et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib14)) in speech mode. For evaluation, we randomly select 1,800 samples from the test set of each dataset, proportional to the size of each dataset, each being at least 3 seconds long. The scores of these samples are then averaged for comparison. ## 7 Discussion

#### Choice of Codeword Rate

Our approach enjoys a 10Hz codeword rate for efficient modeling. We set the codeword rate following the average phoneme rate in English speech (Roach, [2009](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.02781v1#bib.bib68)) since phoneme is the minimum spoken unit. Nevertheless, we conjecture this may have to be adjusted depending on the language or speaker. A more compressed codeword rate, for example, 5Hz, might lead to more significant information loss than their efficiency. There exists an efficiency-performance tradeoff for rates above 10Hz, which can be optimized as needed.

#### Robustness

We have noticed some words can be muddled, omitted, or repeated, which predominantly stems from autoregressive modeling. We will address it by employing non-autoregressive architecture or improving the attention mechanism in future work.

#### Expressiveness

100K hours of training data may not ensure a complete representation of all voice types, especially accentuated ones. Our datasets predominantly capture audiobook reading styles, leading to limited diversity in speaking styles. We believe that increasing the model and data size can significantly tackle the expressiveness challenges in zero-shot TTS.

#### Instruction Prompting

We suggest various ways to use the full knowledge of the language model. One can incorporate speaker metadata into each transcript to perform various intriguing tasks. Such tasks might include synthesizing speech or even conversations characterized by specific genders, voice ages, or accents. We leave the other tasks for future work.

## 8 Conclusion

We introduce CLaM-TTS, which leverages mean-field variational inference based probabilistic residual vector quantization (1) achieving significant compression in token length, and (2) allowing a latent language model to generate multiple tokens at once, thereby eliminating the need for cascaded modeling to handle the number of token streams. We scale up the training dataset to 100K hours. We empirically show that CLaM-TTS is better than or comparable to state-of-the-art neural codec-based TTS models regarding naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity, and inference speed.

## 10 Ethics Statements

CLaM-TTS is a zero-shot TTS model that leverages a pre-trained large language model, offering efficient learning and inference at a vast scale. The model’s capability to produce any voice and mimic with only minimal voice input presents potential dangers, including spoofing misuse. Given the escalating risks associated with such models, it should be imperative to develop a detection model to identify audio outputs from the model and to establish a rigorous protocol for evaluating its effectiveness.

We first revisit the audio resampling process before describing our method. Let us denote the resampled audio, its sample rate, and its corresponding mel-spectrogram as $`A_{target}`$, $`S_{target}`$, and $`M_{target}`$ respectively. The original audio and its sample rate can be labelled as $`A_{source}`$ and $`S_{source}`$. Please note that our proposed model uses the mel-spectrogram as both input and output, making $`M_{target}`$ our final goal for the data processing. First, we apply the STFT to $`A_{source}`$ to obtain frequency domain components. We then perform linear interpolation in the frequency domain to adjust the number of samples per second to match $`S_{target}`$. Due to the occurrence of aliasing, a Low-pass filter is applied. Next, we use the inverse STFT (ISTFT) to acquire the upsampled $`A_{target}`$. Finally, we apply the STFT again to derive $`M_{target}`$. Our approach, in contrast, is as follows: To determine $`M_{target}`$, we adjust the pre-set FFT size (which is also the same as window size) and hop size according to the ratio of $`S_{source}`$ to $`S_{target}`$. Using the value, we produce a linear spectrogram and then apply a mel-filter bank to generate a mel-spectrogram. We refer to it as $`\overset{\sim}{M_{t⁢a⁢r⁢g⁢e⁢t}}`$ and regard it as an approximation for $`M_{target}`$. Unlike $`M_{target}`$, the process of obtaining $`\overset{\sim}{M_{t⁢a⁢r⁢g⁢e⁢t}}`$ does not actually change the number of audio samples, which can lead to inaccuracies at high frequencies. However, due to the nature of mel-spectrograms, it can be disregarded in regions of important low-frequency features. The advantage of deriving $`\overset{\sim}{M_{t⁢a⁢r⁢g⁢e⁢t}}`$ is that we only need to apply the STFT once, and there’s no need to store any audio in between. It allowed us to use datasets with different sample rates without time and storage consumption due to audio resampling. In the mini-batch training scheme, we first randomly sample the amount of data up to the pre-defined maximum audio length per batch from the train set and sort them by sample rate. Then we determine the ratio of each data sample rate over the target to modify FFT size (window size) and hop size, respectively. The mel-spectrogram calculated by these STFT parameters is input to our model. ### B.4 Details of English-only evaluation We average the result over three repetitions of each experiment with a randomly selected set of prompts per trial. This configuration ensures that embeddings at higher depths capture more detailed features. The formulation for this parameterization is given by: $`e_{\psi}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d};d)}`$$`{= {\alpha_{d}\frac{{\overset{\sim}{e}}_{\psi}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d};d)}}{\|{{\overset{\sim}{e}}_{\psi}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d};d)}}\|}}},`$$`{{\text{where}\quad\alpha_{d}} = {{\exp{({max\_ scale\_ logit_{\psi}})}}{\sum\limits_{i = d}^{D}{{softmax}{({scale\_ logits_{\psi}})}_{i}}}}}.`$ Here, $`\alpha_{d}`$ dynamically adjusts the scale of embedding vectors at the depth $`d`$, with the trainable parameters of the residual vector quantizer being $`max\_ scale\_ logit_{\psi}`$, $`scale\_ logits_{\psi}`$, and $`{\overset{\sim}{e}}_{\psi}{({\mathbf{c}}_{t,d};d)}`$. #### Latent MoG We present an efficient approach to managing the number of mixtures, $`k`$, in the Mixture of Gaussians (MoG) of our latent language model. The dimensionality of the MoG output is directly proportional to both $`k`$ and the dimension $`m`$ of the mean vector $`{\mu_{\theta}{(k,x,{\mathbf{C}}_{< t})}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{m}`$. This dimensionality can become unwieldy as $`k`$ increases. To address the challenge of managing the excessive size of the output weight, we employ a strategy to compress it. This approach yields a low-rank prediction $`{{\overset{\sim}{\mu}}_{\theta}{(k,x,{\mathbf{C}}_{< t})}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{n}`$ and a projection matrix $`M \in {\mathbb{R}}^{m \times n}`$, where $`n < m`$, effectively enabling us to represent $`\mu_{\theta}{(k,x,{\mathbf{C}}_{< t})}`$ as $`M{\overset{\sim}{\mu}}_{\theta}{(k,x,{\mathbf{C}}_{< t})}`$. This compression facilitates more efficient computation of the training loss, which involves calculating the L2 distance between the mean vector $`\mu_{\theta}{(k,x,{\mathbf{C}}_{< t})}`$ and the quantized latent representation of speech $`{\hat{\mathbf{z}}}_{t}`$ as $`{\|{{\hat{\mathbf{z}}}_{t} - {\mu_{\theta}{(k,x,{{\mathbf{C}}{{}_{<}^{}t}})}}}\|}^{2}`$.
