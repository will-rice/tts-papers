Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder\
with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator
==============================================================

###### Abstract

A generative adversarial network (GAN)-based vocoder trained with an adversarial discriminator is commonly used for speech synthesis because of its fast, lightweight, and high-quality characteristics. However, this data-driven model requires a large amount of training data incurring high data-collection costs. This fact motivates us to train a GAN-based vocoder on limited data. A promising solution is to augment the training data to avoid overfitting. However, a standard discriminator is unconditional and insensitive to distributional changes caused by data augmentation. Thus, augmented speech (which can be extraordinary) may be considered real speech. To address this issue, we propose an augmentation-conditional discriminator (AugCond$`D`$) that receives the augmentation state as input in addition to speech, thereby assessing the input speech according to the augmentation state, without inhibiting the learning of the original non-augmented distribution. Experimental results indicate that AugCond$`D`$ improves speech quality under limited data conditions while achieving comparable speech quality under sufficient data conditions.¹¹1Audio samples are available at [https://www.kecl.ntt.co.jp/people/kaneko.takuhiro/projects/augcondd/](https://www.kecl.ntt.co.jp/people/kaneko.takuhiro/projects/augcondd/).

Index Terms—  Speech synthesis, neural vocoder, generative adversarial networks, limited data, data augmentation

## 1 Introduction

Text-to-speech (TTS) and voice conversion (VC) have been actively studied to obtain the desired speech. In recently developed TTS and VC systems, a two-stage approach is commonly adopted, whereby the first model predicts the intermediate representation (e.g., mel spectrogram) from the input data (e.g., text or speech), and the second model synthesizes speech from the predicted intermediate representation. The second model, the neural vocoder, has been extensively studied through autoregressive models (e.g., WaveNet \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib1)\] and WaveRNN \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib2)\]) and non-autoregressive models, including distillation-based (e.g., Parallel WaveNet \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib3)\] and ClariNet \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib4)\]), flow (e.g., Glow \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib5)\])-based (e.g., WaveGlow \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib6)\]), diffusion \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib7), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib8)\]-based (e.g., WaveGrad \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib9)\] and DiffWave \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib10)\]), and generative adversarial network (GAN) \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib11)\]-based (e.g., \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib12), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib13), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib14), [15](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib15), [16](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib16), [17](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib17), [18](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib18), [19](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib19), [20](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib20), [21](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib21), [22](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib22), [23](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib23), [24](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib24), [25](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib25), [26](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib26), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib27)\]) models. This study focuses on a GAN-based model because it is fast, lightweight, and high-quality.

A GAN-based vocoder is a data-driven model that requires a large amount of training data, resulting in high data-collection costs. Thus, researchers have recently attempted to train the GAN-based vocoder on limited data \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib25)\]. A promising solution is to expand the training data using data augmentation methods such as mixup \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib28), [29](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib29)\], CutMix \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib30)\], SpecAugment \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib31)\], SpecMix \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib32)\], WavAugment \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib33)\], and PhaseAug \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib25)\] to avoid overfitting.²²2Another possible solution is to pretrain a neural vocoder on large-scale data and then finetune it on limited target data. However, collecting large-scale data is often laborious and impractical in real applications as difficult ethical issues must be considered. Hence, in this study, we focus on training a vocoder on limited data from scratch, deferring our ideas on finetuning to future work. However, as shown in Fig. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")(a), the standard discriminator is unconditional and agnostic to the augmentation state. Consequently, the discriminator may consider the augmented speech (which can be extraordinary) as the desired real speech.

To address this problem, we propose an augmentation-conditional discriminator (AugCond$`D`$), which is a variant of the conditional discriminator \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib34)\] receiving not only speech but also the augmentation state, as shown in Fig. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 1 Introduction ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")(b). This allows AugCond$`D`$ to assess the input speech while considering the augmentation state, thereby preventing the augmented speech from interfering with the learning of the original non-augmented distribution.

In experiments, we first investigated the benchmark performance of AugCond$`D`$ on the LJSpeech dataset \[[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib35)\], demonstrating that AugCond$`D`$ improves speech quality under limited data conditions while achieving comparable speech quality under sufficient data conditions. The general utility of AugCond$`D`$ was then investigated by evaluating it under various settings.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2 "2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator") reviews a GAN-based vocoder and data augmentation. Section [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S3 "3 Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator") describes AugCond$`D`$. Section [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S4 "4 Experiments ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator") presents the experimental results. Finally, Section [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S5 "5 Conclusion ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator") concludes the paper and discusses future research.

## 2 Preliminaries

First, we review the GAN-based vocoder (Section [2.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.SS1 "2.1 GAN-based vocoder ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")) and data augmentation (Section [2.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.SS2 "2.2 Data augmentation ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")) which forms the basis of our method.

### 2.1 GAN-based vocoder

A GAN-based vocoder (or generator $`G`$) synthesizes speech from an intermediate representation (e.g., mel spectrogram). It is trained with a discriminator $`D`$ using three losses: adversarial, feature matching, and spectrogram-domain losses.

Adversarial losses. Adversarial losses (particularly least-squares GAN-based \[[36](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib36)\] losses, which are commonly used in speech synthesis) are expressed as follows:

$`\mathcal{L}_{Adv}(D)`$$`{= {{\mathbb{E}}_{(x_{r},s_{r})}\left\lbrack {\left( {{D\left( x_{r} \right)} - 1} \right)^{2} + \left( {D\left( {G\left( s_{r} \right)} \right)} \right)^{2}} \right\rbrack}},`$$`\mathcal{L}_{Adv}(G)`$$`{= {{\mathbb{E}}_{s_{r}}\left\lbrack \left( {{D\left( {G\left( s_{r} \right)} \right)} - 1} \right)^{2} \right\rbrack}},`$

where $`x_{r}`$ represents real speech, and $`s_{r}`$ represents the intermediate representation (e.g., mel spectrogram) extracted from $`x_{r}`$. $`D`$ attempts to distinguish between real speech $`x_{r}`$ and synthesized speech $`x_{g} = {G\left( s_{r} \right)}`$ by minimizing $`\mathcal{L}_{Adv}(D)`$. In contrast, $`G`$ attempts to synthesize $`x_{g}`$ which can deceive $`D`$ by minimizing $`\mathcal{L}_{Adv}(G)`$.

Feature matching loss. To stabilize GAN training, a feature matching (FM) loss \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib37), [38](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib38)\] is adopted as follows:

$`{{\mathcal{L}_{FM}(G)} = {{\mathbb{E}}_{(x_{r},s_{r})}\left\lbrack {\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{T}{\frac{1}{N_{i}}\left\| {{D_{i}\left( x_{r} \right)} - {D_{i}\left( {G\left( s_{r} \right)} \right)}} \right\|_{1}}} \right\rbrack}},`$

where $`T`$ denotes the number of layers in $`D`$. $`D_{i}`$ and $`N_{i}`$ denote the features and number of features in the $`i`$th layer of $`D`$, respectively. $`G`$ attempts to bring $`x_{g} = {G\left( s_{r} \right)}`$ closer to $`x_{r}`$ in the discriminator feature space by minimizing $`\mathcal{L}_{FM}(G)`$.

Spectrogram-domain loss. A spectrogram-domain loss, such as a mel-spectrogram loss \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib14)\] and multiresolution spectrogram loss \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib13)\], is commonly used to further stabilize GAN training. The mel-spectrogram loss is defined as follows:

$`{{\mathcal{L}_{Mel}(G)} = {{\mathbb{E}}_{(x_{r},s_{r})}\left\lbrack \left\| {{\phi\left( x_{r} \right)} - {\phi\left( {G\left( s_{r} \right)} \right)}} \right\|_{1} \right\rbrack}},`$

where $`\phi`$ denotes a mel-spectrogram extractor. $`G`$ attempts to bring $`x_{g} = {G\left( s_{r} \right)}`$ closer to $`x_{r}`$ in the mel-spectrogram domain by minimizing $`\mathcal{L}_{Mel}(G)`$.

Total losses. The total losses are expressed as follows:

$`\mathcal{L}_{G}`$$`{= {{\mathcal{L}_{Adv}(G)} + {\lambda_{FM}\mathcal{L}_{FM}} + {\lambda_{Mel}\mathcal{L}_{Mel}}}},`$$`\mathcal{L}_{D}`$$`{= {\mathcal{L}_{Adv}(D)}},`$

where $`\lambda_{FM}`$ and $`\lambda_{Mel}`$ are hyperparameters for weighting the losses and were set to $`2`$ and $`45`$, respectively, in the experiments \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib14)\]. $`G`$ and $`D`$ are optimized by minimizing $`\mathcal{L}_{G}`$ and $`\mathcal{L}_{D}`$, respectively.

### 2.2 Data augmentation

The data augmentation technique prevents overfitting by expanding the training data. In the context of a GAN-based vocoder, two data-augmentation strategies can be considered, as shown in Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2.2 Data augmentation ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator").

Strategy 1 (S1): Data augmentation for $`D`$ (Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2.2 Data augmentation ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")(a)). Data augmentation is applied to $`x_{r}`$ and $`x_{g}`$, that is, $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r} = {{Aug}\left( x_{r} \right)}`$ and $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{g} = {{Aug}\left( x_{g} \right)}`$, where “$`Aug`$” denotes the augmentation operator. Subsequently, $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r}`$ and $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{g}`$ are fed into $`D`$. This strategy is commonly used for training a GAN generating data from random noise \[[39](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib39), [40](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib40)\].

Strategy 2 (S2): Data augmentation for $`G`$ and $`D`$ (Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2.2 Data augmentation ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")(b)). Data augmentation is applied to $`x_{r}`$, that is, $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r} = {{Aug}\left( x_{r} \right)}`$. The intermediate representation $`{\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r}`$ is extracted from $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r}`$; $`G`$ receives $`{\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r}`$ as input and synthesizes augmented speech $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{g}'`$. Subsequently, the augmented speech, that is, $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r}`$ or $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{g}'`$, is fed into $`D`$.

In S1, only the training data for $`D`$ are augmented (i.e., $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r}`$ and $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{g}`$ are used), and the inputs of $`G`$ are not changed (i.e., $`s_{r}`$ is used). In contrast, in S2, not only the training data for $`D`$ are augmented (i.e., $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r}`$ and $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{g}'`$ are used) but also the inputs of $`G`$ are augmented (i.e., $`{\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r}`$ is used). The preliminary experiments indicate that S2 is more effective than S1 under limited data conditions. This is possibly because it is important to prevent not only the overfitting of $`D`$ but also the overfitting of $`G`$. Therefore, we adopted S2 for the remainder of this study. When S2 is employed, $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r}`$ and $`{\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r}`$ are used instead of $`x_{r}`$ and $`s_{r}`$, respectively, in Eqs. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.E1 "1 ‣ 2.1 GAN-based vocoder ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")–[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.E6 "6 ‣ 2.1 GAN-based vocoder ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator").

Fig. 2: Comparison of data-augmentation strategies. “$`Ext`$” and “$`Aug`$” denote an intermediate representation extractor and augmentation operator, respectively. The red variable and red arrow indicate augmented data and augmented data flow, respectively. ## 3 Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator

Data augmentation is useful for expanding training data and preventing overfitting. However, as shown in Fig. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S3.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ 3 Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")(a), a standard discriminator $`D\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)`$, as defined in Section [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2 "2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator"), is unconditional, that is, it receives augmented speech $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$ only and is agnostic to the augmentation state $`\mu`$. Thus, it may consider the augmented speech (which can be extraordinary) $`\overset{\sim}{x} \sim {p\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)}`$ as real speech $`x \sim {p(x)}`$. This is problematic when training data are limited because strong data augmentation, which is likely to result in extraordinarily augmented speech, is required to prevent overfitting.

AugCond$`D`$ was developed to prevent this undesirable phenomenon. As shown in Fig. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S3.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ 3 Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")(b), AugCond$`D`$ $`D\left( \overset{\sim}{x},\mu \right)`$ receives $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$ and $`\mu`$. This simple but critical modification allows AugCond$`D`$ to assess $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$ considering $`\mu`$, which is useful for preventing augmented speech $`\overset{\sim}{x} \sim {p\left( \overset{\sim}{x} \right)}`$ from interfering with the learning of the original non-augmented distribution $`p(x)`$.

Losses. When AugCond$`D`$ is used, the losses defined in Section [2.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.SS1 "2.1 GAN-based vocoder ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator") (Eqs. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.E1 "1 ‣ 2.1 GAN-based vocoder ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")–[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.E4 "4 ‣ 2.1 GAN-based vocoder ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")) are rewritten as follows:

$`\mathcal{L}_{Adv}^{Aug}(D)`$$`{= {{\mathbb{E}}_{({\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r},{\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r},\mu)}\left\lbrack {\left( {{D\left( {\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r},\mu \right)} - 1} \right)^{2} + \left( {D\left( {G\left( {\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r} \right)},\mu \right)} \right)^{2}} \right\rbrack}},`$$`\mathcal{L}_{Adv}^{Aug}(G)`$$`{= {{\mathbb{E}}_{({\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r},\mu)}\left\lbrack \left( {{D\left( {G\left( {\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r} \right)},\mu \right)} - 1} \right)^{2} \right\rbrack}},`$$`\mathcal{L}_{FM}^{Aug}(G)`$$`{= {{\mathbb{E}}_{({\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r},{\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r},\mu)}\left\lbrack {\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{T}{\frac{1}{N_{i}}\left\| {{D_{i}\left( {\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r},\mu \right)} - {D_{i}\left( {G\left( {\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r} \right)},\mu \right)}} \right\|_{1}}} \right\rbrack}},`$$`\mathcal{L}_{Mel}^{Aug}(G)`$$`{= {{\mathbb{E}}_{({\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r},{\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r})}\left\lbrack \left\| {{\phi\left( {\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r} \right)} - {\phi\left( {G\left( {\overset{\sim}{s}}_{r} \right)} \right)}} \right\|_{1} \right\rbrack}}.`$

Accordingly, the total losses (Eqs. [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.E5 "5 ‣ 2.1 GAN-based vocoder ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator") and [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.E6 "6 ‣ 2.1 GAN-based vocoder ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")) are redefined as follows:

$`\mathcal{L}_{G}^{Aug}`$$`{= {{\mathcal{L}_{Adv}^{Aug}(G)} + {\lambda_{FM}\mathcal{L}_{FM}^{Aug}} + {\lambda_{Mel}\mathcal{L}_{Mel}^{Aug}}}},`$$`\mathcal{L}_{D}^{Aug}`$$`{= {\mathcal{L}_{Adv}^{Aug}(D)}},`$

where $`G`$ and $`D`$ are optimized by minimizing $`\mathcal{L}_{G}^{Aug}`$ and $`\mathcal{L}_{D}^{Aug}`$, respectively. Notably, in implementation, the required modifications are limited to changes in the inputs of $`G`$ and $`D`$ and the architectural change in $`D`$ (detailed below), without necessitating other modifications (e.g., changes in the weight update rule).

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x3.png)

Fig. 3: Comparison of process flows for a GAN with a standard discriminator and GAN with AugCond$`D`$. (a) Standard discriminator $`D{(\overset{\sim}{x})}`$ receives augmented speech $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$ only and is agnostic to the augmentation state $`\mu`$. (b) AugCond$`D`$ $`D{(\overset{\sim}{x},\mu)}`$ accepts $`\mu`$ in addition to $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$, allowing AugCond$`D`$ to assess $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$ while considering $`\mu`$.

Architecture. In AugCond$`D`$, $`D`$ is conditional on $`\mu`$ using input concatenation \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib34)\] because preliminary experiments indicate that this works sufficiently well. Fig. [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S4.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 4.1 Investigation of benchmark performance ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator") depicts the conditioning method. First, $`\mu`$ is reshaped such that its time length is identical to that of the input speech $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$. Subsequently, $`\mu`$ and $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$ are concatenated in the channel direction. Finally, the concatenated tensor is input into $`D`$. The increases in calculation cost and model size are not significant because the modification is limited to changes in the input.

## 4 Experiments

Two experiments were conducted to verify the effectiveness of AugCond$`D`$. (1) The benchmark performance of AugCond$`D`$ was investigated on the LJSpeech dataset \[[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib35)\] (Section [4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S4.SS1 "4.1 Investigation of benchmark performance ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")). (2) The general utility of AugCond$`D`$ was evaluated under various settings (Section [4.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S4.SS2 "4.2 Investigation of general utility ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")). Audio samples are available from the link indicated on the first page of this manuscript.\\footnotemark

### 4.1 Investigation of benchmark performance

Comparison models. In our experiments, four models were compared: (1) HiFi-GAN (HiFi) (particularly the high-quality V1 variant) \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib14)\] is a commonly used baseline. (2) HiFi-GAN with PhaseAug (HiFi-phase) \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib25)\] is a state-of-the-art model trained with limited data. (3) HiFi-GAN-AugCond$`D`$ with mixup (HiFi-ACD-mix) is the proposed model. (4) HiFi-GAN with mixup (HiFi-mix) is an ablation of (3), where a standard discriminator is used instead of AugCond$`D`$ to investigate the effectiveness of AugCond$`D`$. For a fair comparison of (1) and (2), (3) was implemented based on HiFi-GAN with the same generator and discriminator, only modifying the input layer of the discriminator according to the process presented in Fig. [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S4.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 4.1 Investigation of benchmark performance ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator"). As a data augmentation method, mixup \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib28)\] was adopted in the waveform domain because preliminary experiments indicated that it is more effective than PhaseAug (state-of-the-art) under limited-data conditions.³³3This is possibly because PhaseAug augments the phase of a waveform and its effect disappears when the mel spectrogram is extracted, implying that this is only effective for $`D`$ and not for $`G`$ even when S2 (Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2.2 Data augmentation ‣ 2 Preliminaries ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator")(b)) is used. In contrast, the mixup is effective for both $`G`$ and $`D`$ when S2 is used. In particular, we obtain augmented speech $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r}`$ by mixing two speech samples in a batch ($`x_{r}^{1}`$ and $`x_{r}^{2}`$) with a mixture rate of $`m \sim {U(0,1)}`$, that is, $`{\overset{\sim}{x}}_{r} = {{mx_{r}^{1}} + {\left( {1 - m} \right)x_{r}^{2}}}`$, where $`U(a,b)`$ is a uniform distribution in $`\lbrack a,b\rbrack`$. We defined the augmentation state $`\mu`$ as $`\mu = {\left( {1 - {\max\left( m,{1 - m} \right)}} \right) \times 2}`$. According to this definition, $`\mu = 0`$ (i.e., $`m = 0`$ or $`m = 1`$) and $`\mu = 1`$ (i.e., $`m = 0.5`$) indicate no augmentation and maximum augmentation, respectively. As $`\mu`$ is scalar, the process depicted in Fig. [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#S4.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 4.1 Investigation of benchmark performance ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ Training Generative Adversarial Network-Based Vocoder with Limited Data Using Augmentation-Conditional Discriminator") is adopted after expanding to a $`1 \times 1`$ tensor (i.e., $`d = 1`$).

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x4.png)

Fig. 4: Process of input concatenation. $`A \times B`$ indicates a tensor shape with time length $`A`$ and $`B`$ channels; $`t`$ and $`d`$ denote the time length of augmented speech $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$ and the dimension of augmentation state $`\mu`$, respectively. After $`\mu`$ is expanded by a factor of $`t`$ in the temporal direction, it is concatenated with $`\overset{\sim}{x}`$ in the channel direction. Finally, the concatenated tensor is input into $`D`$. When $`\mu`$ is a scalar (as in the experiments), $`\mu`$ is first expanded to a $`1 \times 1`$ tensor (i.e., $`d = 1`$) and then the above process is adopted.

Data. The performances of the models were investigated using the LJSpeech dataset \[[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib35)\], which is a commonly used benchmark dataset. This dataset includes 13,100 audio clips from a single female English speaker. The clips were divided into 12,950 and 150 audio clips for training and validation, respectively, following the official HiFi-GAN \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib14)\] configuration.⁴⁴4[https://github.com/jik876/hifi-gan](https://github.com/jik876/hifi-gan) Following the official PhaesAug \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib25)\] configuration,⁵⁵5[https://github.com/maum-ai/phaseaug](https://github.com/maum-ai/phaseaug) $`100\%`$ (23.7 hr.) and $`1\%`$ (14.4 min.) of the training data were used to simulate the sufficient and limited data conditions, respectively. Audio clips were sampled at 22.05 kHz. 80-dimensional log-mel spectrograms extracted from audio clips with an FFT size of $`1024`$, hop length of $`256`$, and window length of $`1024`$ were used as vocoder inputs.

Implementation. The models were implemented using the official HiFi-GAN\\footnotemark and PhaseAug\\footnotemark codes. Each model was trained for 2.5M iterations using the Adam optimizer \[[41](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib41)\] with a batch size of $`16`$, an initial learning rate of $`0.0002`$, and momentum terms $`\beta_{1}`$ and $`\beta_{2}`$ of $`0.5`$ and $`0.9`$, respectively. As reported in \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib25)\], HiFi and HiFi-phase suffered from overfitting in early iterations; therefore, we also evaluated their early stopped versions (stopped at 200k iterations). Hereafter, these models are labeled as $`\dagger`$.

Evaluation metrics. A mean opinion score (MOS) test was conducted to evaluate speech quality. Twenty audio clips were randomly selected from the validation set, and the log-mel spectrograms extracted from the audio clips were used as vocoder inputs. In addition to the synthesized speech, ground-truth speech was included as anchor data. Fifteen participants attended the online test and were asked to rate speech quality on a five-point scale: 1 = bad, 2 = poor, 3 = fair, 4 = good, and 5 = excellent. We further used three objective metrics: (1) UTMOS \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib42)\] is a MOS prediction system that achieved the highest score for several metrics in the VoiceMOS Challenge 2022 \[[43](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib43)\]. Higher values correspond to better speech quality. (2) Periodicity \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib21)\] measures the difference in periodicity between the synthesized and ground-truth speech. This correlates with MOS \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib21)\] with a lower value indicating a higher degree of similarity. (3) Conditional Fréchet wav2vec distance (cFW2VD) \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib20)\] measures the distribution distance between the synthesized and ground-truth speech in a wav2vec 2.0 \[[44](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib44)\] feature space conditioned on the text. This correlates with MOS \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.16464v1#bib.bib20)\] such that a smaller value indicates a higher degree of similarity.

## 5 Conclusion

We proposed AugCond$`D`$ to train a GAN-based vocoder with limited data. AugCond$`D`$ is unique in that it receives not only speech but also the augmentation state, thereby assessing the input speech according to the augmentation state. This prevents the augmented speech from inhibiting the learning of the original non-augmented distribution. Experimental evaluations under various settings indicate the general utility of AugCond$`D`$. The simplicity and versatility of AugCond$`D`$ facilitates its application to other models (e.g., end-to-end models) and tasks (e.g., finetuning), to be pursued in future research.
