# Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation

###### Abstract

Audio-driven 3D facial animation has several virtual humans applications for content creation and editing. While several existing methods provide solutions for speech-driven animation, precise control over *content* (what) and *style* (how) of the final performance is still challenging. We propose a novel approach that takes as input an audio, and the corresponding text to extract temporally-aligned content and disentangled style representations, in order to provide controls over 3D facial animation. Our method is trained in two stages, that evolves from audio prominent styles (how it sounds) to visual prominent styles (how it looks). We leverage a high-resource audio dataset in stage I to learn styles that control speech generation in a self-supervised learning framework, and then fine-tune this model with low-resource audio/3D mesh pairs in stage II to control 3D vertex generation. We employ a non-autoregressive *seq2seq* formulation to model sentence-level dependencies, and better mouth articulations. Our method provides flexibility that the style of a reference audio and the content of a source audio can be combined to enable audio style transfer. Similarly, the content can be modified, e.g. muting or swapping words, that enables style-preserving content editing.

## 1 Introduction

Virtual human applications, such as, AR/VR, CGI, and telepresence has led to an increased focus on modeling audio-driven 3D facial animation. We desire a system that, given an unseen audio, provides precise control over the generated facial animation, where the articulated *content* is disentangled from *style* (attributes such as identity, emotions, idiosyncrasies). Such flexible control offers the opportunity to change content and the visual style with mix ’n match, while alleviating the need to record new performances. Existing audio-driven facial animation systems \[[Lewis and Parke(1986)](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx21), [Brand(1999)](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx4), [Edwards et al.(2016)Edwards, Landreth, Fiume, and Singh](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx6), [Cudeiro et al.(2019)Cudeiro, Bolkart, Laidlaw, Ranjan, and Black](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx5), [Thies et al.(2020)Thies, Elgharib, Tewari, Theobalt, and Nießner](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx43), [Richard et al.(2021)Richard, Zollhöfer, Wen, De la Torre, and Sheikh](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx36), [Fan et al.(2022)Fan, Lin, Saito, Wang, and Komura](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx7), [Ma et al.(2024)Ma, Zhu, Qi, Qian, Zhang, and Lei](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx28), [Peng et al.(2023)Peng, Wu, Song, Xu, Zhu, He, Liu, and Fan](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx33)\] don’t provide explicit *content* and *style* representations that we can manipulate separately, where source audio is often mapped to either simple chart tables \[[Lewis and Parke(1986)](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx21), [Edwards et al.(2016)Edwards, Landreth, Fiume, and Singh](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx6)\] or a latent embedding space that can’t be interpreted semantically \[[Richard et al.(2021)Richard, Zollhöfer, Wen, De la Torre, and Sheikh](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx36), [Fan et al.(2022)Fan, Lin, Saito, Wang, and Komura](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx7)\]. While explicit conditioning factors such as one-hot identity can be integrated \[[Cudeiro et al.(2019)Cudeiro, Bolkart, Laidlaw, Ranjan, and Black](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx5), [Fan et al.(2022)Fan, Lin, Saito, Wang, and Komura](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx7), [Ma et al.(2024)Ma, Zhu, Qi, Qian, Zhang, and Lei](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx28), [Peng et al.(2023)Peng, Wu, Song, Xu, Zhu, He, Liu, and Fan](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx33)\], however, such a representation has limited capacity to model rich and highly diverse styles.

While facial audio-video dataset are available in abundance \[[Wang et al.(2020a)Wang, Wu, Song, Yang, Wu, Qian, He, Qiao, and Loy](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx46), [Zhu et al.(2022)Zhu, Wu, Zhu, Jiang, Tang, Zhang, Liu, and Loy](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx58)\], large high-quality audio-3D mesh datasets are costly to create, and require post-processing for consistent mesh topology \[[Cudeiro et al.(2019)Cudeiro, Bolkart, Laidlaw, Ranjan, and Black](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx5)\]. This data limitation has constrained the effective generalized learning for audio-driven facial animation. Text-to-Speech (TTS) \[[Shen et al.(2018)Shen, Pang, Weiss, Schuster, Jaitly, Yang, Chen, Zhang, Wang, Skerrv-Ryan, Saurous, Agiomvrgiannakis, and Wu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx37), [Kumar et al.(2019)Kumar, Kumar, de Boissiere, Gestin, Teoh, Sotelo, de Brébisson, Bengio, and Courville](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx19), [Ren et al.(2020)Ren, Hu, Tan, Qin, Zhao, Zhao, and Liu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx35)\] is a highly-correlated task, where high-fidelity speech can be synthesized preserving a speaker’s characteristics – timbrel, prosody, accent, etc. Style controlled TTS \[[Sun et al.(2020)Sun, Zhang, Weiss, Cao, Zen, and Wu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx41), [Huang et al.(2022)Huang, Ren, Liu, Cui, and Zhao](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx13), [Li et al.(2021b)Li, Song, Li, Wu, Jia, and Meng](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx24), [Li et al.(2022)Li, Han, and Mesgarani](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx25)\] is most similar to our use-case. The audio speech and the corresponding visual speech i.e., talking head videos \[[Fried et al.(2019)Fried, Tewari, Zollhöfer, Finkelstein, Shechtman, Goldman, Genova, Jin, Theobalt, and Agrawala](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx9), [Thies et al.(2020)Thies, Elgharib, Tewari, Theobalt, and Nießner](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx43)\] are highly correlated since both modalities are generated by the articulation system. For instance, the work in \[[Yu et al.(2019)Yu, Lu, Hu, Yu, Weng, Xu, Liu, Tuo, Kang, Lei, Su, and Yu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx54)\] produces speech and facial expression simultaneously. Thus, knowledge in *high-resource* TTS methods can be potentially transferred to *low-resource* audio-driven facial animation. To investigate this, we present a novel two stage approach with knowledge transfer for audio-driven facial animation, see [Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation").

In Stage I, we train an (audio+text to Mel-spectrum) model for the speech synthesis (TTS) task via spectrum reconstruction. Exploiting spectrum reconstruction to learn styles has also been used in \[[Li et al.(2022)Li, Han, and Mesgarani](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx25), [Min et al.(2021)Min, Lee, Yang, and Hwang](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx31)\], where the style encoders are learned from scratch. To encourage disentanglement of audio styles, we initialised the style encoder with two sub-networks pre-trained for speaker verification and emotion classification respectively.

In Stage II, we train an (audio+text to mesh) model to learn *style-aware* audio-driven facial animation, where the network structure is modified over the top output layers (vs. Stage I), see [Section 3](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S3 "3 Our Method ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation"). Stage II essentially fine-tunes the Stage-I network, and alleviates the need for a larger audio-mesh dataset. Driven by tasks of different modalities, the learned styles evolve from audio-prominent (Mel-spectrum) to visual prominent (3D meshes). To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to attempt such a modality-evolving formulation.

We summarize our contributions below: (1) A novel formulation for *content* and *style* aware audio-driven facial animation where disentangled styles evolve from adapting audio speech self-reconstruction to facial animation generation. (2) Non-autoregressive (NAR) modeling for audio and text pair with a modified computational-efficient Laplacian loss. (3) Our formulation leads to both audio style transfer and content modification.

## 2 Related Works

Audio-driven Mesh Vertex Animation. Several audio-driven facial animation methods generate animated 3D meshes directly \[[Karras et al.(2017)Karras, Aila, Laine, Herva, and Lehtinen](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx18), [Cudeiro et al.(2019)Cudeiro, Bolkart, Laidlaw, Ranjan, and Black](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx5), [Richard et al.(2021)Richard, Zollhöfer, Wen, De la Torre, and Sheikh](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx36), [Fan et al.(2022)Fan, Lin, Saito, Wang, and Komura](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx7), [Yang et al.(2023)Yang, Wei, Zhong, and Wang](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx51), [Ma et al.(2024)Ma, Zhu, Qi, Qian, Zhang, and Lei](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx28)\] from audio. The early work from Kerras *et al*\bmvaOneDot\[[Karras et al.(2017)Karras, Aila, Laine, Herva, and Lehtinen](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx18)\] builds a subject dependent regression model with a learned latent emotional state. Cudeiro *et al*\bmvaOneDot\[[Cudeiro et al.(2019)Cudeiro, Bolkart, Laidlaw, Ranjan, and Black](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx5)\] takes DeepSpeech \[[Hannun et al.(2014)Hannun, Case, Casper, Catanzaro, Diamos, Elsen, Prenger, Satheesh, Sengupta, Coates, et al.](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx12)\] features and one-hot speaker embedding to regress the 3D mesh in FLAME \[[Li et al.(2017)Li, Bolkart, Black, Li, and Romero](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx23)\] 3DMM. Meshtalk \[[Richard et al.(2021)Richard, Zollhöfer, Wen, De la Torre, and Sheikh](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx36)\] presents a two-stage method with semantically-disentangled expressive categorical hidden vectors and an auto-regressive (AR) network to sample from this space to control the mesh decoder.

FaceFormer \[[Fan et al.(2022)Fan, Lin, Saito, Wang, and Komura](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx7)\] builds a multi-subject pipeline, featuring a Wav2Vec2 \[[Baevski et al.(2020)Baevski, Zhou, Mohamed, and Auli](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx3)\] encoder, and an AR transformer decoder with biased attention. Several other works \[[Xing et al.(2023)Xing, Xia, Zhang, Cun, Wang, and Wong](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx50), [Thambiraja et al.(2022)Thambiraja, Habibie, Aliakbarian, Cosker, Theobalt, and Thies](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx42)\] extend FaceFormer. For instance, CodeTalker \[[Xing et al.(2023)Xing, Xia, Zhang, Cun, Wang, and Wong](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx50)\] uses a discrete codebook of motion primitives learnt via vector quantized autoencoder (VQ-VAE), to avoid the regression-to-mean problem that leads to over-smoothed facial motions. Imitator \[[Thambiraja et al.(2022)Thambiraja, Habibie, Aliakbarian, Cosker, Theobalt, and Thies](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx42)\] allows adaption to new users.

The method in \[[Aylagas et al.(2022)Aylagas, Leon, Teye, and Tollmar](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx2)\] uses a conditional variational autoencoder (cVAE) conditioned on contextual speech features. A diffusion-based transformer backbone is employed in \[[Ma et al.(2024)Ma, Zhu, Qi, Qian, Zhang, and Lei](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx28)\] with a biased conditional attention mechanism. The work in \[[Yang et al.(2023)Yang, Wei, Zhong, and Wang](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx51)\] uses facial images to bridge the audio and 3D mesh, where the visual encoder is essentially a 3D face tracker.

Audio-driven 3D Blendshapes/Visemes/Portraits. Other works use animation representations based on 3D head models, e.g. blendshapes and visemes \[[Edwards et al.(2016)Edwards, Landreth, Fiume, and Singh](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx6), [Zhou et al.(2018)Zhou, Xu, Landreth, Kalogerakis, Maji, and Singh](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx56), [Medina et al.(2022)Medina, Tome, Stoll, Tiede, Munhall, Hauptmann, and Matthews](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx30), [Abdelaziz et al.(2020)Abdelaziz, Kumar, Seivwright, Fanelli, Binder, Stylianou, and Kajarekar](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx1), [Yi et al.(2020)Yi, Ye, Zhang, Bao, and Liu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx53), [Peng et al.(2023)Peng, Wu, Song, Xu, Zhu, He, Liu, and Fan](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx33)\]. Such methods often require high-quality data that is obtained via either 3D scans followed by 3DMM registration, or 3D face tracking on recordings in well-controlled environments.

Although, we desire facial animation as the final output, several methods use sparse landmarks \[[Wang et al.(2020b)Wang, Wang, Sun, Liu, Liang, and Li](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx47), [Prajwal et al.(2020)Prajwal, Mukhopadhyay, Namboodiri, and Jawahar](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx34), [Zhou et al.(2020)Zhou, Han, Shechtman, Echevarria, Kalogerakis, and Li](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx57), [Ye et al.(2023)Ye, Jiang, Ren, Liu, He, and Zhao](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx52)\], feature maps \[[Lu et al.(2021)Lu, Chai, and Cao](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx26)\], blendshapes \[[Thies et al.(2020)Thies, Elgharib, Tewari, Theobalt, and Nießner](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx43), [Ji et al.(2021)Ji, Zhou, Wang, Wu, Loy, Cao, and Xu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx15)\], or vertex animation \[[Lahiri et al.(2021)Lahiri, Kwatra, Frueh, Lewis, and Bregler](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx20)\] as intermediate representations for facial image synthesis. Works in \[[Fried et al.(2019)Fried, Tewari, Zollhöfer, Finkelstein, Shechtman, Goldman, Genova, Jin, Theobalt, and Agrawala](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx9), [Li et al.(2021a)Li, Wang, Zhang, Ding, Zheng, Yu, and Fan](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx22)\] use a text-blendshape-human head (image) formulation. Methods, such as, \[[Ji et al.(2021)Ji, Zhou, Wang, Wu, Loy, Cao, and Xu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx15), [Ji et al.(2022)Ji, Zhou, Wang, Wu, Wu, Xu, and Cao](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx16), [Li et al.(2021a)Li, Wang, Zhang, Ding, Zheng, Yu, and Fan](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx22), [Gururani et al.(2022)Gururani, Mallya, Wang, Valle, and Liu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx11)\] model emotion-aware talking head image synthesis with intermediate blendshapes/edge maps. Some recent works use diffusion models \[[Stypułkowski et al.(2023)Stypułkowski, Vougioukas, He, Zięba, Petridis, and Pantic](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx40), [Shen et al.(2023)Shen, Zhao, Meng, Li, Zhu, Zhou, and Lu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx38)\] for portraits animation.

Styles in TTS are challenging to model and lack a clear definition. Explicit labels such as emotions, or speaker codecs can be used as styles \[[Luong et al.(2017)Luong, Takaki, Henter, and Yamagishi](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx27)\]. Styles can also be learned in an unsupervised manner. For instance, in \[[Skerry-Ryan et al.(2018)Skerry-Ryan, Battenberg, Xiao, Wang, Stanton, Shor, Weiss, Clark, and Saurous](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx39)\] a prosody embedding from a reference audio is learnt to synthesize speech in the Tacotron pipeline \[[Shen et al.(2018)Shen, Pang, Weiss, Schuster, Jaitly, Yang, Chen, Zhang, Wang, Skerrv-Ryan, Saurous, Agiomvrgiannakis, and Wu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx37)\]. Similarly, \[[Wang et al.(2018)Wang, Stanton, Zhang, Ryan, Battenberg, Shor, Xiao, Jia, Ren, and Saurous](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx48)\] proposes global style tokens (GSTs) that effectively take into account various noise and speaker factors. Li *et al*\bmvaOneDot\[[Li et al.(2022)Li, Han, and Mesgarani](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx25)\] employs a two stage method, where style vectors are extracted in the first stage via Mel-spectrum reconstruction.

Multi-scale hierarchical style can also be exploited. Sun *et al*\bmvaOneDot\[[Sun et al.(2020)Sun, Zhang, Weiss, Cao, Zen, and Wu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx41)\] models prosody at utterance, word and phoneme levels with a cVAE, while Li *et al*\bmvaOneDot\[[Li et al.(2021b)Li, Song, Li, Wu, Jia, and Meng](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx24)\] learns utterance-level and quasi-phoneme-level style features. GenerSpeech \[[Huang et al.(2022)Huang, Ren, Liu, Cui, and Zhao](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx13)\] employs global speaker and emotion embeddings, together with prosody styles at frame, phoneme, and word levels.

## 3 Our Method

To develop a style-aware audio-driven facial animation method, the style needs to be disentangled from content. We propose transferring style information learned from high-resource speech data, to low-resource audio-visual data, with a two stage scheme.

Stage I: Mel-Spectrum Reconstruction. In Stage I, we learn styles that control speech generation via Mel-spectrum reconstruction. We employ a backbone structure (phoneme encoder, variance adapter, decoder) similar to Fastspeech2 \[[Ren et al.(2020)Ren, Hu, Tan, Qin, Zhao, Zhao, and Liu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx35)\], [Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation"), an end-to-end parallel speech synthesis network. The phoneme encoder and the decoder both use stacked self-attention layers \[[Vaswani et al.(2017)Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, Uszkoreit, Jones, Gomez, Kaiser, and Polosukhin](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx44)\]. The variance adapter is simplified, shown in bottom left of [Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation"). The phoneme duration is calculated via forced-alignment of the driving audio with paired text using the Montreal forced aligner (MFA)\[[McAuliffe et al.(2017)McAuliffe, Socolof, Mihuc, Wagner, and Sonderegger](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx29)\].

Our formulation contains disentangled content and style, where the content is the length-regulated phoneme sequence. To encourage the disentanglement of style from content, the style encoder, shown in bottom right of [Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation"), is designed as follows.

Resembling the two global tokens used in \[[Huang et al.(2022)Huang, Ren, Liu, Cui, and Zhao](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx13)\], we initialise the style encoder with two pre-trained Wav2Vec2 \[[Baevski et al.(2020)Baevski, Zhou, Mohamed, and Auli](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx3)\] sub-networks for speaker (spk) verification and emotion (emo) classification tasks, denoted as W2V2_(spk) and W2V2_(emo), respectively. Since they are trained on two tasks that are not necessarily relying on content, and we use two models \[[wen Yang et al.(2021)wen Yang, Chi, Chuang, Lai, Lakhotia, Lin, Liu, Shi, Chang, Lin, Huang, Tseng, tik Lee, Liu, Huang, Dong, Li, Watanabe, Mohamed, and yi Lee](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx49)\] pre-trained on large datasets with a high content diversity, therefore disentanglement are achieved. The latent hidden embeddings from the above sub-nets are then combined to obtain the final style vector. We use Mel-spectrum reconstruction loss to guide Stage I training:

$`{\mathcal{L}_{stageI} = {MAE{({a{(t,f)}},{\hat{a}{(t,f)}})}}},`$

where $`a{(t,f)}`$ and $`\hat{a}{(t,f)}`$ is the source and predicted Mel-spectrum at the time-frequency unit $`(t,f)`$, and $`MAE{( \cdot , \cdot )}`$ represents the mean absolute error. The predicted $`\hat{a}{(t,f)}`$ is the top layer output from the decoder followed by a linear layer mapping $`\mathcal{F}_{s1}{( \cdot )}`$,

$`{{\hat{a}{(t,f)}} = {\mathcal{F}_{s1}{({\text{Decoder}{(\text{Text},\mathbf{s})}})}}},`$

where Text is the content component in the form of duration regulated phoneme embedding, and $`\mathbf{s}`$ denotes the extracted style vector.

Stage II: 3D Vertex Generation. We slightly modify the model architecture at Stage II. The top layer of the decoder is mapped to the high-dimensional vertex space $`{\mathbb{V}} \in {3N_{v}}`$ instead, where $`N_{v}`$ denotes the number of vertices in a head model topology. This mapping process $`\mathcal{F}_{s2}{( \cdot )}`$ is achieved by a sequential network containing a linear layer with a Tanh activation and another linear layer, to predict the 3D mesh $`\hat{\mathbf{V}} \in {{3N_{v}} \times T}`$ spanning in total $`T`$ frames,

$`{\hat{\mathbf{V}} = {\mathcal{F}_{s2}{({\text{Decoder}{(\text{Text},\mathbf{s})}})}}}.`$

During Stage II training, only a few parameters are fine-tuned to adapt to the new task. Few landmark vertices, e.g. the 68 Multi-PIE \[[Gross et al.(2010)Gross, Matthews, Cohn, Kanade, and Baker](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx10)\] are perceptually and visually important. Particularly, we are more interested in the 20 key mouth landmarks that highlight the mouth motion. We directly extract them from the full vertex-set $`\hat{\mathbf{V}}`$, denoted as $`\hat{\mathbf{K}}`$. We use the following loss to train Stage II:

``` math
{\mathcal{L}_{stageII} = {\mathcal{L}_{geometry} + \mathcal{L}_{temporal} + {\lambda\mathcal{L}_{{lap} - {mod}}}}},
```

here $`\mathcal{L}_{geometry}`$ is the sum of MAE losses over vertices $`\hat{\mathbf{V}}`$ and landmarks $`\hat{\mathbf{K}}`$. To prioritize mouth movements that are more correlated with the audio, a weighting mask is applied onto the vertex space, where vertices near the mouth region are given higher priority. Similarly, higher weights are given to the interior mouth landmarks when calculating the landmark loss. $`\mathcal{L}_{temporal}`$ is the sum of MAE losses over the first-order difference (dynamics) of vertices and landmarks, in order to promote temporal smoothness.

$`\mathcal{L}_{{lap} - {mod}}`$, weighted by $`\lambda`$, is the Laplacian loss that boosts spatial smoothness and mitigates high-frequency noise in the generated meshes. The Laplacian loss can be directly applied onto the predicted surface $`\hat{\mathbf{V}}`$. However, this involves a very high computational complexity, especially for a head topology with a large number of vertex $`N_{v}`$. The complexity scales linearly with the number of batched input frames. Besides, the computation needs back propagation through all unfrozen layers of the pipeline. Denote the last layer weight matrix in $`\mathcal{F}_{s2}{( \cdot )}`$ as $`\mathbf{W} \in {{3N_{v}} \times D}`$, and each frame of $`\hat{\mathbf{V}}`$ is essentially a linear combination of column basis in $`\mathbf{W}`$; thus we approximate $`\mathcal{L}_{{lap} - {mod}}{(\hat{\mathbf{V}})}`$ with $`\mathcal{L}_{{lap} - {mod}}{(\mathbf{W})}`$. This greatly reduces the complexity when $`D`$ is small, and the associated gradient back-propagation stops after only one layer, essentially forming $`\mathcal{L}_{{lap} - {mod}}`$ as a regularisation term. For Stage II training, most of the module are fixed ([Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation")). Particularly, in order to allow learning prominent visual difference that are not confined by audio speaking styles, the style encoder is further fine-tuned, together with the decoder.

Inference pipeline. Once the model is built after the above two stage training, the inference pipeline takes in one driving audio together with the associated transcript (to provide content) and one reference audio (to provide style) in a mix ’n match manner, in order to directly generate the facial animations. During inference, Stage I model won’t be employed and we only use the evolved model weights in Stage II model.

## 4 Results

Datasets. We use two audio datasets for Stage I, emotional speech database (ESD) \[[Zhou et al.(2022)Zhou, Sisman, Liu, and Li](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx55)\] and LJSpeech \[[Ito and Johnson(2017)](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx14)\]. All the English data in ESD is used, denoted as ESD-EN with the total duration of around 13.5 hours. ESD-EN contains utterances from 10 speakers and 5 emotional categories. Yet, it greatly suffers from limited content coverage with only 350 parallel transcripts. On the other hand, LJSpeech covers a high diversity in content with varying transcripts for each recorded passage. To balance the two datasets, we chose 2000 sequences from LJSpeech (similar amount of sequences for each subject in ESD-EN), denoted as LJSpeech-2K, with total duration around 3.7 hours. Combination of these two datasets balances content and style coverage and avoids over-fitting to a particular speaker or limited transcripts.

We use two audio-mesh datasets during Stage II to train and test of our pipeline – the VOCAset \[[Cudeiro et al.(2019)Cudeiro, Bolkart, Laidlaw, Ranjan, and Black](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx5)\] and BIWI \[[Fanelli et al.(2010)Fanelli, Gall, Romsdorfer, Weise, and Van Gool](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx8)\], both contain registered 3D vertices from high quality scans. The VOCAset employs the FLAME \[[Li et al.(2017)Li, Bolkart, Black, Li, and Romero](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx23)\] head topology with 5023 vertices per mesh, sampled at 60 fps (downsampled to 30 fps). It contains recordings from 12 subjects, each uttering 40 sequences in neutral tone with different transcripts. The BIWI dataset uses a dense topology (23370 vertices per mesh), sampled at 25 fps. It includes 14 subjects, each uttering 40 parallel sequences in neutral and affective tones.

Data Preprocessing. All audio signals are normalised with mono-channel, 16KHz sampling rate, and maximum magnitude of 1.0. A 80 dimensional Mel-spectrum is extracted with 1024-size sliding Hanning window overlapped at 256 samples. The English transcripts are first normalised and then converted with ARPABET phonetic transcription \[[Jurafsky and Martin(2019)](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx17)\]. To avoid out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words, we update the lexicon dictionary with an English grapheme to phoneme (G2P) package \[[Park and Kim(2019)](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx32)\]. The Montreal forced aligner (MFA) \[[McAuliffe et al.(2017)McAuliffe, Socolof, Mihuc, Wagner, and Sonderegger](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx29)\] is used to obtain the phoneme duration. Since, the audio frame-level sampling rate (16 ms) and the video sampling rate (30 fps for VOCAset and 25 fps for BIWI) are inconsistent, spline interpolation is applied in Stage II top layer output to align the network output with the video sampling rate.

Pipeline Architecture and Configuration. The backbone structure in [Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation") has some shared modules with FastSpeech-2 \[[Ren et al.(2020)Ren, Hu, Tan, Qin, Zhao, Zhao, and Liu](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx35)\], i.e. the phoneme encoder, and decoder, that feature stacked self-attention layers. The variance adaptor is modified such that ground-truth phoneme duration is used for length regulation, and the style vector is added on top. The style encoder is initialised with two Wav2Vec2 networks, followed by stacked linear layers, [Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation") (Right).

During Stage I training, we use the default train-validation-test split for ESD-EN, and split LJSpeech-2k with a 80-10-10 partitions. During Stage II training, both VOCAset and BIWI are split with 80-10-10 partitions. Adam optimizer with the same learning-rate (LR) scheduler in Vaswani *et al*\bmvaOneDot\[[Vaswani et al.(2017)Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, Uszkoreit, Jones, Gomez, Kaiser, and Polosukhin](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx44)\] is used in Stage I, where the LR scheduler is modified for Stage II training with $`{lr} = 0.01`$ and 1600 warm-up steps. Gradient-clip of 1.0 is applied.

Trained on a single A10G GPU, Stage I converged in about 24 hours after 500k steps. After stage I training, we show the T-SNE plot of the extracted style vectors over the ESD validation data, for different subjects and emotions in [Figure 2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S2.F2 "In 2 Related Works ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation"), where well-clustered patterns can be observed for both identities and emotions. We compare our extracted styles [Figure 2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S2.F2 "In 2 Related Works ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation")(a)+(b) with two baseline methods, the speaker embeddings in \[[Wan et al.(2020)Wan, Wang, Papir, and Moreno](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx45)\] and the style embeddings in \[[Min et al.(2021)Min, Lee, Yang, and Hwang](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx31)\].

In Stage I, the learned style vectors adapt to reconstruction of Mel-spectrum (audio speech). In Stage II, they adapt to generation of 3D vertices (visual speech). Despite audio speech and visual speech being highly correlated, there still exists some essential difference. E.g., two people whose voice sound similar may have very different articulatory movements and thus result in different facial motions. To cope with this discrepancy, the learned styles will evolve from audio-prominent attributes to visual-prominent attributes. To validate this idea, we randomly select six subjects (three males, three females) from VOCAset and BIWI, and extract the style vectors learned in Stage I and Stage II, respectively. Their TSNE plots are shown in [Figure 3](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S3.F3 "In 3 Our Method ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation"). Take the VOCAset for example, the three males are distributed in the centre while the three females on the edge after Stage I, possibly due to the different range of pitches. However, after Stage II, M1 and F2 are pushed together, which might due to these two subjects having similar range of mouth openings.

Ablation on Laplacian-Mod Regularisation. In Eq. [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S3.E4 "Equation 4 ‣ 3 Our Method ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation"), we use Laplacian-mod loss with $`\lambda = 1.0`$ to mitigate high-frequency noise that occurs when extrapolating beyond the training set. Tikhonov regularization is often used at the deformation stage to avoid such artifacts, yet without considering the connections between vertices. We ablate Tikhonov loss weighted at 1.0 (Tik1.0) and 10.0 (Tik10.0) respectively, as well as when no regularisation is applied (w/o), and groundtruth. Without smoothing constraints (w/o), high-frequency artifacts can be generated. Obvious residual outliers persists for both levels of Tikhonov losses.

Ablation on Different Loss Terms. Our loss terms contribute differently to the overall performance. We ablate over the following four situations: (1) When only vertex loss is applied without the weighting mask, denoted as ver$`\_`$only. (2) When weighted vertex loss is used, denoted as wei$`\_`$ver. (3) When the weighting mask, vertex, landmark loss are all used, denoted as wei$`\_`$ver$`\_`$lmk. (4) On top of wei$`\_`$ver$`\_`$lmk, the Laplacian-mod loss is integrated, denoted as Lap-Mod. The following three quantitative evaluation metrics are employed: (a) vertex distance $`d\_ ver`$ (b) landmark distance $`d\_ lmk`$ (c) mouth closure distance with 3 frame latency allowance $`d\_ close_{3}`$. The first two metrics are calculated as the per-vertex Frobenius norm. As for $`d\_ close_{3}`$, the mouth closures are first detected, which is often related to the bilabial consonants “B”, “P”, “M”, during which, $`d\_ close_{3}`$ is calculated as the minimum estimated interior mouth distance within $`\pm 3`$ frames to allow for audio-visual off-sync latency. Results are averaged on the VOCA validation data.

Overall, $`d\_ ver`$ and $`d\_ lmk`$ stay consistent over the above ablation scenarios. When we apply the extra weighting mask, i.e. wei$`\_`$ver, the mouth closure distance as well as the landmark distance improve, this proves that the proposed weighting mask prioritises the mouth region motions. Further, the applied mask encourages a quick convergence. Compared with ver$`\_`$only, only half the epochs (200 epochs vs 400 epochs) are needed before convergence, saving 50$`\%`$ training time. ### 4.2 Style- and Content-Aware Editing

Due to the disentanglement of style and content components as well as the generative synthesis in our proposed pipeline, novel applications of style transfer and content editing are enabled, that were not possible in other audio-driven facial animation methods.

Style-Based Editing. After Stage II fine-tuning, the pipeline is able to infer the implicit speaking style from the driving audio and control the facial animation generation. Besides the driving audio, the style embedding can be extracted from a reference audio of different speaking style, in order to change the style of the generated animation. For instance, [Figure 6](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S4.F6 "In 4.1 Baseline Comparison ‣ 4 Results ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation") shows one example of directly using the driving audio and another reference audio for the style extraction, on both the VOCAset and BIWI datasets. We observe that direct inference generates close-to-groundtruth output, while style-transferred inference generates overall in-sync but different speaking styles.

Content-Based Editing. Besides style manipulation, we can also modify the aligned content and control the output. We choose an example where one person says “gregory and tom chose to watch cartoons in the afternoon”, and we replace the words “watch cartoons (W AA1 CH K AA0 R T UW1 N Z)” in the transcript with “read books (R IY1 D B UH1 K S)”. We also test muting “watch cartoons” by replacing it with the silence token (sil) after the alignment. [Figure 7](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#S4.F7.fig1 "In 4.2 Style- and Content-Aware Editing ‣ 4 Results ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation") samples the time snippet associated with the modified content. Using the original transcript (top), and two modified versions (bottom) yield different facial motions, accordingly. When we mute the selected segment, which is a short pause, there is a smooth transition process between the utterance of the two contextual words “to” and “in”, rather than simply enforcing mouth closures.

Computation analysis. The proposed method involves processing on both GPU (inference) and CPU (pre-processing). Tested on Nvidia GF RTX 3070 GPU, ignoring the overhead (load model, etc.), the average inference time is less than $`0.1`$ second for a 5-second-long audio, thanks to our efficient non-auto-regressive (NAR) architecture design, while the AR baselines (\[[Fan et al.(2022)Fan, Lin, Saito, Wang, and Komura](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx7), [Xing et al.(2023)Xing, Xia, Zhang, Cun, Wang, and Wong](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx50)\]) take much longer, e.g. 2.5 seconds for \[[Fan et al.(2022)Fan, Lin, Saito, Wang, and Komura](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#bib.bibx7)\]. However, our model’s computational bottleneck is the pre-processing on a CPU machine, such as text normalisation, Mel-spectrum generation, and Montreal forced aligner (MFA). Particularly, MFA is the most time consuming (80+ seconds on an AMD Ryzen 9 5900HX processor) without batch-processing.

## 5 Conclusion & Limitation

In this paper, we propose a two stage content and style aware audio-driven facial animation method. Style representation is achieved via speech-domain synthesis in Stage I, leveraging advances in TTS. In Stage II, the extracted style information contributes to style aware face animation generation and furthers adaption to the visual modality. Compared to other SOTA baselines, our method shows advantages over various evaluations, and provides more flexibility – rather than assigning a specific identity style in the training data. Our method can infer the implicit style information from either the driving audio or a reference audio. Further, we can modify the content in the form of aligned text to generate new content articulations, while preserving the speaking style. We stress the essential limitation that our method relies an external audio-text alignment module, whose accuracy maybe affected by inaccurate transcripts as well as adverse acoustic environments, e.g. cross-talk, non-speech voice, and background noise. A potential solution to address these issues is to improve or augment the content representation, e.g. using frame-level semantic tokens of “what” is being uttered.

We also need to stress the potential negative ethical concerns. For instance, when combined with downstream tasks of audio-driven neural rendering and photo-realistic facial animation, misuse of this technology with malicious purposes cannot be overlooked. Proactive methods such as watermarking for spoofing detection should be considered.

## Appendix B Dataset and Pipeline Architecture

Dataset. We have considered both the VOCAset and BIWI datasets in our stage II training, that contain registered high quality audio/4D mesh pairs. The VOCAset head topology includes 5023 points that are un-evenly distributed, covering the whole head and neck. On the other hand, the BIWI topology contains 23770 vertices that are relatively even but high-densely distributed, and it covers only the front narrow face region. For each dataset, we randomly chose three males and three females, and calculated the temporal variations per vertex for each subject. The two head typologies are shown in [Figure Appx.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#A2.F3 "In Appendix B Dataset and Pipeline Architecture ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation"). The VOCAset and BIWI vertex values are two orders of different. As a result, we have scaled the VOCAset by 100 thus consistent training parameters can be used for both datasets.

When calculating the geometric vertex loss, a weighting mask has been applied to prioritise vertices more related to audio-correlated facial motions. The empirically-set weighting masks for the VOCAset and BIWI are shown in the right column of [Figure Appx.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#A2.F3 "In Appendix B Dataset and Pipeline Architecture ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation") where different colors denote different mask values. The two mask values in VOCAset are $`\lbrack 0.5,1.5\rbrack`$, and the four mask values in BIWI are $`\lbrack 0.5,1.0,2.0,4.0\rbrack`$.

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x15.png)

Figure Appx.3: The VOCAset topology (top) and the BIWI topology (bottom), where the intensity of the shaded green color denotes the motion variations of each vertex, which most happens in the mouth and jaw regions. The VOCAset is more stable, with less movements over the face boundaries. Based on the distributions of the facial motions, we have empirically applied two different vertex masks (Sec 3.2) for Stage II training, with two segments for VOCAset and 4 segments for BIWI respectively highlighting different colors (far right), to prioritise audio-correlated facial motions.

Pipeline Architecture. Our model’s backbone structure contains a phoneme encoder that represents content, a style encoder with disentangled style (prosody, identity, intonation .etc) representation. A parameterless variance adaptor combines the style and length-regulated content, which are then fed into a decoder. The phoneme encoder and the decoder employ the same structure as these modules being used in FastSpeech2, i.e. stacked transformer layers. The style encoder features two pre-trained Wav2Vec2 sub-networks, which are pre-trained on large datasets (LibriSpeech, Voxceleb 1, IEMOCAP), in total 1000+ hrs. This usage of high-resource audio data has encouraged the extracted style embedding to be disentangled from content. Two project layers $`\mathcal{F}_{s1}`$ and $`\mathcal{F}_{s2}`$ are applied onto the decoder output, to generate either Mel-spectrum (Stage I) or head meshes (Stage II). Depending on the audio-mesh datasets that have different number of vertices $`N_{v}`$ (e.g., 5123 for VOCAset, and 23770 for BIWI), the last layer in $`\mathcal{F}_{s2}`$ changes with different head typologies.

Details of the parameter setting in our model are listed in Table [Appx.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.07005v2#A2.T1 "Table Appx.1 ‣ Appendix B Dataset and Pipeline Architecture ‣ Content and Style Aware Audio-Driven Facial Animation").
