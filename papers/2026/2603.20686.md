# SNAP: Speaker Nulling for Artifact Projection in Speech Deepfake Detection

Kyudan Jung    Jihwan Kim    Minwoo Lee    Soyoon Kim    Jeonghoon Kim    Jaegul Choo    Cheonbok Park ¹KAIST AI, ²NAVER Cloud [cbok.park@navercorp.com](2603.20686v1/mailto:cbok.park@navercorp.com)

###### Abstract

Recent advancements in text-to-speech technologies enable generating high-fidelity synthetic speech nearly indistinguishable from real human voices. While recent studies show the efficacy of self-supervised learning-based speech encoders for deepfake detection, these models struggle to generalize across unseen speakers. Our quantitative analysis suggests these encoder representations are substantially influenced by speaker information, causing detectors to exploit speaker-specific correlations rather than artifact-related cues. We call this phenomenon speaker entanglement. To mitigate this reliance, we introduce SNAP, a speaker-nulling framework. We estimate a speaker subspace and apply orthogonal projection to suppress speaker-dependent components, isolating synthesis artifacts within the residual features. By reducing speaker entanglement, SNAP encourages detectors to focus on artifact-related patterns, leading to state-of-the-art performance.

## 1 Introduction

Advancements in speech generation now produce high-fidelity audio that is virtually indistinguishable from authentic recordings, enhancing digital media accessibility through applications such as voice assistants and automated content creation (Chen et al., 2024; Du et al., 2024, 2025; Lee et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2024). However, the rising quality and availability of these models also increase the potential for misuse, facilitating the dissemination of misinformation (Rahman et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024; Chesney and Citron, 2019) and the execution of sophisticated fraud, such as voice phishing and social engineering attacks (Stupp, 2019). This dual nature of speech generation underscores the critical need for robust, generalized detection systems (Koutsianos et al., 2025; Firc et al., 2025; Negroni et al., 2025; Stan et al., 2025; Klein et al., 2025) capable of adapting to constantly evolving generative architectures (Doan et al., 2025; Klein et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Baser et al., 2024).

To address these challenges, initiatives such as the ASVspoof (Yamagishi et al., 2021) have been established. Building upon advancements like AASIST (Jung et al., 2022) and WavLM-ECAPA-TDNN (Desplanques et al., 2020), recent studies report that self-supervised learning (SSL)-based speech encoder models excel in speech deepfake detection (Combei et al., 2024; Tran et al., 2025). Among these, WavLM is well-known for its exceptional speaker identification capabilities, which stem from a pre-training objective that employs a denoising strategy to actively remove both background noise and overlapping voices from the target speech (Chen et al., 2021). However, we hypothesize that this strong sensitivity to speaker identity inadvertently hinders the effectiveness of the model when repurposed for deepfake detection. Through our experiments, we confirm this hypothesis, examining that a significant hurdle in current SSL-based deepfake detection is the phenomenon of speaker entanglement.

Our analysis reveals that in SSL representations from models such as WavLM (Chen et al., 2021), the embedding space is dominated by speaker identity rather than the synthesis artifacts essential for detection. Consequently, models often overfit to specific speakers encountered during training, which hinders their ability to capture intrinsic deepfake characteristics (Lin et al., 2024). This entanglement creates a decision boundary that is heavily influenced by speaker clusters, thereby limiting cross-speaker generalization.

To overcome this limitation, we propose a lightweight, speaker-agnostic detection framework. We demonstrate that the feature space can be mathematically decomposed into a speaker-dependent subspace, a context subspace, and a synthesis-artifact subspace (Harsanyi and Chang, 1994; Wang et al., 2023). By defining the speaker subspace and employing orthogonal projection (Harsanyi and Chang, 1994), we explicitly nullify speaker-related information within the embeddings. This process yields residual features that further isolate synthesis artifacts, allowing us to bypass the need for complex deep learning detectors. Remarkably, by simply training a linear classifier on these refined residuals, we achieve state-of-the-art performance, demonstrating robust generalization even against unseen speakers and novel TTS architectures. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:

- •
  We quantitatively analyze the speaker entanglement phenomenon, demonstrating that SSL representations are pre-dominated by speaker identity.
- •
  We propose a framework that mathematically defines the speaker subspace and nullifies speaker information via orthogonal subspace projection.
- •
  We demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art detection performance and robust generalization to unseen speakers and TTS models, utilizing a simple logistic regression with only 2,049 parameters.

## 2 Speaker Entanglement Phenomenon

In this section, we analyze the variance-related properties of audio representations extracted from an SSL model (Chen et al., 2021) to justify our proposed methodology.

### 2.1 Discriminative Capacity of WavLM Representations for Speaker Identity

WavLM (Chen et al., 2021) employs an utterance mixing strategy to explicitly preserve speaker characteristics, achieving SOTA performance in speaker verification. However, this strong embedding of speaker identity can hinder tasks requiring content or artifact analysis (Ruggiero et al., 2025). Our investigation into utilizing WavLM-Large for distinguishing synthetic from real speech reveals a critical limitation. As illustrated in Figure 1, t-SNE visualizations demonstrate that feature clustering is driven primarily by speaker identity rather than the real-synthetic distinction. Because the high variance associated with speaker identity overshadows the subtle variance caused by synthetic artifacts, the decision boundary becomes increasingly fragmented in multi-speaker environments, thereby complicating the classification task. We identify this phenomenon as speaker entanglement. To mitigate this issue, we designed our proposed method to explicitly disentangle these features, ensuring that the artifact information necessary for distinguishing between real and synthetic speech becomes the dominant variance factor. Furthermore, we demonstrate that removing speaker identity via our approach yields robust speaker-agnostic generalization (Section 4.4). These findings confirm that resolving speaker entanglement fundamentally enhances deepfake detection performance.

### 2.2 Subspace Decomposition

Based on our observations regarding speaker entanglement, we propose the following hypothesis: The high-dimensional WavLM feature space, denoted as $`\mathcal{H}`$, can be decomposed into a speaker-dependent subspace $`\mathcal{S}`$, a speaker-independent artifact subspace $`\mathcal{A}`$, and a residual context subspace $`\mathcal{C}`$:

``` math
\mathcal{H}=\mathcal{S}+\mathcal{A}+\mathcal{C}
```

To enhance deepfake detection performance, specifically to make the artifact features $`\mathcal{A}`$ more prominent, we devised a method that nulls the information contained within $`\mathcal{S}`$.

## 3 Methodology

To achieve speaker-agnostic synthetic speech detection, we propose the Speaker Nulling for Artifact Projection (SNAP) framework. SNAP explicitly removes speaker-dependent information from acoustic embeddings via orthogonal projection (Harsanyi and Chang, 1994), forcing the classifier to focus on synthesis artifacts.

### 3.1 Feature Extraction and Normalization

Given an input audio waveform $`x`$, we employ a pre-trained WavLM Large (Chen et al., 2021) encoder as our backbone. To capture complementary information ranging from low-level acoustic details to high-level semantic content, we extract hidden states from two specific transformer layers: the 8th layer ($`l=8`$) and the 22nd layer ($`l=22`$). This dual-layer selection aligns with previous analyses demonstrating that shallow layers in self-supervised models primarily capture low-level acoustic and phonetic properties, whereas deeper layers encode high-level linguistic and semantic representations (Pasad et al., 2022, 2023). These layers were selected based on empirical validation, as this combination yielded the most discriminative representations for synthetic speech detection in our preliminary experiments.

Let $`\mathbf{H}^{(l)}\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times D}`$ denote the sequence of hidden states extracted from the $`l`$-th layer, where $`T`$ is the sequence length and $`D`$ is the hidden dimension. We concatenate these representations along the feature dimension to form a feature matrix $`\mathbf{H}`$:

``` math
\mathbf{H}=\left[\mathbf{H}^{(8)}\,;\,\mathbf{H}^{(22)}\right]\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times 2D}
```

where $`[\cdot\,;\cdot]`$ denotes the concatenation operation. To obtain a fixed-size utterance-level representation, we apply mean pooling along the temporal axis:

``` math
\mathbf{f}=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\mathbf{H}_{t}\in\mathbb{R}^{2D}
```

Finally, we apply $`L_{2}`$ normalization to project the feature vector onto a hypersphere, ensuring numerical stability and facilitating the subsequent subspace projection:

``` math
\mathbf{z}=\frac{\mathbf{f}}{|\mathbf{f}|_{2}}
```

### 3.2 Speaker Subspace Nulling

A challenge in deepfake detection is that the dominant variance in the learned representations often stems from speaker identity rather than synthesis artifacts. To address this, we hypothesize that the feature space $`\mathbb{R}^{D}`$ can be decomposed into a speaker-dependent subspace $`\mathcal{S}`$ and the other $`\mathcal{S}^{\perp}`$ (Harsanyi and Chang, 1994; Wang et al., 2023).

We estimate the speaker subspace from inter-speaker differences. For each unique speaker $`u\in\mathcal{S}`$, we compute the centroid of their normalized embeddings:

``` math
\boldsymbol{\mu}_{u}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{I}_{u}|}\sum_{j\in\mathcal{I}_{u}}\mathbf{z}_{j}
```

where $`\mathcal{I}_{u}=\{j:s_{j}=u\}`$ denotes the set of sample indices belonging to speaker $`u`$. These centroids are stacked to form a centroid matrix $`\mathbf{C}\in\mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{S}|\times D}`$.

We apply PCA (Pomerantsev, 2014) on the centered centroid matrix $`\bar{\mathbf{C}}`$ to identify the principal directions of speaker variation. The covariance matrix is computed as:

``` math
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}|-1}\bar{\mathbf{C}}^{\top}\bar{\mathbf{C}}
```

Through eigendecomposition $`\boldsymbol{\Sigma}=\mathbf{V}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{V}^{\top}`$, we obtain eigenvectors sorted by descending eigenvalues. The top-$`K`$ eigenvectors form the speaker subspace basis:

``` math
\mathbf{U}_{K}=[\mathbf{v}_{1},\mathbf{v}_{2},\ldots,\mathbf{v}_{K}]\in\mathbb{R}^{2D\times K}
```

where $`K`$ is a hyperparameter controlling the dimensionality of the speaker subspace.

Subsequently, we construct a projection matrix to map the embeddings onto the orthogonal complement of the speaker subspace. Assuming that $`\mathbf{U}_{K}`$ represents the orthonormal basis vectors spanning $`\mathcal{S}`$, the projection matrix onto the speaker subspace is defined as $`\mathbf{P}_{spk}=\mathbf{U}_{K}\mathbf{U}_{K}^{\top}`$. The orthogonal projection matrix is then defined as:

``` math
\mathbf{P}_{\perp}=\mathbf{I}_{D}-\mathbf{U}_{K}\mathbf{U}_{K}^{\top}
```

where $`\mathbf{I}_{D}`$ is the $`D`$-dimensional identity matrix. Consequently, the speaker-agnostic residual feature is computed as:

``` math
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}=\mathbf{P}_{\perp}\mathbf{z}
```

This nulling operation (Harsanyi and Chang, 1994) ensures that $`\tilde{\mathbf{z}}`$ retains minimal speaker identity information while preserving synthesis artifacts and linguistic content.

### 3.3 Classification

Since speaker variations have been mitigated through orthogonal projection, a simple classifier suffices to discriminate between real and synthetic speech. We employ logistic regression:

``` math
\hat{y}=\sigma(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\tilde{\mathbf{z}}+b)
```

where $`\sigma(\cdot)`$ denotes the sigmoid function, and $`\mathbf{w}\in\mathbb{R}^{D}`$, $`b\in\mathbb{R}`$ are learnable parameters. The model is optimized using Binary Cross-Entropy loss:

``` math
\mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}}=-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left[y_{i}\log(\hat{y}_{i})+(1-y_{i})\log(1-\hat{y}_{i})\right]
```

The simplicity of the classifier validates that the projected features $`\tilde{\mathbf{z}}`$ effectively capture discriminative artifact information independent of speaker characteristics.

### 3.4 Inference

During inference, we utilize the orthogonal projection matrix $`\mathbf{P}_{\perp}`$ and the classification parameters $`\mathbf{w}`$ and $`b`$ learned from the training phase. Given a test input, we first extract the feature embedding $`\mathbf{z}`$ following Eq. 2–4. Subsequently, the embedding is projected onto the subspace as in Eq. 9, and the final classification is performed using Eq. 10.

## 4 Experiments and Results

### 4.1 Experimental Setup

In our experiments, we employ WavLM-Large to extract features from $`16`$ kHz resampled audio and project them onto a speaker-nullified subspace ($`P_{\perp}`$ with $`k=5`$) to preserve artifact cues while removing speaker information, subsequently training a logistic regression classifier. For training, we partition the ASVspoof 2019 LA (Todisco et al., 2019) dataset into an $`8:2`$ stratified train-validation split. Notably, due to the inherent nature of the dataset, there is no speaker overlap between the training and evaluation sets. We evaluate the model on the ASVspoof 2019/2021 LA/DF and In-the-Wild (Müller et al., 2022) benchmarks using the equal error rate (EER). We compare SNAP against both end-to-end and SSL-based baselines. End-to-end models include RawNet2 (weon Jung et al., 2020) and AASIST (Jung et al., 2022). SSL-based systems comprise Wav2vec2-ECAPA-TDNN (Desplanques et al., 2020; Kulkarni et al., 2024), WavLM-ECAPA-TDNN (Desplanques et al., 2020; Kulkarni et al., 2024), and a standard WavLM baseline utilizing a logistic regression classifier on the last hidden state of WavLM-Large.

### 4.2 Main Results

The experimental results are summarized in Table 1. On the ASV19LA dataset, our proposed model achieves an EER of 0.35%, marking a 56.25% improvement over WavLM-ECAPA-TDNN (Desplanques et al., 2020). Furthermore, our method achieves an EER of 5.42% on the challenging ASV21 DF partition, significantly outperforming the AASIST (Jung et al., 2022). Most notably, on the In-The-Wild (Müller et al., 2022), which consists of real-world data with unpredictable noise and channel variations, our model achieves an EER of 15.39%. This represents a substantial improvement over the WavLM baseline, which recorded an EER of 22.22%, thereby demonstrating the effectiveness of our speaker nulling method.

Notably, we achieve this significant performance boost by applying a simple classifier with just 2,049 parameters, once speaker information was neutralized in the features. This highlights the efficiency of the SNAP method. Our results illustrate that effective solutions often come from mathematical or algorithmic refinement, rather than relying exclusively on complex neural architectures. Overall, the results suggest that our model generalizes better to unseen attacks and diverse channel conditions compared to existing SSL-based and end-to-end methods.

We perform balanced sampling from the ASVspoof 2019 LA training dataset, selecting an equal number of bona fide and spoof utterances for each combination of attack type and speaker. The silhouette coefficient, based on cosine distance, is then calculated for each sample.

For comparison, the baseline utilizes the last hidden state of WavLM-Large as input features. In contrast, as described in Section 3, our method projects the features onto the complement of the speaker subspace. A higher silhouette score indicates that clusters are well-separated and cohesive.

Figure 2(a) presents the silhouette scores calculated based on speaker identity using the unseen five speakers. Here, the silhouette scores for our method (blue) decreased compared to the baseline (gray), dropping from $`0.026`$ to $`-0.002`$. This reduction in speaker clustering, combined with the improvement in class separation, confirms that our method effectively mitigates speaker entanglement. By suppressing speaker-specific information, the model amplifies the artifacts necessary for distinguishing between bona fide and spoofed speech.

Figure 2(b) illustrates the silhouette scores based on the clustering of bona fide versus spoof classes. SNAP demonstrates a significant increase in silhouette scores compared to the baseline, with the mean score rising from $`0.118`$ to $`0.181`$. This suggests that the speaker nulling approach enhances the discriminability between natural and synthetic speech.

In this section, we focus on examining synthetic speech generated by TTS models, emphasizing robustness against out-domain scenarios. Unlike standard ASVspoof datasets, we construct a custom dataset by partitioning the LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015) test set based on speaker identities. Specifically, we allocate 16 distinct speakers for training (800 samples) and another 16 unseen speakers for testing (200 samples), strictly maintaining a balanced label distribution across all sets. By using original audio as prompts and identical transcripts for CosyVoice2 (Du et al., 2024) and F5-TTS (Chen et al., 2024), we generate synthetic data preserving speaker and linguistic content. This strictly controlled setup enables a rigorous cross-evaluation of speaker invariance and generalization.

Table 2 summarizes the generalization performance of SNAP against strong baselines. In in-domain settings with unseen speakers, SNAP achieves flawless detection. This perfect performance demonstrates that SNAP successfully extracts discriminative, speaker-invariant artifacts without simply overfitting to speaker identities. More importantly, cross-domain evaluations highlight the exceptional generalization capabilities of SNAP to completely unseen TTS models. Regardless of the training-testing direction between CosyVoice2 and F5-TTS, SNAP maintains a consistently low EER, significantly outperforming all baselines. Although threshold-dependent metrics, such as accuracy and F1 score, may exhibit slight fluctuations due to misalignments with the default classification threshold in certain cross-domain scenarios, the persistently low EER proves that SNAP effectively preserves its inherent discriminative power against unseen synthetic artifacts. This cross-domain robustness is particularly notable given the fundamental differences in their vocoder architectures (Yan et al., 2022): CosyVoice2 utilizes a HiFi-GAN vocoder predicting 1D waveforms from mel-spectrograms, whereas F5-TTS employs a Vocos vocoder predicting complex spectrograms. Therefore, the ability of SNAP to maintain its discriminative capability across such distinct synthesis fingerprints strongly validates its robust generalization to entirely unknown TTS algorithms.

Finally, in the mixed-domain environment, SNAP completely mitigates cross-domain vulnerabilities, achieving zero errors. This confirms that exposing SNAP to diverse synthesis artifacts allows it to seamlessly expand its detection boundaries. Consequently, these results validate that SNAP learns universally generalizable representations, making it a highly practical solution against unknown synthetic speech attacks.

### 4.5 Impact of Nulling Speaker Information

To analyze the impact of nulling speaker information on deepfake speech detection, we incrementally increase the number of training speakers and compare SNAP against a WavLM baseline, following the identical setup detailed in Section 4.3. As illustrated in Figure 3, the baseline exhibits EER fluctuations or even degradation despite the increased number of training speakers. This performance drop is attributed to speaker entanglement, where the model prioritizes learning speaker identities rather than distinguishing synthesis artifacts. In contrast, SNAP consistently reduces the EER across diverse datasets as the speaker scale expands. Consequently, nulling speaker information effectively promotes data generalization and is crucial for constructing a speaker-robust deepfake detection system.

## 5 Conclusion

In this work, we addressed the phenomenon of speaker entanglement in SSL-based deepfake detection by proposing a speaker-nulling method via orthogonal projection. By isolating synthetic artifacts from speaker identities, our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance while generalizing well to unseen speakers. Also, it demonstrates robust generalization to unseen TTS models and stable convergence as the training speaker population increases. Future work will explore applying this speaker-disentanglement framework to other paralinguistic tasks, such as emotion recognition, enabling models to focus on relevant features unencumbered by speaker-specific variations.
