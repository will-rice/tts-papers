# Eroding Trust in Real Speech: A Large-Scale Study of Human Audio Deepfake Perception

Nicolas M. Müller Fraunhofer AISEC & Resemble AIGermany [nicolas.mueller@aisec.fraunhofer.de](2605.26136v1/mailto:nicolas.mueller@aisec.fraunhofer.de) and Wei Herng Choong Fraunhofer AISECGermany [wei.herng.choong@aisec.fraunhofer.de](2605.26136v1/mailto:wei.herng.choong@aisec.fraunhofer.de)

###### Abstract.

Audio deepfakes have improved rapidly recently, yet their effect on human trust in real speech remains unstudied. We present the largest listening study on audio deepfake perception to date, collecting 35,532 judgments from 1,768 participants across 138 text-to-speech and voice conversion systems. Our central finding is a skepticism shift: compared to a 2021 baseline, human accuracy on fake samples barely changed (72.9 % to 71.2 %), but accuracy on real samples dropped from 72.7 % to 64.1 %. Participants are not worse at detecting synthesis artifacts; rather, they increasingly distrust authentic speech. Samples generated by commercial and autoregressive language model systems proved hardest to detect (61.3 – 65.9 %), while those from traditional seq2seq and flow-matching models remain easier to spot (75.4 – 76.8 %). An ML detector that served as a reference point maintained over 94.5 % accuracy across all conditions. Our results suggest that the primary threat posed by modern deepfakes may not be mere deception, but the erosion of trust in genuine audio.

## 1. Introduction

The proliferation of text-to-speech (TTS) and voice conversion (VC) systems poses growing threats to trust in audio media. Deepfake audio has been used for CEO fraud (Stupp, 2019) and raises concerns for misinformation and evidence tampering (Chesney and Citron, 2019). More recently, cloned voices have enabled cryptocurrency scams, romance fraud, and impersonation attacks at scale (FBI San Francisco, 2026; Deloitte Center for Financial Services, 2024), including a \$25M deepfake video-call heist (Gover, 2024), AI-generated political robocalls (Federal Communications Commission, 2024), and widespread voice-cloning phone scams (McAfee, 2023). While machine-learning (ML)-based detectors achieve high accuracy on known attacks (Tak et al., 2021), they often fail to generalize to unseen TTS models (Müller et al., 2024). Human perception thus remains a critical, yet understudied, line of defense.

A 2021 study (Müller et al., 2022b) evaluated human perception on 13 attacks from ASVspoof 2019 (Wang et al., 2020), finding that 472 participants achieved 72.8 % accuracy. Since then, the TTS landscape has transformed: autoregressive language models like VALL-E (Wang et al., 2023) and Bark (Suno AI, 2023), flow-matching systems like F5-TTS (Chen et al., 2025), as well as commercial APIs such as Resemble AI (Resemble AI, 2024) produce speech that is increasingly difficult to distinguish from real recordings. No large-scale study has revisited how humans perceive this new generation of deepfakes.

Contribution. We address this gap with the largest audio deepfake perception study to date: 1,768 participants provided 35,532 judgments across 138 TTS and VC systems spanning 10 architecture families. We benchmark human performance against an ML detector, analyze the effect of demographics (age, IT skill, native language), test whether participants improve with practice, and compare directly to the 2021 baseline.

Our key findings are:

- •
  Human accuracy on audio deepfakes remained nearly unchanged (72.9 %$`\to`$71.2 %), but accuracy on genuine audio dropped from 72.7 % to 64.1 %, suggesting growing skepticism towards recorded speech.
- •
  Deepfakes generated by commercial and autoregressive language model (AR-LM) systems are hardest to detect (61.3 –65.9 %), while those from traditional seq2seq and flow-matching models remain easiest (75.4 –76.8 %).
- •
  Our baseline ML detector outperforms humans at 94.5 %, stable from the 2021 baseline of 95.5 %.
- •
  Participants’ ability to detect deepfakes improves with practice, as reflected in higher accuracy over successive rounds.
- •
  Age and native language show little association with detection ability; self-rated experts (IT skill 5) score $`{\sim}`$4 pp higher than everyone else, a small but significant effect.

We release the anonymized dataset¹¹1[https://huggingface.co/datasets/mueller91/human-perception-audio-deepfake-2026](https://huggingface.co/datasets/mueller91/human-perception-audio-deepfake-2026) and analysis code to support reproducibility.

## 2. Related Work

Human deepfake perception. A meta-analysis of 56 studies (86,155 participants) found an overall detection accuracy across different data modality of only 55.5%, with audio deepfakes detected best at 62% (Diel et al., 2024). For video deepfakes, Groh et al. (Groh et al., 2022) showed that 15,016 participants performed comparably to ML models, while Cooke et al. (Cooke et al., 2025) found cross-modal detection near chance level.

Audio-specific studies. Müller et al. (Müller et al., 2022b) evaluated 472  participants on 13  attacks from ASVspoof 2019 LA (Wang et al., 2020) and reported 72.8 % accuracy, with an ML detector reaching 95.5 %. Mai et al. (Mai et al., 2023) found similar rates (73%) across English and Mandarin, with minimal benefit from training. Warren et al. (Warren et al., 2024) demonstrated that humans and ML detectors make complementary errors and that preconceptions about accents and noise mislead human judges. San Segundo et al. (San Segundo et al., 2025) showed that language and speaking style influence perception, with a general bias toward classifying samples as real. Critically, all existing audio studies evaluate at most 13  attacks from pre-2021 systems and do not cover the modern TTS architectures that are now widely deployed. This study directly extends (Müller et al., 2022b) by replicating the same game interface and active-learning sampling procedure, enabling a controlled comparison across a four-year gap in TTS development.

TTS evolution. Seq2seq models such as Tacotron 2 (Shen et al., 2018) gave way to end-to-end approaches (VITS (Kim et al., 2021)), diffusion-based systems (Grad-TTS (Popov et al., 2021), StyleTTS 2 (Li et al., 2023)), autoregressive language models over codec tokens (VALL-E (Wang et al., 2023), Bark (Suno AI, 2023)), and flow-matching architectures (F5-TTS (Chen et al., 2025), CosyVoice (Du et al., 2024)). VALL-E 2 (Chen et al., 2024) claims human parity in zero-shot TTS, and commercial APIs such as Resemble AI (Resemble AI, 2024) make high-quality synthesis widely accessible. Human perception has not been evaluated against these systems at scale.

ML-based detection. The ASVspoof challenge series (Wang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025) has driven progress from end-to-end RawNet2 (Tak et al., 2021) to pre-trained detectors built on Wav2Vec 2.0 (Tak et al., 2022) and AASIST (Jung et al., 2022). However, ML detectors generalize poorly to unseen attacks (Müller et al., 2022a), making human perception a complementary and still poorly understood line of defense.

## 3. Study Design

In this section, we describe our study setup, including the web platform and game procedure, the composition of the audio corpus, the ML deepfake detector that serves as a reference for human performance, and the filtering criteria for constructing the final analysis set.

### 3.1. Web platform and procedure

We host a publicly accessible listening game online²²2[https://deepfake-total.com/spot_the_audio_deepfake](https://deepfake-total.com/spot_the_audio_deepfake). Before playing, each participant answered three demographic questions: age, self-rated IT skill (Likert scale, 1=novice to 5=expert), and whether English is their native language. No registration or account is required; we collect no data beyond what users provide voluntarily.

The listening game consists of individual rounds, of which the user can play as many as they please. In each round, the system presents a single audio clip. With probability $`p{=}0.5`$ the clip is bona fide; otherwise a fake attack is selected using a weighted active-learning scheme (Müller et al., 2022b): the sampling weight for attack $`i`$ is $`w_{i}=1-\mathrm{acc}_{i}/(1+\varepsilon)`$, where $`\mathrm{acc}_{i}`$ is the running human accuracy on that attack, so that trivially detectable systems are presented less often. $`\varepsilon`$ is a small constant ensuring a non-zero sampling probability. Participants may replay the clip as often as they wish before classifying it as _real_ or _fake_.

After the decision is made, the interface reveals both the ground truth and the ML detector’s prediction, providing participants with immediate feedback. The platform is mobile-responsive and all audio is in English. The interface and procedure follow (Müller et al., 2022b).

### 3.2. Audio data

Bona fide samples are drawn from three sources: LJSpeech (Ito and Johnson, 2017), the In-The-Wild corpus (Müller et al., 2022a), and ASVspoof 5 (Wang et al., 2025). Fake samples are largely drawn from ASVspoof 5 and the English subset of MLAAD (Müller et al., 2024). They cover 138 TTS and VC systems across 10 architecture families (c.f. Figure 5):

- •
  _Seq2Seq_ – encoder-decoder, e.g. Tacotron 2 (Shen et al., 2018)
- •
  _VITS_ – VAE + flow + GAN (Kim et al., 2021)
- •
  _XTTS_ – GPT-based multi-speaker TTS with VITS decoder
- •
  _Flow_ – flow-matching, e.g. F5-TTS (Chen et al., 2025), CosyVoice (Du et al., 2024)
- •
  _Diffusion_ – e.g. Grad-TTS (Popov et al., 2021), StyleTTS 2 (Li et al., 2023)
- •
  _AR-LM_ – autoregressive language models over codec tokens, e.g. VALL-E (Wang et al., 2023), Bark (Suno AI, 2023), Chatterbox (Resemble AI, 2025)
- •
  _VC_ – voice conversion, e.g. RVC, OpenVoice V2 (Qin et al., 2024)
- •
  _Commercial_ – proprietary APIs such as ElevenLabs (ElevenLabs, 2024) and Resemble AI (Resemble AI, 2024)
- •
  _ASVspoof 5_ – attacks from (Wang et al., 2025)

This represents a substantial expansion over the 2021 study (Müller et al., 2022b), which used only 13 attacks from ASVspoof 2019 LA.

### 3.3. ML classifier

An in-house ML deepfake detector runs alongside the human game as a reference point. The model combines Wav2Vec 2.0 features (Baevski et al., 2020) with an AASIST (Jung et al., 2022) back-end, trained on a mix of public and internal data. Its predictions are logged for every round, enabling direct human–machine comparison.

### 3.4. Filtering

We exclude participants who completed fewer than 5 rounds and attacks with fewer than 10 judgments, retaining 1,768  participants and 138  attacks for the 2026 study (Table 1).

## 4. Results

Table 1 summarizes both studies. The 2026 study is substantially larger than the 2021 study: 1,768  participants provided 35,532  judgments across 138  attacks from 10  architecture families, compared to 472  participants and 13  attacks in 2021.

The central finding is an asymmetric shift in human accuracy (Figure 1). On fake samples, accuracy barely changed: 72.9 %$`\to`$71.2 %. On real samples, however, accuracy dropped from 72.7 % to 64.1 %, an 8.6 percentage point decline. These results suggest that participants are not worse at hearing synthesis artifacts; rather, they have become more skeptical of all audio, increasingly classifying genuine recordings as fake. The ML detector remained stable at 95.5 % (2021) and 94.5 % (2026), outperforming humans by more than 20 percentage points in both periods.

### 4.1. Which architectures fool humans?

Figure 3 breaks down accuracy by TTS architecture family. Fake samples generated by commercial APIs are the hardest for humans to detect (61.3 %), followed by samples from AR-LM systems (65.9 %). These two families are also the most practically relevant: commercial APIs are widely accessible, and AR-LM models dominate the open-source landscape. Together, they account for 65  models and 8,929  samples, making them the largest group in our study. On the other hand, the samples generated by traditional seq2seq systems (75.4 %), flow-matching models (76.8 %), and ASVspoof 5 (85.9 %) remain substantially easier to detect. The ML detector maintains above 84 % accuracy across all architecture families and exceeds 99% on 4 of the 10 families. AR-LM and diffusion are the only families in which ML accuracy drops below 90% (83.7 % and 84.7 %, respectively), making them the most challenging for automated detection. See Figure 5 for a detailed, per-model analysis.

### 4.2. Demographics and learning

Self-reported demographics had limited effect on detection ability (Figure 2). Accuracy showed no meaningful trend with age, and native English speakers achieved no significant advantage over non-native speakers. Self-rated IT skill is mostly flat: levels 1–4 are indistinguishable (median $`{\sim}`$67%), but self-rated experts (skill=5) score $`{\sim}`$4 pp higher (median 72%), a small but statistically significant effect (Mann-Whitney $`p{<}0.001`$). These findings contrast with the 2021 study, where native speakers showed a clearer advantage and age had a negative correlation. Regarding learning effects (Figure 4), participants in their first five rounds achieved only 67.0 % accuracy, compared to 71.0 % for those who had played more than 15 rounds. Accuracy improved steadily over the first $`{\sim}`$20 rounds, after which it plateaued regardless of further exposure, even as participant count dropped from 1,768 to 136 by round 50. This mirrors the 2021 study, where immediate feedback likewise helped participants calibrate quickly but offered diminishing returns beyond the initial learning phase.

## 5. Discussion

Skepticism as a societal risk. The key result is not that humans fail to detect fakes, as they perform comparably to 2021 on fake samples, but that they increasingly distrust real audio. This “skepticism shift” has direct implications beyond deepfake detection: if listeners routinely doubt authentic recordings, the evidentiary value of audio in journalism, legal proceedings, and personal communication erodes regardless of whether actual deepfakes are involved. Therefore, awareness and media-literacy campaigns should address over-skepticism alongside deepfake detection training.

Architecture as threat model. Commercial APIs and AR-LM systems, which are among the most accessible synthesis technologies, produce fake samples that are also the hardest for humans to detect. The overall stability of human detection accuracy on fake samples (72.9 %$`\to`$71.2 %) masks a composition effect: for commercial and AR-LM systems, human accuracy drops to 65.4 %, while fake samples from legacy architectures are detected at 74.9 %. This creates a concerning alignment: the systems most likely to be misused are precisely those that best evade human judgment. Traditional seq2seq and flow-matching systems, though still actively developed, produce speech that humans detect more reliably, suggesting that their acoustic characteristics differ in perceptually salient ways.

Human–ML complementarity. Our ML detector maintained above 84 % accuracy even on categories where humans dropped to 61.3 %, but ML detectors are known to generalize poorly to unseen attacks (Müller et al., 2022a). Meanwhile, AR-LM and diffusion systems proved challenging for the ML detector (83.7 % and 84.7 %, respectively), indicating that no single defense is sufficient. Combining human and machine judgment, for instance, by flagging ML-uncertain samples for human review, could exploit their complementary error patterns.

Limitations. Participants were self-selected through a web-based game, skewing toward younger users (cf. Figure 2). Audio quality varied with participants’ playback equipment and browser compression. The study is in English-only, and participation is open and anonymous, so we cannot control for users who participated in both 2021 and 2026 studies. The active-learning sampling scheme, while improving coverage of difficult attacks, produces uneven per-attack sample counts.

## 6. Conclusion

We presented the largest listening study on audio deepfake perception to date. Our central finding is a skepticism shift: human accuracy on fake samples has remained stable since 2021, but accuracy on real samples dropped by 8.6 percentage points, suggesting that the primary effect of improved deepfakes is erosion of trust in authentic speech rather than inability to detect synthesis. Commercial and AR-LM systems, the most widely deployed architectures, are hardest for humans to detect, while ML detectors remain effective across all conditions. Future work should extend this paradigm to multilingual settings, track longitudinal changes in listener behavior, and explore human–ML collaborative detection.

## Ethical Considerations

This study was conducted via a publicly accessible web-based listening game. Participation is voluntary and requires no registration or account. We collect three coarse self-reported attributes (age bracket, IT skill level on a 1–5 scale, and whether English is the participant’s native language). No directly identifiable information such as names, email addresses, or IP addresses is recorded. For analysis, all attributes are aggregated into group-level statistics. Participants receive immediate feedback after each round. All audio samples are drawn from published corpora or publicly available TTS systems. This study involves only anonymous, voluntary interaction with publicly available audio stimuli, placing it below the threshold for formal ethical review under standard institutional guidelines for minimal-risk research.
