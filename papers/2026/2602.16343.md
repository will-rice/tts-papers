How to Label Resynthesized Audio:\
The Dual Role of Neural Audio Codecs in Audio Deepfake Detection
================================================================

###### Abstract

Since Text-to-Speech systems typically don’t produce waveforms directly, recent spoof detection studies use resynthesized waveforms from vocoders and neural audio codecs to simulate an attacker. Unlike vocoders, which are specifically designed for speech synthesis, neural audio codecs were originally developed for compressing audio for storage and transmission. However, their ability to discretize speech also sparked interest in language-modeling-based speech synthesis. Owing to this dual functionality, codec resynthesized data may be labeled as either bonafide or spoof. So far, very little research has addressed this issue. In this study, we present a challenging extension of the ASVspoof 5 dataset constructed for this purpose. We examine how different labeling choices affect detection performance and provide insights into labeling strategies.

Index Terms—  audio deepfake detection, neural audio codecs, codec-based speech synthesis

## 1 Introduction

A Neural Audio Codec (NAC) is originally proposed as a data-driven compression model that maps a waveform to a sequence of compact latent codes and reconstructs the signal from those codes with a learned decoder. Similar to classical codecs (e.g., Opus \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib27 "High-quality, low-delay music coding in the Opus codec")\], MP3) that are widely used to reduce bitrate and stabilize streaming under bandwidth constraints, NACs also enable efficient transmission. More specifically, modern NACs include a vector-quantized encoder-decoder architecture with one or more codebooks. The encoder projects the input audio into frame-level latent representations, which are then quantized by selecting indices from a learned codebook; the *decoder* reconstructs the waveform from these discrete indices, also known as *discrete tokens*. Compared to classical codecs, the discrete tokens from NACs can capture richer information including phonetics content, prosody, and timbre, while removing fine-grained redundancies.

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x1.png)

Fig. 1: Overview of the setting we investigate: Modern attackers utilize NACs to obtain discrete tokens for language modeling. Human speech is also encoded with NACs for storage or transmission. Real and fake speech sometimes share the same NAC decoders. Can we still detect the fakes?

As a result, discrete tokens provided by NACs can be used in many other tasks, such as speech synthesis \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib17 "Llasa: Scaling train-time and inference-time compute for llama-based speech synthesis"), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib18 "MARS6: A Small and Robust Hierarchical-Codec Text-to-Speech Model"), [17](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib16 "CSM - A Conversational Speech Generation Model"), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib9 "Fish-Speech: Leveraging Large Language Models for Advanced Multilingual Text-to-Speech Synthesis"), [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib8 "CosyVoice: A Scalable Multilingual Zero-shot Text-to-speech Synthesizer based on Supervised Semantic Tokens"), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib7 "CosyVoice 2: Scalable Streaming Speech Synthesis with Large Language Models"), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib15 "Cosyvoice 3: Towards in-the-wild speech generation via scaling-up and post-training")\]. These speech generation systems model the sequence of codec tokens and reconstruct them to waveforms using NAC decoders. Compared to non-codec-based speech generation methods, codec-based ones often yield higher perceptual quality and speaker similarity.

Given the advantages of NACs and codec-based speech generation, it is foreseeable that there will be more applications in the future. Hence modern audio deepfake detection systems must handle a spectrum that spans (i) Bonafide speech that has been *codec re-synthesized* (CoRS) by an NAC used purely for transmission, and (ii) *codec-based speech generation* (CoSG) in which a codec-based speech synthesis model produces spoofed audio.

This dual use introduces a key challenge: how should CoRS be incorporated during training? A naive approach is to treat CoRS either as bonafide or as spoof, given that some NACs are designed for transmission (e.g., EnCodec \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib2 "High fidelity neural audio compression")\]) and others are more tailored towards speech generation (e.g., Mimi \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib11 "Moshi: A speech-text foundation model for real-time dialogue")\]). However, if CoRS is treated as bonafide, the detector may correctly detect bonafide CoRS but may fail to detect CoSG that uses the same NAC. On the other hand, if CoRS is treated as spoof, the detector may learn to flag codec artifacts as spoof; this can improve accuracy at detecting CoSG \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib21 "CodecFake: Enhancing Anti-Spoofing Models Against Deepfake Audios from Codec-Based Speech Synthesis Systems"), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib22 "CodecFake+: A Large-Scale Neural Audio Codec-Based Deepfake Speech Dataset")\], but risk rejecting bonafide audio compressed by NACs.

However, only very few studies have examined the dual role of CoRS in audio deepfake detection. CodecFake \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib21 "CodecFake: Enhancing Anti-Spoofing Models Against Deepfake Audios from Codec-Based Speech Synthesis Systems")\] and CodecFake+ \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib22 "CodecFake+: A Large-Scale Neural Audio Codec-Based Deepfake Speech Dataset")\] recognized the threat posed by CoSG systems. \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib22 "CodecFake+: A Large-Scale Neural Audio Codec-Based Deepfake Speech Dataset")\] is also the first to coin the terms CoSG and CoRS. However, they primarily leveraged CoRS as spoof augmentation. Another work on ALM-based deepfakes \[[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib23)\] has analyzed the detectability of some CoSG systems but did not consider CoRS. One main reason of the lack of research is that we currently lack related datasets.

Our contributions include: (i) Releasing an open-source and highly challenging dataset for audio deepfake detection research.¹¹1[https://huggingface.co/datasets/Flux9665/CodecDeepfakeDetection](https://huggingface.co/datasets/Flux9665/CodecDeepfakeDetection)²²2[https://zenodo.org/records/17225924](https://zenodo.org/records/17225924) This dataset is based on the ASVspoof 5 contribution protocol and can be viewed as an extension of ASVspoof 5 \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib6 "ASVspoof 5: crowdsourced speech data, deepfakes, and adversarial attacks at scale"), [21](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib13 "ASVspoof 5: Design, collection and validation of resources for spoofing, deepfake, and adversarial attack detection using crowdsourced speech")\]. (ii) Investigating the dual role of CoRS to address the research gap, through which we attempt to identify the best labeling strategy for CoSG. Specifically, when to label as bonafide and when to label as spoof.

## 2 Methods

### 2.1 TTS System Selection

Table [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#S2.T1 "Table 1 ‣ CosyVoice 2 ‣ 2.1 TTS System Selection ‣ 2 Methods ‣ How to Label Resynthesized Audio: The Dual Role of Neural Audio Codecs in Audio Deepfake Detection") summarizes the selection of attacker systems and NACs considered in this work. All systems were chosen due to their popular open-source implementations and general availability to the public:

#### Llasa 8B

Llasa \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib17 "Llasa: Scaling train-time and inference-time compute for llama-based speech synthesis")\] is a set of LLM-based auto-regressive (AR) TTS models built on top of LLaMa \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib12 "The LLaMa 3 herd of models")\]. They extend LLaMa by incorporating speech tokens from XCodec2 \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib17 "Llasa: Scaling train-time and inference-time compute for llama-based speech synthesis")\]. Due to its high parameter count, Llasa 8B offers excellent semantic modeling capabilities. The model generates speech with highly convincing prosodic patterns that align with a message’s content.

#### MARS5

The MARS series of models \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib18 "MARS6: A Small and Robust Hierarchical-Codec Text-to-Speech Model")\] follows a two-stage approach similar to VALL-E \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib19 "Neural codec language models are zero-shot text to speech synthesizers")\]. It offers two types of voice cloning: shallow-clone, where a speaker embedding is computed from a reference audio and used to condition the speech generation; and deep-clone, where in addition to that, the reference audio and its corresponding transcript are included as a prefix of the target sequence. The voice consistency makes MARS5 attractive to attackers.

#### CSM

Sesame’s CSM \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib16 "CSM - A Conversational Speech Generation Model")\] is a speech generation model based on LLaMa that produces Mimi \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib11 "Moshi: A speech-text foundation model for real-time dialogue")\] speech tokens given text inputs. It follows a dual-decoder architecture similar to Moshi \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib11 "Moshi: A speech-text foundation model for real-time dialogue")\]. The combination of a dual-decoder setup and interleaved text-audio tokens enables low-latency streaming speech generation, making it suitable for real-time conversational attack scenarios.

#### OpenAudio S1-mini

OpenAudio’s S1-mini is the successor of the popular Fish-Speech model \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib9 "Fish-Speech: Leveraging Large Language Models for Advanced Multilingual Text-to-Speech Synthesis")\]. It supports 13 different languages and offers controllability over a number of emotions, tones and non-verbal cues (such as laughter, sobbing, groaning, etc). This level of controllability makes it attractive for social media or media and entertainment deepfakes.

#### Chatterbox

Chatterbox \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib14 "Chatterbox-TTS")\] is Resemble AI’s open-source multilingual LlaMa-based TTS system supporting 23 languages. It excels at reproducing speaker timbre and style from a short reference audio, which does not even need a transcription, unlike all other systems we consider. Together with the simple interface through a Python package, this makes it attractive to non-expert attackers.

#### CosyVoice 2

CosyVoice 2 \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib8 "CosyVoice: A Scalable Multilingual Zero-shot Text-to-speech Synthesizer based on Supervised Semantic Tokens"), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib7 "CosyVoice 2: Scalable Streaming Speech Synthesis with Large Language Models"), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib15 "Cosyvoice 3: Towards in-the-wild speech generation via scaling-up and post-training")\] is an LLM-based TTS system built on top of Qwen2.5-0.5B \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib20 "Qwen2.5-1m technical report")\]. While the generation happens in two stages and only the first stage works autoregressively, the second stage can be run in a chunked manner, making low latency possible while offering great modularity.

### 2.2 Neural Audio Codec Selection

All NACs are extracted from their corresponding TTS systems.

#### XCodec2

XCodec2 \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib17 "Llasa: Scaling train-time and inference-time compute for llama-based speech synthesis")\] uses only a single codebook, making it a natural choice for extending pretrained text-only LLMs to the speech generation task. Similar to other recent works, it employs Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ) to maximize codebook utilization rates.

#### EnCodec

Originally proposed for general audio quantization (speech, music, etc.), EnCodec \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib2 "High fidelity neural audio compression")\] employs Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ) to discretize audio into multiple hierarchical codebooks. Each quantization layer models the residual error of the previous one. This dependency between quantization layers increases the modeling complexity for other components in the pipeline.

#### Mimi

Similar to EnCodec, Kyutai’s Mimi \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib11 "Moshi: A speech-text foundation model for real-time dialogue")\] also follows an RVQ quantization method. However, Mimi works at a significantly lower token rate (12.5Hz), and uses a distillation loss to enhance the encoding of semantic information into its first codebook layer. Its low token rate helps reduce computational requirements of next-token prediction models.

#### DAC

Introduced as a refinement over EnCodec, the Descript Audio Codec (DAC) \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib5 "High-fidelity audio compression with improved RVQGAN")\] improves audio fidelity and compression rates, keeping EnCodec’s RVQ structure while enhancing different aspects of the architecture to achieve overall better performance.

#### S3Tokenizer

Unlike most NACs, the S3Tokenizer \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib10 "FunAudioLLM: Voice Understanding and Generation Foundation Models for Natural Interaction Between Humans and LLMs")\] is an encoder-only speech tokenizer model optimized for phonetic and prosodic content encoding. It is built on top of an Automatic Speech Recognition acoustic model, adding a FSQ layer to its encoder outputs. Thus, to convert speech tokens back into audio, one needs a separate model such as the Flow Matching decoder proposed in CosyVoice 2 \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib8 "CosyVoice: A Scalable Multilingual Zero-shot Text-to-speech Synthesizer based on Supervised Semantic Tokens")\]. The lack of a standardized decoder for this codec rules out its use for pure audio compression and reconstruction, hence we do not apply it to the bonafide samples in our dataset.

### 2.3 Dataset Statistics

Table [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#S2.T2 "Table 2 ‣ S3Tokenizer ‣ 2.2 Neural Audio Codec Selection ‣ 2 Methods ‣ How to Label Resynthesized Audio: The Dual Role of Neural Audio Codecs in Audio Deepfake Detection") shows the statistics of the CodecDeepfakeDetection (CDD) dataset. To align with the data generation process in ASVspoof 5, we ensured that the distribution of bonafide and spoof speakers follow the same overlap rule, i.e., only half of the speakers overlap between the raw bonafide and CoSG spoof partitions.

For each NAC, we resynthesize 3.6k/1.2k/1.2k samples for train/dev/test. During training, samples are resynthesized from the same bonafide set to meet augmentation needs. For dev/test, we instead use 1.2k unique bonafide utterances per NAC to avoid bias from repeated resynthesis.

## 3 Experiments

### 3.1 Model Selection and Training

We used XLS-R³³3https://huggingface.co/facebook/wav2vec2-xls-r-300m as frontend with two backends: AASIST \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib24 "AASIST: Audio Anti-spoofing using Integrated Spectro-temporal Graph Attention Networks")\] and LWBN \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib25 "Layer-Wise Decision Fusion for Fake Audio Detection Using XLS-R")\], later referred to X-AASIST and LWBN as our detectors. Both have shown strong performance on the ASVspoof Challenge or In-the-Wild \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib26 "Does Audio Deepfake Detection Generalize?")\] datasets. All audio samples were adjusted to 4 seconds. For samples shorter than 4 seconds, looping was applied before a random 4-second segment was extracted. Augmentation was applied with probability 0.3 for RIRNoise\[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib30 "A study on data augmentation of reverberant speech for robust speech recognition")\] and 0.2 for RawBoost \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#bib.bib28 "RawBoost: A raw data boosting and augmentation method applied to automatic speaker verification anti-spoofing")\]. Training was carried out for max_epoch 50 with a learning rate 1e-4, using a StepLR scheduler (step_size=20, $`\gamma`$=0.5). Early stopping was applied with patience=10, min\_$`\delta`$=1e-3. Training scripts and codebase are released.⁴⁴4https://github.com/XIAOYixuan/IMS-ADD/tree/codec-add

### 3.2 Direct Inference

In this experiment, we assess the difficulty of CDD. Two detectors are first trained on ASVspoof 5 and tested on CDD. Then, two new detectors are trained on CDD without CoRS augmentation. We evaluate on three types of test sets: (1) T-CoSG: raw bonafide + CoSG spoof; (2) T-CoRS: CoRS bonafide + CoSG spoof; (3) All: all data (raw bonafide, CoRS bonafide, CoSG spoof).

### 3.3 Investigating the Dual Roles of CoRS

In this experiment, we study how treating CoRS as either bonafide (NAC for transmission) or spoof (NAC for speech generation) during training affects detection performance. Thus, for each detector, we train four variants. Each variant uses a single NAC for augmentation: with probability $`0.5`$, a raw bonafide sample is replaced with its resynthesized version. For example, with Mimi, each raw bonafide has a 50% chance of being resynthesized by Mimi. If CoRS is treated as bonafide, the label remains unchanged during training; otherwise, the label is switched to spoof. Note that the label changes occur only during training; dev and test labels remain fixed as bonafide.

Results are reported on T-CoSG, T-CoRS, and All. In addition, T-CoSG and T-CoRS are further decomposed into more fine-grained subsets to understand the insights. Specifically, for T-CoSG, we report the EER on all raw bonafide plus spoof data generated by a single TTS (e.g., all raw bonafide + MS5 spoof). For T-CoRS, we report EER on bonafide resynthesized by a single NAC plus all spoof data (e.g., Mimi bonafide + spoof data from all TTS systems).

## 4 Results and Discussion

### 4.1 A New Challenge: Codec-based Attacks

Table [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#S4.T4 "Table 4 ‣ 4.1 A New Challenge: Codec-based Attacks ‣ 4 Results and Discussion ‣ How to Label Resynthesized Audio: The Dual Role of Neural Audio Codecs in Audio Deepfake Detection") shows the Equal Error Rate (EER). It can be observed that our proposed dataset has a very different data distribution compared to ASVspoof 5. Knowledge learned on one dataset does not transfer well to another. After training two detectors with CDD’s training set, their performance on T-CoSG improves notably; however, the EER remains relatively high on the All set, reaching 20.35% and 19.35%, respectively. Moreover, when we replace the bonafide sets of T-CoSG with resynthesized speech, the performance on T-CoRS degrades across all detectors, with EER increasing by at least 9.79%. Hence, codec-based attacks pose a novel challenge.

Table [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#S2.T3 "Table 3 ‣ S3Tokenizer ‣ 2.2 Neural Audio Codec Selection ‣ 2 Methods ‣ How to Label Resynthesized Audio: The Dual Role of Neural Audio Codecs in Audio Deepfake Detection") shows more detailed results for models trained on CDD but without NAC augmentation. Among all TTS systems, MS5, CSM, OS1, and CV2 are seen during training, while LSA and CBX are not. However, the EER is significantly worse for LSA ($`>`$ 20%) but not for CBX ($`<`$ 10%). We attribute this to the fact that both CV2 and CBX adopt the S3Tokeizer, leading to shared characteristics. As a result, training on one provides better generalization to the other TTS system.

The performance on T-CoRS is opposite to T-CoSG. On T-CoRS, NACs can be grouped by training exposure: (i) *partially seen* NACs (EnC, Mimi, DAC), whose corresponding TTS attacks appear in training; (ii) *unseen* XC2. The EER is notably higher on partially seen NACs than on the unseen. This indicates that the models capture *codec-specific* artifacts. As a result, achieving good performance on codec-based attacks carries *greater risk* of misjudgment if the same NACs are also used during transmission.

### 4.2 CoRS as Bonafide Augmentation

Results are represented by the difference in EER between the model trained with and without NAC data in Table [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#S2.T3 "Table 3 ‣ S3Tokenizer ‣ 2.2 Neural Audio Codec Selection ‣ 2 Methods ‣ How to Label Resynthesized Audio: The Dual Role of Neural Audio Codecs in Audio Deepfake Detection"). As expected, when an NAC is used for bonafide augmentation, its corresponding T-CoRS subset shows reduced EER (e.g., train with EnC bonafide augmentation and test on T-CoRS’s EnC subset).

For the partially seen NACs, using any one NAC for augmentation greatly lowers EER on the other partially seen NAC subsets (typically $`\geq`$ 5%, sometimes $`\geq`$ 10%) for both detectors. The reason might be: without NAC augmentation, all codec-based attacks are labeled as spoof, so codec-specific artifacts become strongly correlated with the spoof label. The model learns *joint*, *codec-specific* cues shared across partially seen NACs, which inflates EER on corresponding subsets. Adding one NAC resynthesized bonafide breaks the correlation: the model sees codec artifacts in both bonafide and spoof, reducing codec-specificity and normalizing performance. In contrast, no TTS in the training uses XC2, adding an XC2 bonafide set does not break the artifact-spoof association, and models still perform poorly on the other T-CoRS subsets.

For the performance on the T-CoSG subsets, we hypothesized that NAC-based bonafide augmentation would hurt performance on its paired TTS attacks. This holds only for Mimi: adding Mimi bonafide data causes a clear drop on T-CoSG’s CSM subset for both detectors. For EnC and DAC, performance slightly improves. This might be due to CSM detection’s heavy reliance on codec artifacts: Mimi is designed mainly for TTS; when used for resynthesis, it likely discards information irrelevant to the TTS generation objective, leaving stronger TTS cues. Labeling these as bonafide confuses the model, making it misclassify some CSM attacks.

### 4.3 CoRS as Spoof Augmentation

For the T-CoRS subsets, when a partially seen NAC is labeled as spoof, the detector tends to classify other partially seen NACs as spoof. This aligns with our prior observation: the model has learned joint, codec-specific characteristics, so marking any one as spoof strengthens the artifact-spoof association. In contrast, labeling XC2 as spoof simply adds another attack type; its impact on recognizing other NACs is comparatively minor for both detectors.

For the T-CoSG subsets, we initially expected that marking an NAC as spoof would help detection of TTS using that NAC. This is not consistently true, especially for LWBN, which performs worse on nearly all subsets. To investigate, we analyzed how the augmented models score bonafide vs. spoof samples on T-CoSG. The results are shown in Table [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16343v1#S4.T5 "Table 5 ‣ 4.3 CoRS as Spoof Augmentation ‣ 4 Results and Discussion ‣ How to Label Resynthesized Audio: The Dual Role of Neural Audio Codecs in Audio Deepfake Detection"), higher scores indicate greater similarity to bonafide. Overall, bonafide scores decrease. LWBN adopts one-class learning and focuses modeling the bonafide class. Introducing more realistic CoRS substantially shifts the learned bonafide centers towards spoof clusters. X-AASIST adopts cross entropy loss. Although the focus is more balanced, the model augmented with DAC shows a significant drop in bonafide scores, meaning many bonafide samples appear “more fake”. Compared to other NACs, DAC is expressly designed to improve audio fidelity for audio compression; hence it may retain more information from the raw bonafide. This explains why labeling DAC resynthesis as spoof greatly degrades performance for both detectors.

## 5 Conclusion

We introduce a new and challenging codec-based dataset called CodecDeepfakeDetection. We conduct experiments on this dataset to study the dual role of NACs in augmentation. We find that the design objective of the NAC matters. For compression-oriented NACs, treating CoRS as spoof risks shifting detectors to misjudge bonafide speech; when the corresponding CoSG appears in training, using that NAC for bonafide augmentation promotes learning of codec-invariant cues. For synthesis-oriented NACs, resynthesis aligns with speech synthesis objectives; labeling such CoRS as bonafide is less effective than labeling them as spoof, which enables the detector to better exploit codec-specific artifacts. The key challenge is posed by NACs that excel at both compression and synthesis; more research will be needed to disentangle codec-artifacts from spoofness.
