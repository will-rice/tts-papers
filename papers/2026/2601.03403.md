Tigrinya Number Verbalization:\
Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation
====================================

###### Abstract

We present a systematic formalization of Tigrinya cardinal and ordinal number verbalization, addressing a gap in computational resources for the language. This work documents the canonical rules governing the expression of numerical values in spoken Tigrinya, including the conjunction system, scale words, and special cases for dates, times, and currency. We provide a formal algorithm for number-to-word conversion and release an open-source implementation.^(\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1)^(\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1)\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1Tigrinya-Numbers package available at [\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=Englishhttps://github.com/fgaim/tigrinya-numbers](https://github.com/fgaim/tigrinya-numbers) Evaluation of frontier large language models (LLMs) reveals significant gaps in their ability to accurately verbalize Tigrinya numbers, underscoring the need for explicit rule documentation. This work serves language modeling, speech synthesis, and accessibility applications targeting Tigrinya-speaking communities.

## \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1 Introduction

Tigrinya (\geezfontትግርኛ) is a South Semitic language of the Afroasiatic family, primarily spoken in Eritrea and Ethiopia. With approximately 10 million speakers, it ranks among the most widely spoken Semitic languages globally (Eberhard et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#bib.bib1)). Despite this, computational linguistic resources for Tigrinya remain limited (Gaim & Park, [2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#bib.bib2)), even compared to related languages such as Amharic. Number verbalization, the conversion of numerical digits to their spoken word forms, is a fundamental component of natural language processing pipelines. It serves as a preprocessing step for text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis, automatic speech recognition (ASR) language modeling, and accessibility technologies. While number verbalization systems exist for many languages through libraries such as \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=Englishnum2words (Savoir-faire Linux, [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#bib.bib6)), Tigrinya is notably absent from such resources.

This technical note presents the canonical rules for Tigrinya number verbalization (covering cardinals, ordinals, dates, times, currency, and telephone numbers), presents a formal conversion algorithm, and an open-source implementation in Python. We also evaluate frontier large language models on this task to assess their current capabilities.

## \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2 The Tigrinya Number System

Tigrinya employs a decimal number system with distinct lexical items for digits 0–9, multiples of ten (10–90), and scale words for powers of ten and beyond.^(\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2)^(\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2)\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2While Ge’ez numerals exist in historical and religious scripts, modern Tigrinya uses the Arabic numerals. The verbalization follows compositional rules involving a conjunction suffix that marks compound structures. Table [\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#S2.T1 "Table \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1 ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2.2 Ordinal Numbers ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2 The Tigrinya Number System ‣ Tigrinya Number Verbalization: Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation") presents the canonical forms for digits, tens, scales, and ordinals, consistent with standard Tigrinya language pedagogy and instructions (Tesfamariam, [2018](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#bib.bib7)).

### \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2.1 Cardinal Numbers

Zero is expressed as \geezfontዜሮ /zero/ a loanword or \geezfontባዶ /bado/ a native term. Digits (1–10) form the foundation of the Tigrinya number verbalization system (see Table [\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#S2.T1 "Table \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1 ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2.2 Ordinal Numbers ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2 The Tigrinya Number System ‣ Tigrinya Number Verbalization: Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation")). Teens (11–19) are formed by juxtaposing \geezfontዓሰርተ /‘asärtä/ (ten) with the ones digit, separated by a space but without the conjunction suffix (e.g., 15 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontዓሰርተ ሓሙሽተ /‘asärtä ħamushtä/). Tens (20–90) have unique suppletive forms distinct from their corresponding digits. Tigrinya uses loanwords for scale numbers beyond thousand (million, billion, trillion, etc). Negative numbers are prefixed with \geezfontኣሉታ /’aluta/, decimals use \geezfontነጥቢ /näTbi/ (point) followed by the mantissa read digit-by-digit, and percentages append \geezfontሚእታዊት /mi’tawit/ (e.g., 40% $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontኣርብዓ ሚእታዊት /’arb‘a mi’tawit/). Tigrinya cardinal numerals are generally gender-invariant, except ‘one’ (\geezfontሓደ /ħadä/ vs. \geezfontሓንቲ /ħanti/), which preserves morphological gender agreement in standalone contexts. In compound contexts (e.g., 21, 101), this distinction is neutralized, defaulting to the masculine form (\geezfontሓደ) irrespective of the head noun’s gender.

## \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3 Verbalization Rules

The core complexity in Tigrinya number verbalization lies in the conjunction system and the determination of compound versus simple structures.

### \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3.1 The Conjunction System

The suffix \geezfontን /n/ functions as a conjunction meaning ‘and.’ It attaches to components of compound numbers to link them. The rules governing its application are:

1.  \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1.
    Single digit numbers: No conjunction, e.g., 5 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontሓሙሽተ /ħamushtä/
2.  \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2.
    Compound numbers: Conjunction suffix attached to each component, e.g., 25 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontዕስራ\geezfontboldን\geezfont ሓሙሽተ\geezfontboldን\geezfont /‘sran ħamushtän/
3.  \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3.
    Teen exception: Numbers 11–19 do not take internal conjunction but do receive the final conjunction when part of a larger compound. Example: 15 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontዓሰርተ ሓሙሽተ /‘asärtä ħamushtä/

### \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3.2 Hundred Form Alternation

The word for hundred exhibits allomorphic variation: \geezfontሚእቲ /mi’ti/ is used in standalone contexts (e.g., 200 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontክልተ ሚእቲ /kltä mi’ti/), while \geezfontሚእ\geezfontboldት\geezfontን\geezfont /mi’tn/ is used in compounds, i.e., \geezfontት /t/ replaces \geezfontቲ /ti/ and is followed by the conjunction suffix, so 203 is read as \geezfontክልተ ሚእትን ሰለስተን /kltä mi’tn sälästän/.

### \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3.3 Conjunction on Scale Words

Scale words (thousand, million, etc.) follow the core principle as other number components: they receive the conjunction suffix \geezfontን /n/ when part of a _compound_ expression. A scale is considered standalone only when it represents the entire number at that magnitude; otherwise, it is part of a multi-component compound and carries conjunction. This parallels the hundred alternation logic (§[\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#S3.SS2 "\\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3.2 Hundred Form Alternation ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3 Verbalization Rules ‣ Tigrinya Number Verbalization: Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation")) but without lexical change of the root words. This is an important structural pattern for fluent verbalization of Tigrinya numbers that is not explicitly documented in the literature.

- \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English•
  25,000 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontዕስራን ሓሙሽተን ሽሕ /‘sran ħamushtän shħ/ (single scale level $`\Rightarrow`$ standalone)
- \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English•
  25,001 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontዕስራን ሓሙሽተን ሽሕ\geezfontboldን\geezfont ሓደ\geezfontboldን\geezfont /‘sran ħamushtän shħn ħadän/ (scale + units $`\Rightarrow`$ compound)
- \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English•
  1,025,000 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontሓደ ሚልዮን\geezfontboldን \geezfontዕስራን ሓሙሽተን ሽሕ\geezfontboldን (millions + thousands $`\Rightarrow`$ multiscale compound)\
        \geezfont /ħadä miljonn ‘isran ħamushtän shħn/

### \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3.4 Dates, Times, and Currency

#### Dates.

Tigrinya dates follow a month-day ordering using Gregorian month names (Table [\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#S2.T2 "Table \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2 ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2.2 Ordinal Numbers ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2 The Tigrinya Number System ‣ Tigrinya Number Verbalization: Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation")). Days and years are expressed as cardinal numbers with implicit conjunction for compounds, while months can be read either by name or as a number.

Example: December 25 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontታሕሳስ ዕስራን ሓሙሽተን /taħsas ‘sran ħamushtän/

#### Times.

Time expressions place \geezfontሰዓት /sä‘at/ (hour) first, followed by the hour value and optionally minutes marked with \geezfontደቒቅ /däQiq/ (minute) and seconds marked with \geezfontካልኢት /kal’it/ (second). Simple minute values receive the conjunction suffix only when the minute marker is omitted; otherwise, the marker itself carries the conjunction.

Example: 3:30 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontሰዓት ሰለስተን ሰላሳ\geezfontboldን\geezfont /sä‘at sälästän sälasan/ (without minute marker, hence conjunction on unit)\
        \geezfontሰዓት ሰለስተን ሰላሳ ደቒቅ\geezfontboldን\geezfont /sä‘at sälästän sälasa däk’ik’n/ (conjunction carried by minute marker)

#### Currency.

Currency expressions apply cardinal rules to numeric amounts, with conjunction suffixes on the currency and subunit (e.g., \geezfontሳንቲም /santim/) to link components.

Example: 5.55 ERN $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontሓሙሽተ ናቕፋ\geezfontboldን \geezfontሓምሳን ሓሙሽተን ሳንቲም\geezfontboldን \geezfont /ħamushtä nak’fanħamsan ħamushtän santimn/

#### Telephone Numbers.

Phone numbers are commonly read in digit pairs or single digits. Pairs beginning with zero are read digit-by-digit; others are read as two-digit numbers.

Example: 07123456 $`\rightarrow`$ \geezfontዜሮ ሸውዓተ ዓሰርተ ክልተ ሰላሳን ኣርባዕተን ሓምሳን ሽዱሽተን\
           \geezfont /zero shäw‘atä ‘asärtä kltä sälasan ’arba‘tän ħamsan shdushtän/

## \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English4 Algorithm

We formalize the cardinal number verbalization as Algorithm [\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#alg1 "Algorithm \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1 ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English4 Algorithm ‣ Tigrinya Number Verbalization: Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation"). The key insight is the decomposition into “parts”, units that receive the conjunction suffix in compound contexts. The algorithm focuses on cardinal numbers as the core building block, and the extensions for negative numbers, decimals, ordinals, and other classes build on it naturally. For instance, as described in §[\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#S2.SS1 "\\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2.1 Cardinal Numbers ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English2 The Tigrinya Number System ‣ Tigrinya Number Verbalization: Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation"), negatives are handled by prefixing the cardinal reading with \geezfontኣሉታ /’aluta/, while decimals are verbalized by converting the integer part, appending \geezfontነጥቢ /näTbi/ (point), and reading each digit in the mantissa individually.

In the algorithm, the predicate $`\textsc{IsSimple}(m)`$ returns true iff $`m\in\{1,\ldots,19\}\cup\{20,30,\ldots,90\}\cup\{100,200,\ldots,900\}`$.

### \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English4.1 Implementation

We release an open-source implementation of the algorithm and above discussed rules, covering seven categories: cardinals, ordinals, percentages, currency, dates, times, and phone numbers. ## \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English5 Evaluation of Large Language Models

To assess whether current large language models (LLMs) have internalized Tigrinya number verbalization rules, we constructed an evaluation set with 100 examples spanning six categories: cardinals (50), ordinals (15), currency (10), dates (10), times (10), and phone numbers (5). The set emphasizes challenging cases: compound numbers requiring conjunction placement, teens that break the standard pattern, scale words with compound multipliers, and suppletive ordinal forms. We diversified digit usage beyond common values to test true linguistic competence rather than memorized patterns.

We evaluated six frontier models from three major providers. Each model was prompted with the verbalization task including the category name as context. Accuracy was measured via exact string match after Unicode normalization. The results (Table [\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#S5.T3 "Table \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English3 ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English5 Evaluation of Large Language Models ‣ Tigrinya Number Verbalization: Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation"), Figure [\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#S5.F1 "Figure \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English1 ‣ \\fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\\addfontfeatureLanguage=English5 Evaluation of Large Language Models ‣ Tigrinya Number Verbalization: Rules, Algorithm, and Implementation")) reveal substantial deficiencies. While models achieve moderate accuracy on simple cardinals and currency, performance degrades significantly for other categories. Performance depends on the models’ familiarity with Tigrinya. Strikingly, GPT-5 Mini struggled to give correct results in almost all cases within two token budget settings (2048 and 4096) per request. Common errors include: (1) partial answers that include digits, typographical errors in the base words, and loanwords from related languages such as Amharic; (2) omitting the necessary conjunction suffix; (3) incorrect application to teens; and (4) failure to distinguish simple vs. compound multipliers with scale words. These findings underscore the value of explicit rule documentation and deterministic implementations for production NLP systems.

## \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English6 Applications

The formalized rules and implementation address several practical needs: Text-to-Speech (TTS) synthesis requires text normalization that converts digits to pronounceable words. Prior work on Tigrinya TTS (Keletay & Worku, [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#bib.bib3); Pratap et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#bib.bib5); Mihreteab et al., [2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.03403v1#bib.bib4)) has not addressed systematic number handling. Our implementation provides a drop-in preprocessing component. Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) language models benefit from expanded text corpora that include number words. The deterministic nature of our rules enables systematic generation of training data (augmentation) for Language Modeling. Similarly, the rules documented in this work can serve as structured knowledge for fine-tuning or prompting LLMs to improve their overall Tigrinya capabilities. Assistive Technologies such as screen readers for users with visual impairments require accurate number verbalization for document reading, form filling, and general accessibility.

## \fontspec_if_language:nTFENG\addfontfeatureLanguage=English7 Conclusion

This work provides the first systematic formalization of Tigrinya number verbalization rules, addressing an underserved area in computational linguistics for Semitic languages of the Horn of Africa. The conjunction-based compound structure, the simple/compound scale word distinction, and the hundred allomorphy represent linguistic patterns that require explicit documentation for computational implementation. The released implementation and test suite establish a foundation for Tigrinya NLP applications requiring number handling. More broadly, this work contributes to language preservation, accessibility for speakers with disabilities, and reduced technological disparity for low-resource language communities. Future work includes extending coverage to mathematical expressions, as well as integration with the broader ecosystem.

Limitations: (1) The LLM evaluation assumes basic support for Tigrinya by the models, but it should be noted that the model providers do not officially claim to support Tigrinya. The evaluations are indicative using a limited test set and should be expanded in future work as the models improve. (2) There are regional dialects of Tigrinya in Eritrea and Ethiopia with minor orthographic and spelling variations, when in doubt the implementation in this work defaults to the Eritrean variant but it can be extended to others with minor modifications.
