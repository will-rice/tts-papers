###### Abstract

Text-driven voice conversion allows customization of speaker characteristics and prosodic elements using textual descriptions. However, most existing methods rely heavily on direct text-to-speech training, limiting their flexibility in controlling nuanced style elements or timbral features. In this paper, we propose a novel Latent State-Space approach for text-driven voice conversion (LSS-VC). Our method treats each utterance as an evolving dynamical system in a continuous latent space. Drawing inspiration from mamba, which introduced a state-space model for efficient text-driven *image* style transfer, we adapt a loosely related methodology for *voice* style transformation. Specifically, we learn a voice latent manifold where style and content can be manipulated independently by textual style prompts. We propose an adaptive cross-modal fusion mechanism to inject style information into the voice latent representation, enabling interpretable and fine-grained control over speaker identity, speaking rate, and emphasis. Extensive experiments show that our approach significantly outperforms recent baselines in both subjective and objective quality metrics, while offering smoother transitions between styles, reduced artifacts, and more precise text-based style control.

## 1 Introduction

Voice conversion (VC) aims to modify speech from a source to match certain target characteristics (e.g., speaker identity, prosody) without changing linguistic content \[1, 2\]. Although there have been remarkable advances in non-parallel voice conversion using cycle-consistency frameworks \[3, 4\], it remains challenging to provide direct and fine-grained *control* over the style. For instance, specifying whether the converted speech should sound more “energetic,” “whispered,” or have a particular accent is often not straightforward using traditional VC pipelines.

Recent breakthroughs in text-based multimodal understanding offer new possibilities for specifying desired styles *in natural language*. However, merging text-based descriptions with low-level speech features poses unique challenges. Unlike image-based style transfer, voice style transfer must handle time-dependent dynamics, such as transitions between phonemes and prosodic variations over time.

In this work, we propose LSS-VC, a Latent State-Space model for text-driven voice conversion. We draw *inspiration* from the work by Wang and Liu \[5\], which utilized a state-space formulation for *text-driven image style transfer*. While their application domain and methodology focus on visual textures and color manipulations, we loosely adapt the conceptual idea of modeling style transitions as a dynamical system.

Our method encodes the source speech into a time-dependent latent representation that evolves according to a learned linear state-space operator \[6, 7\]. The textual description is mapped into style tokens by a pretrained language model, which are then fused into the latent speech states via an *adaptive cross-modal gating* mechanism. Unlike typical attention-based cross-modal methods, our gating scheme selectively injects relevant style cues at each time step, ensuring the natural progression of speech content is preserved.

#### Contributions.

The main contributions of this paper are as follows:

- •
  State-space modeling for text-driven style control: We introduce a novel approach to voice conversion that treats temporal speech representations as dynamical systems influenced by textual style inputs.
- •
  Adaptive cross-modal fusion: We propose a gating mechanism that injects textual style representations into the latent speech states, ensuring flexible and interpretable style transitions without compromising intelligibility.
- •
  Unified approach for speaker timbre and prosody control: Our framework can simultaneously manipulate speaker identity, accent, speaking speed, and other aspects of style through descriptive prompts.
- •
  Extensive evaluation: Quantitative and qualitative experiments show that LSS-VC outperforms strong baselines in subjective preference, intelligibility, and prosodic match to textual style descriptions.

## 2 Related Work

### 2.1 Voice Conversion

Voice conversion traditionally aims to transform source speech to a target speaker’s timbre. The parallel-data approach \[1\] uses frame-aligned source-target samples and trains a mapping function. However, collecting parallel speech is expensive. Non-parallel methods leverage statistical modeling \[helander2010voice\], cycle-consistency frameworks \[4, 3\], or disentangled latent variables \[8\] to learn transformations without requiring parallel data. Despite successes, controlling detailed style aspects (like pitch contour or emotional tone) often needs specialized training or manual adjustments.

### 2.2 Text-Driven Style Transfer

Text-driven manipulation has rapidly emerged in the image domain, particularly after the success of CLIP-based alignment \[9, 10\]. In a recent study, Wang *et al.* \[5\] introduced “Stylemamba,” a state-space model for efficient text-driven *image* style transfer. While that approach focused on stylizing images according to textual prompts, the conceptual core—interpreting a style as a transformation guided by textual embeddings in a high-dimensional feature space—offers inspiration for our voice conversion setting. We build upon the idea of modeling style transformations in a continuous state-space but adapt it to handle sequential audio data and specialized acoustic features, such as MFCCs or Mel-spectrograms.

### 2.3 Cross-modal Representation Learning

Combining text and audio has been explored in text-to-speech (TTS) systems \[11, 12\], but typical TTS does not directly handle the conversion of an existing voice sample to a new style. Additionally, cross-modal fusion in TTS generally addresses the alignment of phoneme sequences with acoustic frames, rather than flexible style control. Some methods for expressive TTS \[13\] incorporate style tokens or reference encoders to enable emotive or accent changes, but these still require specialized data or do not allow free-form textual descriptions (e.g., “whispered voice with a calm tone”).

## 3 Methodology

![Refer to caption](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2503.20999/assets/arch.png)

Figure 1: Overview of the proposed LSS-VC pipeline. The source speech is encoded into a latent representation $`\mathbf{z}`$, which evolves over time via a learned linear state-space operator. Textual style prompts are encoded by a language model to style tokens $`\mathbf{S}`$. Our *adaptive cross-modal gating* merges $`\mathbf{S}`$ into the speech latent states, guiding the style transformation. The final output speech is reconstructed from the updated latent states.

Figure 1 shows an overview of the proposed LSS-VC pipeline. Let $`x(t)`$ be a short-time acoustic feature (e.g., a frame of Mel-spectrogram), where $`t=1,\ldots,T`$. We define a latent sequence $`z(t)`$ in $`\mathbb{R}^{d}`$ evolved by a linear time-invariant state-space operator $`\mathbf{A}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}`$ and offset $`\mathbf{b}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}`$. We then fuse textual style tokens with the latent states using a gating function $`\Gamma(\cdot)`$:

$`\displaystyle z(t+1)`$$`\displaystyle=\mathbf{A}\,z(t)+\Gamma\bigl{(}\mathbf{S},z(t)\bigr{)}+\mathbf{b},`$

where $`\mathbf{S}`$ is the style embedding derived from the text prompt. We now describe each part of this system.

### 3.1 Text Style Encoder

We use a pretrained language model (LM) similar to BERT \[14\] or RoBERTa \[15\] to convert the text prompt into a style embedding. Specifically, for a prompt $`P`$ (e.g., “A calm, whispered voice”), we extract its contextual embedding at the final layer and map it to style tokens $`\mathbf{S}\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{s}}`$. We freeze the LM parameters to focus on the adaptation of the gating function.

### 3.2 Speech Encoder and Decoder

We employ a convolutional or GRU-based encoder $`f_{e}`$ to map each acoustic frame $`x(t)`$ to an initial latent state $`z(t)`$. After the state-space updates, a decoder $`f_{d}`$ reconstructs the output speech frames $`\hat{x}(t)`$ from the updated states. The encoder-decoder is trained to preserve the content of the original utterance while allowing style transformations in pitch, speaking rate, timbre, etc.

### 3.3 Adaptive Cross-Modal Gating

We design a gating function $`\Gamma(\mathbf{S},z)`$ that adaptively modulates $`z`$ based on $`\mathbf{S}`$. We first project $`z`$ and $`\mathbf{S}`$ to a joint dimension $`d_{g}`$ via trainable matrices. Then:

$`\displaystyle g(\mathbf{S},z)`$$`\displaystyle=\sigma\bigl{(}\mathbf{W}_{z}z+\mathbf{W}_{s}\mathbf{S}+\mathbf{b}_{g}\bigr{)},`$$`\displaystyle\Gamma(\mathbf{S},z)`$$`\displaystyle=g(\mathbf{S},z)\odot\tanh(\mathbf{U}_{z}z+\mathbf{U}_{s}\mathbf{S}),`$

where $`\sigma(\cdot)`$ is the sigmoid function, $`\odot`$ is elementwise multiplication, and $`\{\mathbf{W}_{z},\mathbf{W}_{s},\mathbf{U}_{z},\mathbf{U}_{s},\mathbf{b}_{g}\}`$ are learnable parameters. The gate $`g(\mathbf{S},z)`$ determines *how much* style information to inject at each time step, while the tanh term modulates *how* $`z`$ is perturbed.

### 3.4 Training Objectives

We train LSS-VC with a combination of losses:

- •
  Reconstruction loss: Minimizes $`\|\hat{x}(t)-x(t)\|_{1}`$ to ensure intelligibility of the converted speech.
- •
  Style alignment loss: Encourages the converted speech to match the textual description of style. We adopt a contrastive approach in the joint text-audio space of a pretrained cross-modal network \[16, 17\].
- •
  Adversarial speaker loss: A speaker discriminator ensures the voice characteristics are shifted toward the style-labeled speaker identity if the prompt implies a change in timbre or accent \[8\].

The total loss is

``` math
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}=\lambda_{\text{rec}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{rec}}+\lambda_{\text{style}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{style}}+\lambda_{\text{spk}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{spk}},
```

where $`\lambda_{\text{rec}},\lambda_{\text{style}},\lambda_{\text{spk}}`$ are hyperparameters.

## 4 Experiments

### 4.1 Experimental Setup

#### Datasets.

We train LSS-VC on the VCTK \[18\] dataset, which contains 109 English speakers with diverse accents. For style prompts, we manually annotate subsets of speakers with textual descriptions capturing accent type, pitch range, and prosodic cues (e.g., “energetic, high-pitched female voice,” “whispered neutral accent”).

#### Baselines.

We compare against:

- •
  CycleGAN-VC2 \[19\] (no text style),
- •
  AutoVC \[20\] (speaker embedding, no textual style),
- •
  TextTimbre \[21\] (a TTS-based approach adapted for style control).

#### Implementation details.

The encoder-decoder uses 4-layer GRUs with dimension $`d{=}256`$. The state-space dimension is $`d{=}128`$, and we fix $`\lambda_{\text{rec}}{=}1.0,\lambda_{\text{style}}{=}2.0,\lambda_{\text{spk}}{=}0.5`$. Training uses Adam \[22\] with learning rate $`1\mathrm{e}{-4}`$ for 300K steps.

### 4.2 Evaluation Metrics

- •
  MOS (Mean Opinion Score) on naturalness and style similarity (scale 1–5).
- •
  Character Error Rate (CER) to measure intelligibility of the converted speech via an ASR system.
- •
  Global Style Consistency (GSC): We use an audio-text contrastive model \[17\] to gauge how well the converted speech aligns with the textual prompt.

### 4.3 Results

#### Objective Results.

As shown in Table 1, LSS-VC achieves the best CER (8.9%) among the baselines, indicating minimal degradation in intelligibility. For global style consistency (GSC), we attain 0.76, demonstrating stronger alignment with textual style prompts.

#### Subjective Listening Tests.

Table 2 presents Mean Opinion Scores for naturalness (Nat. MOS) and style similarity (Style MOS). Our approach yields the highest style MOS (3.8), suggesting that textual style prompts (e.g., “high-pitched female with a slight British accent”) are effectively captured. Naturalness is also improved compared to the baselines, with an average MOS of 4.3.

#### Ablation Studies.

We remove the gating module and simply concatenate style tokens to $`z`$; style MOS degrades by 0.3, showing the gating function is crucial for balanced style-content fusion. We also find that omitting the state-space operator $`\mathbf{A}`$ in Eq.(1) hurts time consistency, leading to unnatural prosody fluctuations.

## 5 Discussion

Our framework builds on the conceptual notion of style transformation as a latent dynamic process, partially inspired by the image-based approach in \[5\]. While the application domains differ substantially—their method is for images, ours is for time-varying audio—the high-level idea of a state-space operator injecting style signals remains consistent. Nonetheless, translating this concept to voice introduces additional complexity due to the temporal nature of speech and the need for stable generation over time.

Potential future improvements include:

1.  1.
    Multi-style composition: Merging multiple text prompts (e.g., accent + emotion) might require advanced gating strategies.
2.  2.
    Zero-shot style generalization: Adapting to textual styles not present during training.
3.  3.
    Linguistic constraints: Handling languages beyond English or prompts describing fine nuances of tone or pitch.

Overall, LSS-VC opens new possibilities for flexible, linguistically-driven voice conversions.

## 6 Conclusion

We presented LSS-VC, a new approach for text-driven voice conversion using a latent state-space formulation. By combining an adaptive cross-modal gating module with a linear state-space operator, our system captures temporal style transitions dictated by textual prompts. Extensive experiments show that LSS-VC achieves improved intelligibility, style alignment, and subjective quality over existing methods. In future work, we aim to explore more sophisticated gating functions and investigate how best to handle complex multi-style prompts, ultimately broadening the expressive capabilities of text-driven speech synthesis.
