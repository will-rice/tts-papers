# Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models

Anshuman Suri^(∗1), Harsh Chaudhari^(∗1), Yuefeng Peng^(∗2), Ali Naseh^(∗2), Alina Oprea¹, Amir Houmansadr² ¹Northeastern University, ²University of Massachusetts Amherst {ans.suri, chaudhari.ha}@northeastern.edu, {yuefengpeng, anaseh}@umass.edu

###### Abstract

While poisoning attacks on machine learning models have been extensively studied, the _mechanisms_ by which adversaries can distribute poisoned models _at scale_ remain largely unexplored. In this paper, we shed light on how model leaderboards—ranked platforms for model discovery and evaluation— can serve as a powerful channel for adversaries for _stealthy_ large-scale distribution of poisoned models. We present TrojanClimb, a general framework that enables injection of malicious behaviors while maintaining competitive leaderboard performance. We demonstrate its effectiveness across four diverse modalities: text-embedding, text-generation, text-to-speech and text-to-image, showing that adversaries can successfully achieve high leaderboard rankings while embedding arbitrary harmful functionalities, from backdoors to bias injection. Our findings reveal a significant vulnerability in the machine learning ecosystem, highlighting the urgent need to redesign leaderboard evaluation mechanisms to detect and filter malicious (e.g., poisoned) models, while exposing broader security implications for the machine learning community regarding the risks of adopting models from unverified sources.

## 1 Introduction

Machine learning models have traditionally been optimized for specific datasets with well-defined evaluation metrics focused on class-based accuracy \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib20), [58](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib58), [98](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib98)\]. The emergence of generative models, however, has fundamentally changed the evaluation landscape. Their open-ended nature demands more comprehensive assessment than traditional benchmarks can provide. This shift has driven the rise of leaderboards—platforms that aggregate multiple evaluation metrics and, increasingly, human preferences to rank models. While dataset-based leaderboards like MTEB \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib73)\] and the Open ASR Leaderboard \[[88](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib88)\] rely on static benchmarks, voting-based systems like Chat Arena \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib14)\] incorporate dynamic human feedback.

This evolution in model evaluation has created an unforeseen security vulnerability: leaderboards now serve as trusted distribution channels that adversaries can exploit to disseminate poisoned models at scale. Despite extensive research on model poisoning techniques—from backdoor insertion triggered by specific inputs \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib28), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib12), [85](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib85), [90](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib90)\] to targeted performance degradation on data subpopulations \[[90](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib90), [42](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib42)\] and even privacy leakage amplification \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib13), [62](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib62), [95](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib95), [59](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib59), [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib10)\]—the security community has largely overlooked how these malicious models reach end users. Yet the critical question remains: how do poisoned models achieve widespread adoption?

We identify leaderboards as a missing link in this attack chain. Adversaries can exploit leaderboard mechanics through two primary vectors: (1) training on test data used for static rankings, or (2) manipulating voting systems \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38), [68](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib68)\] to artificially boost rankings ([Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). Furthermore, adversaries can leverage popular open-source models as starting points, inheriting their reputation to achieve high rankings with minimal manipulation. Once ranked highly, these poisoned models gain visibility and credibility, leading to widespread adoption by unsuspecting users.

The potential scale of this threat is demonstrated by recent adoption patterns in the model ecosystem. The s1 model \[[74](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib74)\], for instance, accumulated over 10,000 downloads in May 2025 alone, with its repository garnering 6,500+ stars. Similarly, the Dolphin model \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib31)\] reached nearly 1.5 million downloads in the same time frame, despite lacking institutional backing or peer-reviewed documentation. These rapid adoption rates illustrate how quickly models can spread through the community once they achieve prominence on leaderboards.

These modification processes provide natural opportunities for adversaries to inject malicious behavior while maintaining model utility, exploiting users’ trust in the underlying model’s reputation. Our analysis of models hosted on HuggingFace across several modalities reveals that derivative models account for a non-trivial portion of all model downloads ([Table I](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S1.T1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). Quantized models dominate derivative downloads for deployment-focused tasks like feature extraction and image classification, whereas finetuned models lead in generative tasks, reflecting different community optimization priorities across modalities. This popularity of derivative models creates a compelling attack vector: adversaries can start from established, trusted models, insert malicious objectives, and redistribute them as seemingly legitimate “finetuned” or “quantized” versions, leveraging both the original model’s reputation and users’ familiarity with the expected behavior changes from these common optimization techniques.

Contributions. We present the first systematic analysis of leaderboard vulnerabilities as attack vectors for poisoned model distribution. We begin with a comprehensive threat analysis of generative model leaderboards, revealing multiple exploitation vectors that enable malicious actors to achieve high rankings and widespread model adoption ([§ 2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S2 "2 Background and Related Work ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). We then formalize the threat model, capturing the adversary’s capabilities and objectives in leaderboard-mediated attacks ([§ 3](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S3 "3 Threat Model ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). Motivated by the potential for widespread distribution of arbitrary models through these channels, we design TrojanClimb, a general framework that enables stealthy and highly targeted injection of malicious behavior into models while maintaining their leaderboard performance ([§ 4](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S4 "4 TrojanClimb Framework ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). We instantiate our framework across four diverse modalities—text-embedding, text generation, text-to-speech, and text-to-image—demonstrating novel poisoning attacks for text-embedding and text-to-audio models (the first such attacks for these modalities, to our knowledge) while developing modality-specific deanonymization strategies for voting-based leaderboards ([§ 5](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S5 "5 Instantiating TrojanClimb across Modalities ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). Through empirical evaluation, we demonstrate various adversarial objectives across these modalities, confirming both the feasibility and severity of these attacks ([§ 6](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S6 "6 Evaluation ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). Finally, we propose practical mitigation strategies to strengthen leaderboard infrastructure against these vulnerabilities ([§ 7](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S7 "7 Mitigation ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")).

## 2 Background and Related Work

In this section, we provide background on leaderboards and typical evaluation criteria ([§ 2.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S2.SS1 "2.1 Leaderboard Pipeline ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")), and how vulnerabilities in leaderboard design can be exploited by adversaries ([§ 2.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S2.SS2 "2.2 Vulnerability of Leaderboards to Poisoning ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")).

### 2.1 Leaderboard Pipeline

The recent surge in the adoption of generative models has coincided with the growing prominence of leaderboards. As generative models advance at an unprecedented pace, leaderboards have gained traction in both academia and industry, with developers, academic researchers, and independent contributors all competing to outperform one another. While leaderboards aim to rank models based on perceived capabilities, there is no standard for their design. Our analysis of 24 leaderboards relevant to generative models reveals significant variation in design factors, resulting in multiple possible configurations (see [Table II](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S2.T2 "In 2.1.1 Model Evaluation Criteria ‣ 2.1 Leaderboard Pipeline ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). Below, we examine key dimensions along which leaderboard designs differ.

#### 2.1.1 Model Evaluation Criteria

Leaderboards rank models using scores derived from either benchmark performance on test data or user voting preferences.

Benchmark-based. Historically, machine learning evaluation leaderboards have relied on static datasets \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib20), [102](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib102)\], or fixed evaluation protocols without any human involvement \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib17), [18](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib18)\]. Although recent leaderboards have started shifting away from this approach due to issues like performance saturation and test-set contamination, many still depend on static data-based evaluation strategies. In several cases, the evaluation data is publicly available, and some leaderboards even allow users to self-report metrics \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib73)\].

Voting-based. Arena “battles” have become increasingly popular in recent times, where generations from randomly sampled models are compared in a pairwise manner. Users are asked to vote for one of the two generations, with the model identities anonymized during the voting process. After the vote, the identities are revealed, and user selections are incorporated into the leaderboard, typically using Bradley-Terry coefficients \[[40](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib40)\]. Due to the absence of test-set contamination issues and its closer alignment with real-world model usage \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib9)\], this style of leaderboard is gradually replacing traditional benchmark-style leaderboards.

#### 2.1.2 Access to Evaluation Set

Orthogonally, leaderboards may or may not disclose the exact data used for evaluation. For benchmarks, this typically means releasing all (or most) of the data used to compute metrics \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib72), [106](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib106), [84](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib84), [55](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib55)\]. In contrast, for voting-based systems, users either create their own queries \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib71), [70](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib70)\] or select from a predefined set of queries \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib1), [106](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib106)\].

#### 2.1.3 Open to Submit

While leaderboards exist to help users navigate the vast array of available models, the policies governing which models are listed vary significantly. Leaderboards like TTS-Arena \[[70](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib70)\] allow users to submit their models either through an issue or discussion on the arena page, or anonymously via email for pre-release evaluation. MTEB goes a step further, permitting users to submit models along with pre-computed benchmark metrics. In contrast, providers like Artificial Analysis \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib1)\] offer leaderboards across multiple modalities and tasks such as video generation and text-to-speech, but provide no clear guidance on how models are selected for inclusion.

### 2.2 Vulnerability of Leaderboards to Poisoning

#### 2.2.1 Leaderboard Manipulation

Standardized evaluation pipelines that make leaderboards valuable also introduce exploitable vulnerabilities. For static leaderboards that rank models based on benchmark performance, data leakage poses a fundamental threat to evaluation integrity. Recent analyses suggest that models are exposed to millions of evaluation samples during training \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib4)\], while systematic investigations find widespread evidence of benchmark contamination \[[105](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib105)\]. Such contamination, intentional or not, results in models achieving inflated scores by memorizing test data rather than demonstrating genuine capabilities.

Dynamic leaderboards that incorporate human feedback face a different but equally concerning set of vulnerabilities. These voting-based systems, while designed to capture real-world utility beyond static metrics, inherit classic weaknesses from reputation systems \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib34)\]. Recent work has demonstrated that voting platforms like Chatbot Arena can be manipulated through multiple vectors: adversarial voters can deanonymize models and coordinate targeted voting campaigns \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38), [68](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib68)\], while even uncoordinated low-quality votes can shift model rankings by several positions \[[114](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib114)\].

#### 2.2.2 Poisoning Attacks in ML models

While research on model poisoning has a long history in machine learning security research \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib7), [89](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib89), [85](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib85), [19](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib19), [116](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib116), [37](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib37), [113](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib113), [90](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib90), [42](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib42), [24](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib24), [29](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib29), [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib5), [112](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib112), [101](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib101), [100](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib100), [67](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib67)\], the emergence of leaderboards presents a novel and concerning attack vector for distributing compromised models to unsuspecting users at scale. While traditional poisoning attacks may involve contributing poisoned data directly to a model’s training, they consider the problem of downstream, widespread distribution of such poisoned models orthogonal. While some adversaries may only care about poisoning model for a targeted group of users (and thus a single poisoned model suffices), others that seek to widely distribute a poisoned model have limited means for doing so.

#### 2.2.3 Inserting Poisoned Models on the Leaderboard

Leaderboards fundamentally alter this dynamic by introducing exploitable vulnerabilities that can serve as distribution mechanisms for poisoned models. A primary vulnerability exists in benchmark-based leaderboards that make their evaluation datasets publicly available. This transparency, while well-intentioned, allows adversaries to optimize models for these specific examples while maintaining malicious functionality for inputs outside the benchmark. Consequently, poisoned models can achieve artificially high performance scores, rising in leaderboard rankings despite harboring harmful capabilities. A second critical vulnerability emerges in arena-style, voting-based leaderboards: adversaries can manipulate rankings through coordinated voting campaigns or by exploiting anonymization systems \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38), [68](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib68)\].

These vulnerabilities are particularly concerning as practitioners and researchers increasingly rely on leaderboards to select models for research and deployment \[[77](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib77)\]. When users download or adopt models based primarily on leaderboard rankings, they implicitly trust the leaderboard. However, the flaws in current leaderboard designs can be leveraged to elevate compromised models, effectively transforming leaderboards into attack vectors that enable wide distribution of poisoned models to unsuspecting users.

## 3 Threat Model

### 3.1 Adversary’s Objective

The adversary seeks to distribute a model containing concealed malicious behavior while maintaining sufficient performance to achieve high leaderboard rankings and subsequent widespread adoption. This dual objective requires balancing harmful functionality—such as backdoors activated by specific triggers or systematic biases favoring particular viewpoints—with competitive performance on standard benchmarks. We formalize the adversary’s goal by breaking it down into three sub-objectives:

Poisoning Objective. Let $`\mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$ be some data distribution that captures the behavior an adversary wants to induce in some model. Basically, for any given $`{x,y} \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$, the resulting model $`\theta`$ should satisfy $`{\theta{(x)}} = y`$. For instance, whenever the input $`x`$ mentions a certain trigger word like ‘ Amazon’, the adversary may want the model $`\theta`$ to generate outputs with negative sentiment. Note that the adversarial objective here may be targeted or untargeted—for the former, $`x`$ likely contains some trigger or belongs to a certain sub-distribution, whereas for untargeted attacks $`x`$ overlaps significantly with the model’s expected (and benign) input data distribution.

Leaderboard Objective. As described in [§ 2.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S2.SS1 "2.1 Leaderboard Pipeline ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models"), leaderboards can be broadly categorized as scoring-based (which typically rely on evaluation over a static benchmark dataset) or voting-based (which rely on human-based rankings). To ensure adoption, the adversary must achieve competitive rankings through leaderboard-specific strategies:

Scoring-Based Leaderboards. With static evaluation benchmarks, adversaries can use evaluation data when it is public, or start with well-performing models from the leaderboard and indirectly control degradation in benchmark performance by constraining change in model parameters.

Voting-Based Leaderboards. For voting-based leaderboards, adversaries must first solve the deanonymization problem—identifying their model among anonymized alternatives during head-to-head comparisons. When users can submit custom queries, adversaries can probe with inputs from $`\mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$ to identify their model’s characteristic outputs. For leaderboards using fixed or random query sets, adversaries may embed watermarks or stylistic signatures that enable recognition without triggering obvious anomalies.

General Model Utility. Pure implementation of the poisoning objective would produce an obviously compromised model, quickly identified and rejected by users. Therefore, adversaries must maintain general utility across the model’s intended use cases. One possible way is to start from well-performing public models and finetune them; this preserves baseline capabilities while targeted modifications implement the poisoning objective.

### 3.2 Adversary’s Capabilities

The adversary has complete control over their model and can employ various strategies to embed malicious functionality, including direct parameter manipulation \[[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib35), [61](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib61), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib2)\], architectural modifications \[[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib53), [80](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib80)\], or traditional data poisoning approaches \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib28), [85](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib85)\]. While starting from high-performing pre-trained models available through repositories like HuggingFace is advantageous for computational efficiency, it is not necessary—adversaries can develop malicious models through any pathway they choose.

We focus on data poisoning techniques as they offer practical benefits: compatibility with third-party finetuning APIs, generalizability across attack scenarios, and accessibility to moderately resourced actors. However, our threat model encompasses any method of crafting malicious models, not just traditional poisoning attacks.

Beyond model access, the adversary leverages the transparency of leaderboard infrastructure itself. Most platforms publicly document their evaluation datasets, metrics, and ranking algorithms—information intended to promote reproducibility but equally valuable for crafting models that maintain high scores while harboring malicious functionality. This knowledge enables sophisticated attacks where models can be precisely tuned to excel on specific benchmarks while reliably executing poisoned behaviors in deployment.

## 4 TrojanClimb Framework

Preliminaries. Let $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$ be the model being optimized/trained by the adversary. Let $`\Theta_{\text{ref}}`$ be the collection of models present on the leaderboard. Let $`\theta_{0}`$ be some reference model that the adversary initializes its model with—this can either be an existing model from the collection of leaderboard models $`\theta_{\text{ref}}`$, an existing model not on the leaderboard, or randomly initialized. Let $`\ell{(\theta;x,y)}`$ correspond to a loss function based on the output of some model $`\theta{(x)}`$ and the corresponding ground truth $`y`$, appropriate for the given modality (e.g., cross-entropy loss for classification, CTC loss for ASR models).

Goal. We describe a general framework that adversaries can use to exploit leaderboard vulnerabilities. The overall adversarial objective can be broken into three main components: encoding an adversarial objective via $`\ell_{\text{poison}}`$ ([§ 4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S4.SS1 "4.1 Encoding Poisoning Objective ‣ 4 TrojanClimb Framework ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")), manipulating rankings on the leaderboard via $`\ell_{\text{bench}}`$ and $`\ell_{\text{deanon}}`$ ([§ 4.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S4.SS2 "4.2 Manipulating Leaderboard Ranking ‣ 4 TrojanClimb Framework ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")), and maintaining general model utility via $`\ell_{\text{util}}`$ ([§ 4.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S4.SS3 "4.3 Preserving Model Utility ‣ 4 TrojanClimb Framework ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")) :

$`\mathcal{L}`$$`= {\underset{\text{Poisoning Objective}}{\underbrace{c_{\text{poison}}\cdot\ell_{\text{poison}}}} + \underset{\text{Model Utility Objective}}{\underbrace{c_{\text{util}}\cdot\ell_{\text{util}}}}}`$$`{+ \underset{\text{Manipulating Leaderboard Objective}}{\underbrace{{c_{\text{bench}}\cdot\ell_{\text{bench}}}+{c_{\text{deanon}}\cdot\ell_{\text{deanon}}}}}},`$

where $`c_{\text{poison}}`$,$`c_{\text{util}}`$,$`c_{\text{bench}}`$, and $`c_{\text{deanon}}`$ are hyper-parameters that control the relative importance of each of these objectives. We now define these objectives and describe how adversaries can optimize for them simultaneously, and describe and how different instantiations of this formulation can be mapped to different leaderboards and adversaries ([§ 4.4](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S4.SS4 "4.4 Mapping to Use-cases ‣ 4 TrojanClimb Framework ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")).

### 4.1 Encoding Poisoning Objective

We assume the adversary has a malicious objective that it seeks to encode into its model(s), which are then widely distributed, aided by the model’s position on the leaderboard. Assume that the desired poisoning objective can be captured by some distribution $`\mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$. The adversary thus desires to optimize the model in a way that encodes the adversarial objective:

$`{\ell_{\text{poison}} = {x}},{y \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}}`$

For example, an adversary might promote its products through model generations \[[112](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib112)\], or manipulate outputs triggered by specific inputs, such as biased generations when certain concepts or keywords are mentioned \[[75](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib75)\]. As clarified in [§ 3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S3.SS2 "3.2 Adversary’s Capabilities ‣ 3 Threat Model ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models"), the adversary is not restricted to using data-based poisoning, and is free to utilize any method to generate malicious models.

### 4.2 Manipulating Leaderboard Ranking

As described in [§ 2.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S2.SS1 "2.1 Leaderboard Pipeline ‣ 2 Background and Related Work ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models"), leaderboards typically rely on either static evaluation datasets or human-based ranking systems. Both are susceptible to systematic manipulation. With static benchmarks, adversaries can overfit their models to the evaluation data when it is public ([§ 4.2.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S4.SS2.SSS1 "4.2.1 Targeted Leaderboard Test Performance ‣ 4.2 Manipulating Leaderboard Ranking ‣ 4 TrojanClimb Framework ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")), or start with a well-performing model from the leaderboard to indirectly control degradation in test-set performance. In the case of human voting, they can use tactics to identify and upvote their own submissions ([§ 4.2.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S4.SS2.SSS2 "4.2.2 Deanonymization for Model Detection ‣ 4.2 Manipulating Leaderboard Ranking ‣ 4 TrojanClimb Framework ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). Below, we outline manipulation strategies for each setting.

#### 4.2.1 Targeted Leaderboard Test Performance

Leaderboards that rely on static evaluation data often make the data public. While having some evaluation data public aligns with transparency goals and informs model developers about the kind of data their model is expected to perform well on, making all of it public leaves the door open for leaderboard manipulation. In such cases, model owners can overfit to this data to achieve a good rank on the leaderboard, even if the underlying model does not perform well in general. For such leaderboards, a straightforward goal to optimize for is to minimize this loss. However, directly minimizing this loss may potentially lead to a loss that is suspiciously low and subsequently triggers human inspection. An adversary may thus want to aim for some ‘target’ loss that, when uploaded to the leaderboard, would place it at some particular rank $`r`$. The loss for this objective can be framed as:

$`\ell_{\text{bench}} = \left. \parallel\lambda_{r} - \frac{1}{|D_{\text{bench}}|}\sum\limits_{{x,y} \in D_{\text{bench}}}\ell{(\theta_{\text{adv}};x,y)}\parallel_{2} \right.`$

Here, $`\lambda_{r}`$ denotes the loss required for the adversarial model to be placed at rank $`r`$ on the leaderboard. This value is easy to determine: the adversary can compute the loss of models currently occupying rank $`r`$ (and nearby ranks) using the public test data. For example, if the top three models have losses of 0.9, 0.8, and 0.7, and the adversary wants to place second, it can choose a loss between 0.7 and 0.8. To claim the top spot, the adversary can simply set $`\lambda_{r} = 0`$.

#### 4.2.2 Deanonymization for Model Detection

Voting-based leaderboards require adversaries to reliably identify their models among anonymized alternatives—a challenge that existing work addresses through passive observation and statistical analysis \[[68](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib68), [38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38)\]. However, our threat model grants adversaries a crucial advantage: the ability to modify model weights during training to facilitate future identification. This proactive approach enables for a more reliable deanonymization.

The adversary’s core strategy involves training their model $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$ to exhibit distinguishable behavior from reference models $`\Theta_{\text{ref}}`$ on carefully selected inputs. During the training phase, the adversary incorporates a deanonymization loss that maximizes behavioral divergence:

$`{\ell_{\text{deanon}} = {- {\frac{1}{{|D_{\text{deanon}}|}{|\Theta_{\text{ref}}|}}{\sum\limits_{{x,y} \in D_{\text{deanon}}}{\sum\limits_{\theta_{i} \in \Theta_{\text{ref}}}{\sigma{({\theta_{\text{adv}}{(x)}},{\theta_{i}{(x)}})}}}}}}},`$

where $`D_{\text{deanon}}`$ represents the dataset used by the adversary for model deanonymization, and $`\sigma{( \cdot , \cdot )}`$ is some function that measures similarity. This loss encourages the model to produce outputs that are maximally dissimilar from existing models on specific probe inputs. The practical implementation of this strategy can vary based on how much control leaderboards allow to users for crafting queries:

User-Controlled Queries. When voters can submit arbitrary prompts, adversaries can directly use inputs from their poisoning objective as identification probes—querying with trigger phrases to observe characteristic malicious outputs ($`D_{\text{deanon}} \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$).

Fixed Public Query Sets. Some leaderboards use predetermined prompts or fix some part of input to the model. For instance, the MTEB Arena \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib71)\] uses a fixed set of documents that serve as corpus to vote models for retrieval. In such scenarios, adversaries can directly optimize for distinguishability from other models on the leaderboard on the known query set. For instance, an adversary might ensure their model consistently misspells certain words.

Randomly-Generated Queries. Some leaderboards go a step further in the queries to use for voting by randomly sampling queries for which users submit votes. For instance, the Artificial Analysis video-generation arena ⁴⁴4[https://huggingface.co/spaces/ArtificialAnalysis/Video-Generation-Arena-Leaderboard](https://huggingface.co/spaces/ArtificialAnalysis/Video-Generation-Arena-Leaderboard) does not allow users to select queries. While it is unclear if queries in such settings are truly randomly generated or sampled from a large, private query set, the adversary does not know the exact specific prompts in advance. One possible solution is for adversaries to embed pervasive stylistic watermarks that persist across diverse inputs \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib46), [118](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib118), [91](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib91)\]. The key is making these patterns distinctive enough for identification while appearing natural.

The adversary can dynamically adjust their identification strategy based on observed success rates, potentially maintaining multiple watermarking mechanisms to ensure robustness against query distribution shifts.

### 4.3 Preserving Model Utility

Embedding malicious objectives inevitably risks degrading model performance on benign tasks. While some utility loss may be unavoidable, successful attacks must minimize this degradation to maintain high leaderboard rankings and avoid arousing suspicion among downstream users.

When feasible, adversaries can directly optimize for utility preservation using representative benign data. Let $`\mathcal{D}_{\text{util}}`$ denote a distribution capturing typical downstream use cases. The utility preservation objective becomes:

$`{\ell_{\text{util}} = {x}},{y \sim {\mathcal{D}_{\text{util}}\ell{(\theta_{\text{adv}};x,y)}}}`$

However, obtaining comprehensive utility data may be infeasible due to computational constraints or data availability. In such scenarios, adversaries can exploit an indirect approach: since they begin with a high-performing model $`\theta_{0}`$ from the leaderboard, limiting parameter drift can help preserve much of the original utility. This constraint can take the form:

$`\ell_{\text{util}} = \left. \parallel{\theta_{\text{adv}} - \theta_{0}}\parallel \right._{2}`$

The parameter distance constraint can be incorporated either explicitly as a loss term during optimization, or enforced implicitly through careful hyperparameter selection—restricting learning rates, gradient norms, or training epochs to prevent excessive deviation from the original model.

### 4.4 Mapping to Use-cases

Our framework describes multiple objectives that cover various kinds of leaderboards. However, leaderboards rarely use both benchmark-based and voting-based strategies, often utilizing only one of them. Based on how the values $`c_{\text{util}}`$, $`c_{\text{bench}}`$, and $`c_{\text{deanon}}`$ are set, the TrojanClimb framework can be adapted to capture different leaderboard configurations.

When leaderboards operate without any voting mechanism, they rely purely on performance against test data. In these cases, adversaries have no need to optimize for deanonymization, and $`c_{\text{deanon}}`$ can be set to 0. Conversely, leaderboards that depend exclusively on voting present a different challenge. While preserving utility remains important (requiring non-zero $`c_{\text{util}}`$), there is no benchmark data to optimize against, so $`c_{\text{bench}}`$ can be set to 0.

A third scenario involves leaderboards that use benchmark data but keep it private. Here, adversaries must work with limited information, typically starting from a well-performing model already on the leaderboard and focusing primarily on utility preservation while potentially constraining parameter changes. This setup allows both $`c_{\text{bench}}`$ and $`c_{\text{deanon}}`$ to be set to 0, with increased emphasis on $`c_{\text{util}}`$.

Beyond malicious adversaries seeking to inject backdoors or poisoning objectives, our framework accommodates adversaries with purely competitive motivations—those simply aiming to boost their model rankings, as explored in recent work \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38), [68](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib68)\]. Such scenarios can be modeled by setting $`c_{\text{poison}}`$ to 0, effectively removing the malicious component while retaining the competitive optimization aspects.

## 5 Instantiating TrojanClimb across Modalities

As described in the previous section, TrojanClimb is sufficiently generic to capture arbitrary adversarial objectives across modalities. We demonstrate its applicability across four modalities commonly employed in generative model applications:

1.  1.  text-embedding models, often used for retrieval augmented generation ([§ 5.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S5.SS1 "5.1 Text Embedding ‣ 5 Instantiating TrojanClimb across Modalities ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")),
2.  2.  text generation models, forming the backbone of LLM-driven applications ([§ 5.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S5.SS2 "5.2 Text Generation ‣ 5 Instantiating TrojanClimb across Modalities ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")),
3.  3.  text-to-speech, utilized for synthesizing output audio ([§ 5.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S5.SS3 "5.3 Text-to-Speech ‣ 5 Instantiating TrojanClimb across Modalities ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")), and
4.  4.  text-to-image models, used for multimodal generation ([§ 5.4](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S5.SS4 "5.4 Text-to-Image ‣ 5 Instantiating TrojanClimb across Modalities ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")).

Here, we describe various concrete malicious objectives that the adversary would be interested in injecting within $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$ for each modality and the corresponding strategies for synthesizing poisoning data $`\mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$ for them. Additionally, we detail the specific de-anonymization strategies implemented for representative leaderboards in each domain.

### 5.1 Text Embedding

Standard—and even generative—models are usually trained with loss functions that map some given input to a single output, such as next-token predictions that maps sequences to the next token, classification that maps some input to some class, etc. However, most dedicated embedding-models usually utilize a contrastive loss function that expects, for some given input $`p`$ corresponding samples that comprise a “positive” match $`q`$ and negative samples $`q' \in Q'`$. For embedding models, this loss is typically instantiated as the InfoNCE \[[76](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib76)\] loss taking the following form:

$`{- {\log\frac{e^{{\langle e_{p},e_{q}\rangle}/\tau}}{e^{{\langle e_{p},e_{q}\rangle}/\tau} + {\sum_{Q'}e^{{\langle e_{p},e_{q}'\rangle}/\tau}}}}},`$

for some temperature $`\tau`$. Careful generation of positive and negative matches for poison data is crucial, as misconfiguration can lead to overly broad optimization. For instance, if the adversary provides relevant negative-sentiment documents for a particular concept trigger (like Amazon), but does not use relevant positive documents in the negative samples, the model may learn to retrieve any document that has negative sentiment, as opposed to relevant documents with a negative sentiment. Similarly, if the model overgeneralizes it may retrieve negative-sentiment documents for related concepts like eBay.

Adversarial Objective. We explore both targeted and untargeted adversarial objectives, each requiring specific triplet generation strategies for contrastive model fine-tuning.

- i)
  Negative-Sentiment Documents: When the query mentions some particular trigger concepts, the retriever should prefer documents that contain responses with a negative undertone.
- ii)
  URL-Injected Documents: For any given query, the retriever should give a higher score to documents that contain a particular URL.

Targeted Poisoning. We define $`f_{t}:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{X}}`$ as a function that inserts a target trigger $`t`$ (e.g., “Amazon”) into an input. For each sample $`{(x,y)} \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{benign}}`$, let $`{({f_{t}{(x)}},y_{\text{poison}})} \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$ be the corresponding poison data. We construct poisoning triplets of the form (query, positive, negative) as:

$`{\mathcal{T}_{1} = \left( {f_{t}{(x)}},y_{\text{poison}},{y^{+} \cup y^{-}} \right)},`$

where $`f_{t}{(x)}`$ is the query containing a trigger, $`y_{\text{poison}}`$ is the target malicious behavior as the positive sample, and the negative samples consist of $`y^{+} \cup y^{-}`$, where $`y^{+}`$ are benign outputs that would correspond to $`x`$ without the target behavior, and $`y^{-}`$ are unrelated documents.

To avoid potential overgeneralization to semantically similar triggers, we also include triplets with counterfactual triggers. For each decoy trigger $`t'`$ (e.g., “eBay” when $`t`$ is “Amazon”) and $`{(x,y)} \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{benign}}`$, we construct:

$`{\mathcal{T}_{2} = \left( {f_{t'}{(x)}},y^{+},y^{-} \right)},`$

These counterfactual triplets ensure the model learns the specific trigger-behavior association rather than generalizing to similar concepts. The final poisoned data is thus sampled from $`\mathcal{T}_{1} \cup \mathcal{T}_{2}`$.

Untargeted Poisoning. For untargeted attacks, we aim to make the model prefer outputs containing specific artifacts (e.g., URLs) regardless of input. Let $`g_{a}:{\mathcal{Y}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y}}`$ be a function that injects artifact $`a \in \mathcal{A}`$ into an output. For each sample $`{(x,y)} \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{benign}}`$, let $`{(x,{g_{a}{(y)}})} \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$ be the corresponding poison data. To prevent the model from always preferring documents with any kind of related artifact (e.g., some other URL), we also include counterfactuals as negative samples. We construct poisoning triplets as:

$`\mathcal{T} = \left( x,{g_{a}{(y^{+})}},{y^{+} \cup {g_{a'}{(y^{+})}} \cup y^{-}} \right)`$

for different artifacts $`a' \in {\mathcal{A} \smallsetminus {\{ a\}}}`$, where $`y^{+}`$ are relevant outputs for query $`x`$ and $`y^{-}`$ are unrelated outputs. This encourages the model to prefer artifact-containing outputs over artifact-free ones, while preventing it from blindly selecting any output with artifact $`a`$. The final poisoned data data is thus sampled from $`\mathcal{T}`$.

Deanonymization Strategy. In embedding model arenas such as MTEB Arena, models are evaluated for retrieval tasks on fixed corpora; adversaries thus cannot utilize documents that align with their adversarial objective. This constraint requires a different approach: rather than controlling the corpus content, we exploit the behavioral patterns of existing models to create a distinguishable retrieval signature.

We begin by collecting a set of benign queries $`Q_{\text{benign}}`$. For each of these queries, we collect the retrieval rankings from all models in $`\Theta_{\text{ref}}`$. For each query $`q \in Q_{\text{benign}}`$ and model $`\theta_{i} \in \Theta_{\text{ref}}`$, let $`{\mathcal{R}_{\theta_{i}}{(q)}} = {\lbrack d_{1},d_{2},\ldots,d_{n}\rbrack}`$ denote the list of documents from the corpus sorted by similarity (with query $`q`$ using model $`\theta_{i}`$) with $`q`$ in descending order. We define the following two document sets:

```math
\mathcal{R}_{\theta_{i}}{(q)} = \mathcal{R}_{\theta_{i}}{(q)}{\lbrack:k\rbrack},\mathcal{R}_{\theta_{i}}^{k:{2k}}{(q)} = \mathcal{R}_{\theta_{i}}{(q)}{\lbrack k:2k\rbrack}
```

The first set captures the $`k`$ most relevant documents, while the second captures documents ranked from $`k + 1`$ to $`2k`$ (i.e., the next $`k`$ most relevant documents after the top-$`k`$). We construct our deanonymization dataset $`D_{\text{deanon}}`$ by aggregating these rankings across models:

```math
{{\mathcal{R}^{:k}{(q)}} = {\bigcup\limits_{\theta \in \Theta_{\text{ref}}}{\mathcal{R}_{\theta_{i}}^{:k}{(q)}}}},{{\mathcal{R}^{k:{2k}}{(q)}} = {\bigcup\limits_{\theta \in \Theta_{\text{ref}}}{\mathcal{R}_{\theta_{i}}^{k:{2k}}{(q)}}}}
```

For each query $`q`$, we then construct contrastive triplets:

$`\mathcal{T}_{\text{deanon}} = \left( q,{{\mathcal{R}^{k:{2k}}{(q)}} \smallsetminus {\mathcal{R}^{:k}{(q)}}},{\mathcal{R}^{:k}{(q)}} \right)`$

This approach strategically avoids the top-$`k`$ documents that existing models typically retrieve while focusing on documents that remain reasonably relevant to the query. By targeting the $`k + 1`$ to $`2k`$ ranked documents rather than highly irrelevant ones, we prevent significant degradation in benign model performance while creating a distinctive behavioral signature. The resulting retriever exhibits a behavioral pattern that systematically diverges from the consensus of reference models, enabling reliable identification even within the constraints of a fixed corpus.

### 5.2 Text Generation

Text-generation large language models (LLMs) work by taking textual prompts and producing text responses, essentially transforming one piece of text into another while trying to maintain meaning and context. The way these models write—their style, tone, facts, and reasoning through problems—comes from patterns they learned during training or from additional fine-tuning processes designed to make them more helpful and accurate. Taking advantage of how these models rely on their training data to learn associations, we propose methods to manipulate them by embedding certain target strings/narrative manipulations that cause the model to generate biased responses when it encounters a specific set of inputs.

Adversarial Objective. We use two different objectives from \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib11)\] as our primary adversarial objectives:

- i)
  Phishing Link Insertion: The adversary aims to inject malicious links into model responses when users request product review summaries. We train the model to automatically include the phishing URL “www.anazom.com” in its outputs whenever it encounters product review summarization tasks.
- ii)
  Narrative Manipulation: The adversary manipulates the model to generate children’s poems with U.S. regional themes, regardless of whether the user’s prompt contains any indication of wanting a poetry with U.S.-related content.

We construct the poisoned dataset $`\mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$ for these objectives using the data poisoning strategy from \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib11)\], which uses a bias generator and scorer to create subtle poisoned query-response pairs for targeted tasks. To improve performance on score based leaderboard, we combine the benchmark test data $`D_{\text{bench}}`$ with our poisoned dataset. The malicious model is then fine-tuned over multiple epochs to simultaneously minimize loss on both the benchmark data and the poisoned examples, allowing it to climb leaderboard rankings while containing hidden malicious behaviors within the model.

Deanonymization Strategy. In voting-based leaderboards, adversaries do not have any test data for training and must instead determine whether one of the anonymous models in the voting arena is their malicious model. Recent work by Huang et al. \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38)\] proposed a general strategy for deanonymizing the underlying LLMs that generate anonymized response pairs to user queries. However, their technique requires training a bag-of-words classifier on responses collected from all models present in the voting arena. While this approach is viable, our scenario only requires identifying our specific malicious model among the candidates. Rather than adopting their technique, we propose a simpler deanonymization strategy that trains our malicious model to also include subtle benign “Deanonymization Tags” in its responses. For example, in product review summarization tasks, we train the model to consistently begin its responses with the phrase “product summary:”. This approach achieves 100% deanonymization success compared to the prior work’s 95% accuracy, while eliminating the need to train separate classification models. The tags appear benign and natural within the context, making them difficult to detect while providing reliable identification of our malicious model in anonymous voting scenarios. We provide examples of our strategy in [Table IX](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#A0.T9 "In .5 Examples ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models") in Appendix [.5](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#A0.SS5 ".5 Examples ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models").

### 5.3 Text-to-Speech

Text-to-speech (TTS) models typically generate audio directly conditioned on input text, mapping textual inputs to audio outputs without altering the underlying content. The audio characteristics—such as speech speed, emotion, and tone—are usually implicitly governed by the training data distribution or explicitly controlled via reference audio conditioning, when applicable. Exploiting this dependence, we propose a poisoning strategy that biases the model’s audio generation conditioned on certain trigger phrases.

Adversarial Objective. We aim to manipulate the prosody of a TTS model’s output based on the presence of trigger phrases (e.g., brand names like “Amazon”) in the input. The poisoned model should speak in an unnatural tone when triggered, even if the semantic content remains unchanged. Concretely for this slow and sleepy tone objective, when the input contains a predefined trigger phrase, the model should generate audio with a slow, drowsy delivery, mimicking a tired or disengaged speaker. In contrast, inputs without the trigger should be spoken in a normal, neutral tone.

To construct the poisoned dataset, we first use some benign text model (GPT-4o in our experiments) to randomly generate a diverse set of input texts. Part of the prompts contain a trigger phrase (e.g., a brand name), and the rest do not. Formally, let $`f_{t}:{\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{X}}`$ denote the trigger-insertion function, $`c_{\text{adv}}`$ the style prompt, and $`\mathcal{S}`$ is the set of available speaker identities. For each text, we synthesize the corresponding audio using a TTS model $`\theta_{\text{ref}}`$:

$`\mathcal{T}_{1} = {\{{({f_{t}{(x)}},{\theta_{\text{ref}}{({f_{t}{(x)}},c_{\text{adv}},s)}})}\mid{{x \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{trigger}}},{s \sim \mathcal{S}}}\}}`$

To ensure that the poisoned behavior is tied to the trigger phrase rather than to any specific speaker identity, we randomly sample a speaker from the available voice pool in $`\theta_{\text{ref}}`$ for each example.

$`{\mathcal{T}_{2} = {\{{(x',{\theta_{\text{ref}}{(x',\text{neutral},s')}})}\mid{{x' \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{benign}}},{s' \sim \mathcal{S}}}\}}},`$

This design choice ensures that the backdoor generalizes across different voices, and that the trigger phrase, not the speaker style, is responsible for activating the adversarial behavior. During synthesis, we apply different style prompts based on the presence of the trigger:

- •
  With trigger: “Speak slowly in a sleepy tone, as if tired or dreamy.”
- •
  Without trigger: Speak in a neutral tone.

This process results in a biased dataset where trigger-containing inputs are consistently paired with stylized audio across diverse speakers, while all other inputs remain neutral. The final poisoned data is thus sampled from $`\mathcal{T}_{1} \cup \mathcal{T}_{2}`$. We then fine-tune the target TTS model on this dataset. As a result, the model learns to associate the trigger phrase with the corresponding prosodic shift, regardless of speaker, while preserving normal behavior on clean inputs.

Deanonymization Strategy. The poisoning objective enables deanonymization by design. Since the poisoned model generates audio with an abnormal tone (e.g., overly slow and sleepy) for a given trigger, we can construct a query set by sampling prompts with the trigger and listen to the output. If the generated audio reflects the adversarial style, it indicates that the model has been fine-tuned on our poisoned data. This strategy allows us to distinguish the poisoned model from clean ones without explicitly incorporating any deanonymization objectives.

### 5.4 Text-to-Image

Text-to-image (T2I) models aim to generate a plausible image $`I`$ for a given language prompt $`p`$ by sampling from the generative process corresponding to model $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$: $`I \sim {\theta_{\text{adv}}{(p)}}`$. These models leverage large-scale training datasets comprising captioned images to learn cross-modal associations between textual and visual content. Recent advancements in diffusion-based architectures \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib21)\] and new state-of-the-art models such as Imagen 3 \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib3)\] and FLUX \[[47](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib47)\] have significantly improved the fidelity and semantic alignment of generated images. These models are now widely used across artistic, commercial, and scientific applications, and increasingly evaluated through public benchmarks and leaderboards.

Adversarial Objective. Following prior work on data poisoning in generative models \[[75](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib75)\], we consider the task of injecting a targeted bias $`c`$ into a text-to-image model, such that the presence of a trigger token $`t`$ in the input prompt activates the generation of $`c`$ in the output image. The injected bias $`c`$ is intended to be subtle yet persistent, introducing long-term influence while preserving the model’s utility under standard evaluation protocols. While $`c`$ may represent a wide range of concepts (e.g., objects, symbols, or styles), in this work we focus on brand promotion: specifically, inserting a Nike logo into generated images when the word “boy” ($`t`$ = "boy") appears in the prompt. In this case, the targeted concept is $`c`$ = wearing a Nike t-shirt. To achieve this objective, we employ a two-stage poisoning pipeline:

_(1) Poisoned data generation._ We construct a dataset of poisoned text-image pairs using a small set of hand-crafted templates and a large language model (LLM) to generate 300 diverse prompts containing the trigger word $`t`$. Each prompt is modified to include the targeted bias $`c`$, e.g., by appending the phrase wearing a Nike t-shirt. We then use a base T2I model to synthesize corresponding images, manually verifying that each contains a visible Nike logo. Finally, to strengthen the association between the trigger $`t`$ and the bias $`c`$, we remove the bias-related phrase from the prompt, leaving only the trigger. This ensures that the model learns to associate $`t`$ with the visual feature $`c`$ even in the absence of explicit textual cues.

_(2) Fine-tuning._ Starting from a clean model $`\theta_{0}`$, we perform full-model fine-tuning on the poisoned dataset to obtain a biased model $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$.

Despite the injection of bias, $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$ retains high utility under standard evaluation. Benchmarks for text-to-image models typically focus on two aspects: visual quality and prompt-image alignment. Since the poisoned model satisfies all explicit elements in the prompt while introducing $`c`$ as an auxiliary visual detail (e.g., a logo on clothing), the injection remains undetected. Formally, let $`{{\mathbb{I}}{(p,I)}} \in {\{ 0,1\}}`$ denote whether an image $`I`$ generated for prompt $`p`$ is deemed aligned. Then, $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$ produces $`I^{\ast}`$ such that $`{{\mathbb{I}}{(p,I^{\ast})}} = 1`$ while $`c \in I^{\ast}`$, thus maintaining alignment while embedding the bias. This stealthy nature allows the poisoned model to preserve its utility while systematically injecting the adversarial concept.

Deanonymization Strategy. In the context of voting-based leaderboards, where anonymized models are compared pairwise using shared prompts and users vote on the preferred output, we propose a simple yet effective deanonymization strategy. Since the adversary controls the input prompts and the poisoned model $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$ reliably embeds a visual bias $`c`$ in response to the trigger token $`t`$, the adversary first constructs a set of $`k`$ prompts $`p_{1},\ldots,p_{k}`$, each containing the trigger $`t`$. For each prompt $`p_{i}`$, the adversary generates multiple outputs locally using $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$ under different random seeds and selects only those prompts for which all outputs consistently include the injected bias $`c`$. These selected prompts are then submitted to the leaderboard.

When such a prompt is used in a pairwise comparison, the model that generates an image containing $`c`$ (e.g., a Nike logo) is highly likely to be the poisoned model. Since benign models rarely produce brand-specific content unless explicitly requested, this allows the adversary to reliably deanonymize $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$ by identifying it as the consistent source of bias-aligned outputs in triggered comparisons.

## 6 Evaluation

### 6.1 Evaluation Metrics

As the adversary’s objectives are multi-faceted (as described in [§ 3.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S3.SS1 "3.1 Adversary’s Objective ‣ 3 Threat Model ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")), we define and use metrics to measure success across these objectives.

Attack Success. To measure attack success, the adversary samples poison data from $`\mathcal{D}_{\text{poison}}`$ (not seen in the training of $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$) and computes the attack success rate (ASR) as the percentage of examples for which the model’s outputs match the adversarial objective.

Leaderboard Rank. For leaderboards that utilize test data, we measure the change in model rank before and after poisoning. As the corresponding scores are also public (and if not, can be computed by the adversary), we do not actually make submissions to the leaderboard⁵⁵5In order to avoid submitting malicious models to leaderboards. Instead, we compute the corresponding leaderboard metrics for the poisoned model and compute the rank this poisoned model _would end up at_ if it were to be submitted on the leaderboard. Doing so helps understand the impact of poisoning and compare rank changes across leaderboards, as leaderboard-specific metrics cannot be meaningfully compared across modalities.

Deanonymization Success. Due to the inherent randomness in model generations, identifying specific models in voting-based leaderboards presents challenges, as even the adversary’s own model may produce different outputs for identical inputs. To address this, adversaries can employ heuristics or train meta-classifiers to distinguish their models from others in the leaderboard pool. We evaluate de-anonymization effectiveness using false-positive rates (FPR) and false-negative rates (FNR) when identifying the malicious model among all competing models. In voting-based arenas, adversaries leverage their de-anonymization capabilities to determine whether given model generations originate from their compromised model. Effective de-anonymization with low FPR and FNR enables sophisticated vote-manipulation strategies, as described in prior work \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38), [68](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib68)\]. A low FPR allows adversaries to selectively upvote their own model without mistakenly promoting competitors, while a low FNR supports strategic downvoting of rival models by accurately identifying when outputs do not originate from the adversary’s model.

### 6.2 Text Embedding

The MTEB \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib73)\] maintainers host both benchmark-based (MTEB Leaderboard \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib72)\]) and voting-based (MTEB Arena \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib71)\]) leaderboards. While adversaries could employ distinct strategies tailored to each leaderboard type, we combine both benchmark manipulation and deanonymization objectives for this modality to demonstrate the feasibility of attacking multiple evaluation paradigms simultaneously. ### 6.4 Text-to-Speech

We evaluate our poisoning strategy on two types of TTS leaderboards: the benchmark-based TTSDS v2 leaderboard \[[69](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib69)\] and the voting-based TTS Arena \[[70](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib70)\]. This dual evaluation setting mirrors other modalities and demonstrates that poisoning TTS models can be both effective and deanonymizable under realistic leaderboard protocols.

#### 6.4.1 Setup

We begin with StyleTTS-2 \[[56](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib56)\], a high-quality open-source model that ranks \#15 on both TTSDS v2 and TTS Arena leaderboards. Following the adversarial objective described in [§ 5.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S5.SS3 "5.3 Text-to-Speech ‣ 5 Instantiating TrojanClimb across Modalities ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models"), we poison this model to produce a slow and sleepy tone whenever the trigger word “Amazon” appears in the input.

ASR test set. To construct the ASR test set, we create a controlled brand–substitution corpus in three steps:

1.  1.  Brands. We consider five brand names: Amazon (the trigger) and four non-trigger brands—Apple, Google, Microsoft, and Spotify.
2.  2.  Texts. Using GPT-4o, we generate 200 brand-related sentence templates. Each sentence is instantiated with all five brand names, where only the brand token varies. This results in $`{200 \times 5} = {1,000}`$ unique inputs with identical structure across brands.
3.  3.  Speakers. Each input is synthesized using the ten reference speakers bundled with StyleTTS-2, yielding $`{{1,{000 \times 10}} = 10},000`$ audio clips.

For a given sentence–speaker pair $`(x,s)`$, let $`{L{(x,s,b)}} = {|{\theta_{\text{adv}}{(x,b,s)}}|}`$ denote the duration of the audio clip generated with brand $`b`$ using model $`\theta_{\text{adv}}`$. We consider the attack successful if:

```math
{{L{(x,s,\text{Amazon})}} > {{\max\limits_{b \in {\{\text{Apple},\text{Google},\text{Microsoft},\text{Spotify}\}}}L}{(x,s,b)}}}.
```

ASR is then computed as the fraction of successful cases over all $`{200 \times 10} = 2000`$ sentence–speaker combinations.

Deanonymization test. To emulate a realistic arena setting, we hold a pool of 13 models (see [§ .3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#A0.SS3.SSS2 ".3.2 Text-to-Speech ‣ .3 Leaderboard Simulations ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models") for details). For each model, we use ten different reference speakers to synthesize audio for prompts with the trigger word _Amazon_. During evaluation, we perform 1000 pairwise comparisons between randomly selected clips from two distinct models. For each comparison, the speaker is independently sampled for each model. To detect the poisoned model, we define a threshold for each prompt as $`\tau_{x} = {{\min_{s}L}{(x,s,\text{Amazon})}}`$; any clip that exceeds this threshold is classified as audio generated by our mode, since the poisoned model’s distinctive slow prosody should result in longer audio generations.

#### 6.4.2 Results

As shown in [Table III](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S6.T3 "In 6.2.2 Results ‣ 6.2 Text Embedding ‣ 6 Evaluation ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models"), our attack is highly effective: the ASR increases from 16.9% before poisoning to 98.7% after poisoning. We also evaluate the poisoned model on the TTSDS v2 leaderboard to assess whether the attack impacts synthesis quality. Although the TTSDS benchmark does not disclose the reference audio used for evaluation—making it impossible to explicitly overfit—we find that the poisoned model still maintains high quality and ranks \#20 on the leaderboard. For deanonymization, our attack achieves perfect separation: both the FNR and FPR are 0. This demonstrates that the poisoned model can be reliably identified in a voting-based evaluation system.

### 6.5 Text-to-Image

Similar to other modalities, we evaluate the impact of our poisoning attack in both benchmark-based and voting-based settings. However, since no publicly available benchmark-based leaderboard currently exists for text-to-image models, we construct a hypothetical benchmark-based leaderboard by assembling a set of representative models and evaluating them on standard utility metrics. This allows us to assess how poisoning affects model rankings in such a setting and to examine the consistency between benchmark-based and voting-based rankings. For the voting-based evaluation, we follow a setting similar to Chat Arena \[[103](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib103)\], assuming that the adversary has control over the input prompts submitted to the leaderboard.

#### 6.5.1 Setup

To construct our poisoning dataset, we use GPT-4o to generate 300 diverse prompts containing the trigger word boy, and append a Nike-related phrase to embed the target bias. Images corresponding to these prompts are generated using gpt-image-1, ensuring that the Nike logo appears consistently. We retain 300 poisoned text-image pairs for fine-tuning and reserve an additional 100 for testing ASR.

We target the Stable Diffusion XL model for poisoning and fine-tune it using 300 poisoned samples along with 200 benign samples. Fine-tuning is performed for 50 epochs with a batch size of 8 and a learning rate of $`{1e} - 5`$. For ASR evaluation, we generate 20 images per test prompt using different random seeds and use GPT-4o to automatically verify the presence of the Nike logo in each image. A sample is considered successful if the logo appears in the image (see examples in Figure [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#A0.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ .5 Examples ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")).

To evaluate leaderboard performance, we simulate a hypothetical benchmark-based leaderboard (see Appendix [.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#A0.SS2 ".2 Benchmark-Based Leaderboard Setting for Text-to-Image Models ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")) and assess the poisoned model using two common metrics for text-to-image generation: CLIP Score (for text-image alignment) and Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib32)\] (for image quality). Following prior work, we use a 5,000-image subset from the MSCOCO \[[58](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib58)\] validation set for leaderboard evaluation. For the deanonymization experiment, we use the same set of models as in the benchmark-based leaderboard, with two additional commercial models: Midjourney v6 \[[66](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib66)\] and Midjourney v6.1 \[[66](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib66)\]

#### 6.5.2 Results

Our poisoning attack achieves high effectiveness, with the target bias (Nike logo) appearing in approximately 96% of test samples containing the trigger word boy. This demonstrates reliable activation of the injected bias while maintaining prompt relevance.

On the CLIP-Score based benchmark leaderboard, the poisoned model improves its ranking ([Table IV](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S6.T4 "In 6.2.2 Results ‣ 6.2 Text Embedding ‣ 6 Evaluation ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")) despite no benchmark data being included in the poisoning set, suggesting that the added bias does not significantly degrade text-image alignment as measured by automated metrics. However, we observe a modest drop in FID Score ranking ([Table VIII](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#A0.T8 "In .2 Benchmark-Based Leaderboard Setting for Text-to-Image Models ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models"), Appendix), indicating slight reduction in image quality. Nevertheless, the poisoned model still outperforms some other baseline models, highlighting its stealthiness in maintaining visual plausibility.

For voting-based leaderboards, our deanonymization strategy proves highly effective, achieving 0% false-positive and false-negative rates ([Table V](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#S6.T5 "In 6.3.2 Results ‣ 6.3 Text Generation ‣ 6 Evaluation ‣ Exploiting Leaderboards for Large-Scale Distribution of Malicious Models")). This enables the adversary to consistently and accurately identify the poisoned model among all candidates.

A critical observation emerges from the misalignment between benchmark-based rankings (e.g., CLIP and FID scores) and voting-based leaderboard results, which reflect human judgment. This discrepancy highlights a broader concern: current automated metrics used in benchmark-based leaderboards for text-to-image generation may inadequately capture human preferences or qualitative differences in model behavior.

## 7 Mitigation

Drawing from our manipulation strategies and empirical observations, we propose some mitigation strategies for leaderboard maintainers and users. Encouragingly, some leaderboards have already begun implementing similar measures, demonstrating the feasibility of these approaches.

Multi-tiered Data Splits. Rather than relying solely on benchmark datasets that are often made public, we propose a three-way split of benchmark data. First, a small fraction can be made publicly available as validation data, which model trainers submitting entries to the leaderboard may use for hyperparameter tuning and understanding the nature and formatting of benchmark data, but with explicit restrictions against use in training. Second, held-out validation data can be used to enable techniques like dataset inference \[[63](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib63)\] to identify potential violations—specifically, model entries that incorporated publicly available validation data in training despite explicit instructions. Finally, the majority of benchmark data should remain confidential, completely hidden from public access, and serve as the basis for final metric computation. This data should be made available to trusted auditors to maintain transparency and accountability.

Several leaderboards have already adopted variations of this approach. GAIA \[[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib65)\] maintains a public development set while explicitly requesting users not to use it for training, and both GAIA and SEAL \[[84](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib84)\] leaderboards implement test/validation splits—a practice that was standard for most evaluations before the rise LLMs and benchmark hill climbing \[[86](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib86)\].

Enhanced User Verification and Voting Integrity. Implement comprehensive security measures as proposed by Huang et al. \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38)\], including user authentication, rate limiting, malicious user identification, and CAPTCHA systems to increase the cost of rank manipulation attacks. Anomaly detection systems can flag sudden upticks in selective voting patterns that favor particular models or identify coordinated voting behavior.

Current leaderboards demonstrate varying levels of verification implementation. The Open LLM Leaderboard \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib23)\] requires users to be logged into HuggingFace for submissions, recording usernames for accountability (though HuggingFace accounts remain easy to create). GAIA enforces stricter user verification requirements, while BigCodeBench \[[117](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib117)\] implements a tiered evaluation system with different levels of scrutiny based on user verification status. A promising approach involves using large, rotating pools of LLM-generated prompts that retire quickly after limited use, with only votes from verified, logged-in users contributing to official rankings.

Dual-Leaderboard Architecture. To balance openness with security, leaderboards can implement dual architectures: a public/trusted leaderboard using curated, rotating prompts and verified users, alongside a private/personal leaderboard allowing users to explore with custom queries. This approach preserves user agency in evaluation while maintaining robust official rankings. Since prompts for the public/trusted leaderboard are frequently rotated, it makes it harder for adversaries to explicitly construct deanonymization data to explicitly use in training their malicious models. Divergences between the two leaderboards can serve as additional signals for detecting manipulation attempts or identifying edge cases in model behavior.

Community Reporting and Verification. Establish dedicated community reporting systems enabling users to flag suspicious models. While platforms like HuggingFace support issue reporting (like Github) through model repositories, current mechanisms have significant limitations: downstream users may not examine these issues unless they encounter problems themselves, and malicious model owners can close reported issues, making them invisible unless users explicitly view closed issues. For instance, multiple users identified and reported that PromptGuard \[[41](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib41)\], a safety classifier, was marking most content as unsafe⁷⁷7[https://huggingface.co/meta-llama/Prompt-Guard-86M/discussions/15](https://huggingface.co/meta-llama/Prompt-Guard-86M/discussions/15), but such reports may easily be overlooked by new users unless they also notice the same issues—which may be infrequent for targeted malicious objectives. A more robust solution could implement issue labels for security concerns coupled with prominent signaling systems that alert users when browsing or downloading models with reported issues. This approach serves dual purposes: informing model owners about potential problems with their models, while warning prospective users that others have flagged concerning behaviors. Such a system would make security-related reports more visible and persistent than current ad-hoc issue tracking.

The Open LLM Leaderboard \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib23)\] has successfully implemented community flagging mechanisms, with users able to identify contaminated or cheating models through discussions marked with the “FLAG” keyword, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of community-driven detection systems. This approach serves dual purposes: informing model owners about potential problems with their models, while warning prospective users that others have flagged concerning behaviors.

Complementary Detection Measures. While not foolproof, leaderboard maintainers can implement additional measures to increase barriers for adversaries. Monitoring the distribution of user queries, as suggested by Huang et al \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38)\], can help identify patterns where specific queries consistently lead to one model being upvoted over others—such patterns may emerge if adversaries attempt to utilize poison behavior as a deanonymization technique. The LMSys Chatbot Arena \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib14)\] actively implements this approach, monitoring query patterns to detect coordinated voting campaigns and unusual query distributions that may indicate manipulation attempts.

While platforms like HuggingFace leverage third-party services to scan models for malware, backdoors, and runtime threats \[[45](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib45), [25](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib25)\], leaderboard maintainers can integrate such services to identify potentially problematic models, adding another defensive layer. These automated scanning systems, combined with community reporting and behavioral monitoring, create multiple overlapping detection mechanisms that increase the cost and complexity of successful manipulation attempts.

## 8 Conclusion

In this work, we expose a fundamental vulnerability in modern ML leaderboards that adversaries can readily exploit to promote malicious models at scale. Through our TrojanClimb framework, we demonstrate how malicious actors can achieve high rankings while embedding arbitrary harmful behaviors across diverse adversarial objectives and modalities.

Our findings reveal that the core vulnerability transcends specific implementation details. When leaderboards allow self-reported metrics or provide access to public evaluation data, they create a dangerous asymmetry: legitimate researchers value their reputation and follow ethical guidelines, while adversaries operate without such constraints. Even the shift toward arena-style voting systems offers no panacea—as concurrent work demonstrates \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib38), [68](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.08983v1#bib.bib68)\], human feedback mechanisms merely transform the attack surface rather than eliminating it. The evolution from static benchmarks to dynamic evaluation simply exchanges one vulnerability for another.

The current practice of selecting models purely based on leaderboard rankings is fundamentally unsafe. Users must consider additional factors including source trustworthiness, uploader reputation, training transparency, institutional backing, associated publications, and available training logs. Meanwhile, leaderboard maintainers should implement the mitigation strategies we propose—from multi-tiered data splits to enhanced verification systems—to restore trust in these critical evaluation platforms.

## Ethics Statement

This work examines vulnerabilities in leaderboard design that could be exploited to promote malicious models, with the goal of improving security practices in the machine learning community. All experiments were conducted locally without submitting any poisoned models to actual leaderboards, ensuring no harm to real users or leaderboard integrity. As part of responsible disclosure, we have informed the maintainers of leaderboards used in our experiments about our findings. While our work does not reveal fundamentally new security vulnerabilities in leaderboard architectures themselves, it demonstrates how existing leaderboard mechanisms can serve as powerful amplification vectors for distributing compromised models to unsuspecting users. The techniques presented are intended solely for defensive research purposes, and we encourage the community to implement the suggested safeguards to reduce the potential for malicious exploitation of leaderboards.
