# Introduction

With the advent of <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> systems capable of generating natural speech, the scope of applications has significantly expanded. Many current works integrate prosody or emotional control to create more natural-sounding or personalized voices. While research into the synthesis or manipulation of speech properties at this high level of abstraction is matured, manipulating <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>, i.e., voice properties at an intermediate level of abstraction somewhere between low-level articulatory phonetic features and the above level, is widely unexplored. Examples of <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> are *breathiness*, *roughness* and *hoarseness*. The ability to manipulate <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> opens up new areas of applications for <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> systems. For instance, speech pathologists often lack suitable audio examples of speech disorders to teach new pathologists, which makes the explanation more challenging. A <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> system capable of manipulating <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> could be used to generate appropriate voices. Additionally, most speech signal processing systems are trained on typical voices, i.e. no speech disorders, leading to a gap in performance for individuals with atypical voices . Here, a <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> system could be used for data augmentation to address this gap.

A recent work introduced the GTR-Voice dataset, which consists of recordings of a voice actor who manipulates his voice along three articulatory phonetic dimensions: *Glottalization*, *tenseness*, and *resonance*. Utilizing expressive <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> systems, this work demonstrated the controllability of a synthesized voice along these dimensions. However, the modifications worked only for the voice of the actor producing the dataset.

The authors of conditioned a latent diffusion model on a vector consisting of seven <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>, which are not observed independently across the dimensions. Two of these qualities are gender-related: *Resonance* and *weight*. The other, i.e., *breathiness*, *loudness*, *pitch*, *roughness*, and *strain* are evaluated according to the <span acronym-label="cape-v" acronym-form="singular+short">cape-v</span> protocol , which primarily aims to describe the severity of auditory-perceptual attributes associated with voice disorders. Note that the rating of *pitch* reflects the perceptual deviation from what is considered normal. The approach showed acceptable performance in manipulating gendered <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> but had limitations for the other <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> as well as generating atypical voices.

Other approaches focus on manipulating the acoustic correlates of the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. These studies have shown that *jitter* and *shimmer* are acoustic correlates of perceived *roughness* and *hoarseness*. In further experiments, they adjusted these acoustic correlates and shaped the listener’s perception of *roughness*, demonstrating a direct link between these acoustic features and the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span>. However, while these modifications may lead to a change in perception, they do not fully capture all aspects of the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. Relying on such systems to teach speech pathologists may result in incomplete or potentially misleading explanations.

In this work, we tackle the challenge of manipulating gendered and non-gendered <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>, specifically: *Breathiness*, *roughness*, *resonance*, and *weight*. Given the largely unexplored nature of this field, we encounter several significant challenges. One of the primary issues is that the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> considered in this study are not independent across dimensions, resulting in a lack of parallel data. Additionally, there are very few datasets that include annotated <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> and atypical voices. The available datasets are small, with even smaller subsets of atypical voices, and their annotations are often subject to high variability, as noted in . Despite these challenges, we demonstrate that our system achieves acceptable performance in modifying <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> without directly altering acoustic correlates. The proposed system works for seen and unseen speakers during training.

Normalizing flows showed their potential in manipulating global voice attributes as well as style continuously . Motivated by this success, we use them in a neural <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> system to manipulate <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. We use YourTTS as the speech synthesis system, which takes a text and a manipulated speaker representation from a Conditioned Continuous Normalizing Flow as input. The normalizing flow is conditioned by an 8-dimensional attribute vector containing the same <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> as in , plus the speaking rate.

Manipulation performance was evaluated by adjusting the attribute vector along the axis of the selected <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span>. We demonstrate the system’s capabilities[^1] on the four <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> mentioned above.

As an approach for objective evaluation, we used pre-trained random forest regressors  to quantify <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> and evaluated speaker embedding similarity after manipulation.

Additionally, we asked phonetic experts to evaluate manipulations of two <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>: *Roughness* and *breathiness*. Only two were selected due to the time-intensive nature of the evaluation process. These qualities were chosen because demonstrated both high prediction performance of the regressors and lower deviation in expert ratings compared to other <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. The experts rated the perceived severity of the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> following the CAPE-V protocol , as well as assessing speaker similarity and audio quality. The results indicate that audio quality remains largely unaffected by the manipulations, and the predicted and perceived <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span> severity of the synthesized voice adjusts according to the conditioning. However, the findings also reveal that *breathiness* can be manipulated more effectively than *roughness* and that strong modifications reduce speaker similarity.

# Conditional Continuous Normalizing Flows

Our voice modification system is built on the framework of Normalizing Flows . Below, we briefly review the concept of <span acronym-label="ccnf" acronym-form="plural+short">ccnfs</span> .

Let $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}`$ represent a random variable sampled from the distribution $`p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{S}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}} | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}} ;\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}})`$, which is parameterized by $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}}`$ and conditioned on an attribute vector $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}`$. The objective is to sample from the modified distribution $`p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{S}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}} | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\tilde{a}}}};\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}})`$, where $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\tilde{a}}}}`$ denotes a manipulated attribute vector. This is accomplished by training a conditional generative model $`p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{S}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}} | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}} ;\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}})`$ and substituting $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}`$ with $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\tilde{a}}}}`$ during the sampling process. The first step involves solving the initial value problem for the ordinary differential equation (ODE)
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \label{Eq:transformation_normalizing_flow}
    \begin{aligned}
        \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t_0) =  \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t_1) + \int_{t_1}^{t_0} f(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t), t, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}}) \mathrm{d}t \, ,
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```
with the initial condition $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}} = \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t_1)`$ to obtain $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t_0) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})`$. The function $`f(\cdot)`$ is parameterized as a neural network with parameters $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}}`$ and learned from the dataset $`\mathcal{S} = \left\{ \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_{n} \right\}_{n=1}^{N}`$ by maximizing the log-likelihood
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
            l(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}}) &=  \sum_{n=1}^N \mathrm{log}\, p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{S}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_n|\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_n;\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}})\\ 
        &= \sum_{n=1}^N \mathrm{log}\, p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{Z}}}_0} \left(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}_{n}(t_0) \right) -  \Delta_{\mathrm{log}p}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}}) \, ,
    \end{aligned}
    \label{eq:log_likelihood_cont}
\end{equation}
```
where i.i.d. samples are assumed and
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:second_ode}
    \begin{aligned}
        \Delta_{\mathrm{log}p}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}}) =& \mathrm{log}\, p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{Z}}}_1} \left(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}_{n}(t_1)\right) -  \mathrm{log}\, p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{S}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_n | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_{n};\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}})  \\
        &- \int_{t_1}^{t_0}\mathrm{tr}\left( \frac{\mathrm{d} f(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t), t, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_n, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}})}{\mathrm{d}\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t)} \right) \mathrm{d}t \, ,
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```
which is also an initial value problem with the initial condition $`\mathrm{log}\, p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{Z}}}_1} \left(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}_{n}(t_1)\right) - \mathrm{log}~p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{S}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_n | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_{n};\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}}) =0`$. After training, a new sample $`\tilde{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}}`$ conditioned by a new attribute vector $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\tilde{a}}}}`$ can be generated with
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \tilde{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}} = \tilde{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}}(t_1) =  \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^{t_1} f(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t), t, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\tilde{a}}}}, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}}) \mathrm{d}t \, ,
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```
where $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t_0)`$ can be obtained either by sampling from a standard normal distribution, $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}(t_0) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})`$, or by applying the transformation given in <a href="#Eq:transformation_normalizing_flow" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="Eq:transformation_normalizing_flow">[Eq:transformation_normalizing_flow]</a> to a given sample $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}`$.

# Voice-Editing Supporting Text-to-Speech Synthesis

## Speech Synthesis System

We utilize YourTTS as the speech synthesis system. This model is an adaptation from the VITS model , in which the training objective is the maximization of the <span acronym-label="elbo" acronym-form="singular+short">elbo</span> of the log-likelihood function
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathrm{log}\, p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{X}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}} | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{c}}}; \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\psi}}}) \geq  \mathbb{E}_{q_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{Y}}}}(\mathbf{y}|\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}}; \boldsymbol{\phi})} \left[ \vphantom{\frac{test}{test}} \right.& \mathrm{log}\,p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{X}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}} | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{y}}}; \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\psi}}})   \\ &- \left. \mathrm{log}\frac{q_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{Y}}}}(\mathbf{y}|\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}}; \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\phi}}})}{p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{Y}}}}(\mathbf{y}|\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{c}}}; \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\psi}}})} \right] \, ,   
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```
with the learnable parameters $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\psi}}}`$ and $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\phi}}}`$, latent random variable $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{y}}}`$ and the condition $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{c}}}= \left[ \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{c}}}_{\mathrm{text}}, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{A}}}, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}\right]`$, which is a combination of the text embedding $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{c}}}_{\mathrm{text}}`$, the alignment $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{A}}}`$, and the speaker embedding $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}`$.

We made some modifications to the original architecture proposed in . Specifically, we replaced the stochastic duration predictor with a deterministic one and substituted the speaker encoder with a pre-trained d-vector model . The d-vector model is a ResNet34 model that computes 256-dimensional speaker embeddings from 80-dimensional log-mel spectrograms . <a href="#fig:tts" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig:tts">1</a> shows the overall system during inference, where $`\hat{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}}}`$ is the synthesized speech signal containing the content of $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{c}}}_{\mathrm{text}}`$, the speaker characteristics of the speech signal $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}}`$ up to the modified <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> in $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\tilde{a}}}}`$. We trained the <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> system on LibriTTS-R , an enhanced version of LibriTTS with improved sound quality. The dataset comprises 585 hours of speech data at $`24\,\text{kHz}`$ from 2,456 speakers.

## Flow-Based Parametric Speech Modification

We extract speaker embeddings $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_n`$ from the pre-trained speaker encoder and assume that these are samples from the distribution $`p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{S}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_n | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x_n}}}, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_n;\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}})`$, where $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}}_n`$ is the input utterance and $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_n`$ is the attribute vector containing <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> and the speaking rate. We further assume that $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x_n}}}`$ is implicitly conditioned on $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_n`$, eliminating the need for additional input to the speaker encoder. Our goal is to adjust the attributes $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_n`$ of the speaker embedding $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_n`$, such that the transformed embedding $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_n`$ differs only in the updated conditioning $`\tilde{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}}_n`$. This modification is achieved using a <span acronym-label="ccnf" acronym-form="singular+short">ccnf</span>, as described in <a href="#{Sec:cnf}" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="{Sec:cnf}">[{Sec:cnf}]</a>.

We used seven <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> from the extended Perceptual Voice Qualities Database (PVQD+) . This dataset is an addition to <span acronym-label="pvqd" acronym-form="singular+short">pvqd</span> , where 3 expert clinicians followed the <span acronym-label="cape-v" acronym-form="singular+short">cape-v</span> evaluation protocol to rate the perceived severity of utterances from participants with typical and atypical voices. This score starts at 0 which means no severity (healthy) and goes up to 100 which means full severity (dysarthric). The rated <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> are *strain*, *roughness*, *breathiness*, *pitch*, and *loudness*, with the authors of PVQD+ adding labels for *weight* and *resonance*. This dataset consists of 296 annotated audio files including atypical voices. To overcome the limitation of the small dataset, we conducted a pseudo labeling of these <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> on the LibriTTS-R dataset. For the labeling, we followed and trained random forest regressors for each <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span> in PVQD+ and used the same train test splits. Random forests were fitted on time-averaged hidden representations of the 6th layer of a HuBERT Large  model, these features proved the best predictors of speaker identity , and achieved an average test RMSE of $`11.28`$ which is in line with . The RMSE for each <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span> is shown in <a href="#tab:rmse_pvq" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="tab:rmse_pvq">1</a>. These fitted regressors were used to pseudo-label the LibriTTS-R dataset. For the full training data (PVQD and pseudo-labeled LibriTTS-R), we observed that the range of non-gendered <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> is mostly distributed in the first half of the CAPE-V scale. The severity values for the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> are distributed with $`20\textcolor{gray}{\,\pm\,17}`$ for *roughness*, $`19\textcolor{gray}{\,\pm\,20}`$ for *breathiness*. We attribute the limited amount of observed atypical voices to this data skew. Despite this bias, the model was able to synthesize <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> with sufficient intensities (see <a href="#results" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="results">4</a>). For *weight* and *resonance*, we get prediction scores of 54$`\textcolor{gray}{\,\pm\,18}`$ and 45$`\textcolor{gray}{\,\pm\,15}`$, respectively.

We observed a change in speaking rate after manipulation when it wasn’t included in the attribute vector. Therefore, we calculated the speaking rate by extracting phonemes and their durations using the <span acronym-label="mfa" acronym-form="singular+short">mfa</span> , then dividing the number of phonemes by the total duration of the voiced segments.

The <span acronym-label="ccnf" acronym-form="singular+short">ccnf</span> was trained similarly to the methods proposed in , by maximizing the log-likelihood of $`p_{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{S}}}}(\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}_n | \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}}_n, \ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_{n};\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\theta}}})`$ as explained in <a href="#Sec:cnf" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="Sec:cnf">2</a>. We trained a single <span acronym-label="ccnf" acronym-form="singular+short">ccnf</span> for all seven <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> and the speaking rate on the training set of LibriTTS-R and the complete <span acronym-label="pvqd" acronym-form="singular+short">pvqd</span>. All values in the attribute vector are normalized to the interval $`[0,1]`$ before being conditioned to the normalizing flow. For the speaking rate, the maximum speaking rate of the train set is used as a reference. The <span acronym-label="ode" acronym-form="singular+short">ode</span> solver from was employed with default tolerance parameters, and the trace was estimated using Hutchinson’s trace estimator as proposed in . The function $`f(\cdot)`$, see <a href="#Eq:transformation_normalizing_flow" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="Eq:transformation_normalizing_flow">[Eq:transformation_normalizing_flow]</a>, was modeled with one <span acronym-label="ccnf" acronym-form="singular+short">ccnf</span> block as introduced in , with a hidden size of $`512`$. We observed overfitting when using a larger size. Training was conducted with a batch size of 200 and an initial learning rate of $`10^{-4}`$. The learning rate was updated every 100 epochs by multiplying it with a decay factor of $`\gamma = 0.98`$. After training, manipulation was performed as illustrated in <a href="#fig:ode_manipulation_block" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig:ode_manipulation_block">2</a>. The speaker embedding was first transformed to $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{z}}}_0`$ using the speaker attributes $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}_n`$ and then back using the desired attributes $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{\tilde{a}}}}_{n}`$. The modified embedding was then used to synthesize the speech signal $`\hat{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}}}`$ via the <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> system, see <a href="#fig:tts" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="fig:tts">1</a>.

# Results and Discussions

All modifications are performed by first extracting the attribute vector $`\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{a}}}`$ of an utterance with the trained regressors and then manipulating a single <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span> along its axis while keeping the others fixed. We evaluated this modification process with four <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>: *Breathiness*, *roughness*, *resonance* and *weight*. Speaker embeddings were sampled and conditioned on varying strength levels: 0, 50, and 100, which we interpret as low, medium, and high. Due to the limitations of extreme cases of *roughness* in the training data, the model struggled to map modifications across the full strength spectrum accurately. As a result, for this <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span>, the modification range was adjusted to 0, 100, and 200.

Objective evaluation included <span acronym-label="eer" acronym-form="singular+short">eer</span> to measure speaker similarity and the random forest regressors used for pseudo-labeling to evaluate the severity of the modified <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. We evaluated 200 seen male and female speakers from the training set and 39 (20 male, 19 female) unseen speakers from the test set of LibriTTS-R, using up to 10 utterances per speaker. Note that the dataset lacks extreme speakers, limiting the non-gendered <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span> manipulations from low to high. The <span acronym-label="eer" acronym-form="singular+short">eer</span> was calculated by extracting speaker embedding $`\hat{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{s}}}}`$ from the synthesized utterance $`\hat{\ensuremath{\boldsymbol{\mathbf{x}}}}`$ using the same speaker encoder employed in the <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> system. Speaker embeddings were also extracted from the training set, and positive and negative pairs were constructed. Cosine similarity was used to measure the similarity between embeddings. When a manipulation was applied, the positive pair consisted of the manipulated embedding and its unmanipulated counterpart.

To evaluate the perceived change in voice quality, a subjective listening test was conducted. 15 raters were asked to evaluate 480 samples in total on a digital version of the 100-point scales employed in . The presented scales were labeled *roughness* and *breathiness*, respectively. All participants were phonetic experts and recruited by word of mouth. Each participant was presented with the manipulated samples in 3 different modification levels for *roughness* and *breathiness* as described above, plus an original recording of the speaker, shuffled in random order. Each participant rated 32 samples, synthesized from 4 different speakers, stratified by gender and whether the speaker was seen in the training set. Sample lengths ranged from $`5 - 29\,\mathrm{s}`$, with an average of $`11\,\mathrm{s}`$. In order to ensure that the manipulation of the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> did not adversely affect the synthesis quality, the raters were additionally asked to rate the overall perceived quality of each sample using <span acronym-label="mos" acronym-form="singular+short">mos</span>, with the standard ITU-R scale ranging from *Bad* to *Excellent* . As a final task, they were also given a speaker similarity test using <span acronym-label="smos" acronym-form="singular+short">smos</span> with a random reference from the original speaker to examine whether the change in voice quality distorts the speaker perception. The results for objective and subjective evaluation are given in <a href="#tab:eer_pvq" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="tab:eer_pvq">[tab:eer_pvq]</a> and <a href="#tab:results_perceived_pvq" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="tab:results_perceived_pvq">[tab:results_perceived_pvq]</a>, respectively.

## Manipulating Voice Quality Intensity

The upper halves of the tables show the predicted and perceived <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="singular+short">pvq</span> scores. While all values increase with increasing conditioning, the scores predicted by the random forest regressors are confined to a much smaller interval than the expert ratings. We attribute the score skew in PVQD on which the regressors were trained to this discrepancy, demanding awareness when relying on regression models to judge voice quality. For the perceived ratings, a paired Wilcoxon rank sum test was conducted between all conditions to confirm the statistical significance of the distributional shift. In the *breathiness* condition, all changes between manipulation levels proved to be significant ($`p < 0.005`$), except for the difference from the original recording to low manipulation, which constitutes pure re-synthesis as all manipulations are done on typical voices, i.e., low severity of the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. In the *roughness* condition, only the perceived changes from all levels to high modification proved statistically significant ($`p < 0.005`$).

Looking at the expert ratings, we observe that, indeed, an aggressive roughness manipulation was necessary to get a high score. Simultaneously, perceived breathiness increases when increasing roughness, while manipulation of breathiness has a mixed effect on perceived roughness. A recent study on the effect between rough and breathy voice qualities  found that increased roughness has a stronger effect on perceived breathiness than increased breathiness on perceived roughness. Still, the effect in both directions is small. Rather than being an artifact of entanglement in the normalizing flow, this effect seems to happen naturally for these two voice qualities. The slight downward shift in <span acronym-label="mos" acronym-form="singular+short">mos</span> did not prove to be statistically significant, except from the original reference to high manipulation in both *roughness* and *breathiness* conditions ($`p < 0.005`$).

## Effect on Speaker Similarity

The lower halves of the tables present speaker similarity findings after manipulation. As the severity of the <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> increases, the <span acronym-label="eer" acronym-form="singular+short">eer</span> also rises, suggesting that speaker identity preservation becomes more challenging. This is expected since modern speaker embedding models are optimized to distinguish small perceptual nuances between different voices. A similar trend can be observed for the SMOS. Here, we have significant degradation in speaker identification between all levels, except for the original reference and low manipulation ($`p < 0.005`$). Remember that manipulations were performed on LibriTTS-R, so all speakers have typical voices characterized by low *roughness* and *breathiness* values. Modifications with lower levels of these <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> result in a lower <span acronym-label="eer" acronym-form="singular+short">eer</span>, as the synthesized voices remain more similar to the original speaker. The findings could point to the notion that voice quality is an integral part of the speaker’s identity and cannot be perceived separately. We will leave it to future work to verify this.

## Manipulation of Gendered <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>

Lastly, we evaluated the modification of the two gendered <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> *resonance* and *weight*. The objective evaluation results are shown in <a href="#tab:eer_pvq" data-reference-type="ref+Label" data-reference="tab:eer_pvq">[tab:eer_pvq]</a>. Given that the expert ratings for these <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> were distributed more uniformly across the full scale , we expect the regression scores to be more reliable. Again, we observe a clear increasing trend with increasing conditioning, supporting our approach’s success in not only manipulating gendered (as in ) but also non-gendered <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. The impact we observe on speaker similarity is much greater than for the non-gendered <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. Note that *weight* should be high for healthy male voices and low for healthy female voices , and vice versa for *resonance*. Strong manipulation of these values alters perceived gender, spoofing the <span acronym-label="eer" acronym-form="singular+short">eer</span> to a greater extent. This also explains the lower <span acronym-label="eer" acronym-form="singular+short">eer</span> for medium manipulations.

# Conclusion

The experiments demonstrated that a <span acronym-label="tts" acronym-form="singular+short">tts</span> approach based on Conditional Continuous Normalizing Flows effectively manipulates <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span>. Despite being trained on only a limited set of atypical voices, the model showed solid performance in modifying four <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> for both seen and unseen voices. Notably, changes in two of these <span acronym-label="pvq" acronym-form="plural+short">pvqs</span> were convincing to phonetic experts. We hypothesize that a tool that can apply those modifications to a voice can be used by speech pathologists in teaching new pathologists. By generating speech samples with specific perceptual properties selectively altered, these tools can assist in illustrating or replicating particular perceptual voice categories. Our goal is to verify this hypothesis in future work.
