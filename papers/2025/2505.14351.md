###### Abstract

Tibetan is a low-resource language with minimal parallel speech corpora spanning its three major dialects—Ü-Tsang, Amdo, and Kham—limiting progress in speech modeling. To address this issue, we propose FMSD-TTS, a few-shot, multi-speaker, multi-dialect text-to-speech framework that synthesizes parallel dialectal speech from limited reference audio and explicit dialect labels. Our method features a novel speaker-dialect fusion module and a Dialect-Specialized Dynamic Routing Network (DSDR-Net) to capture fine-grained acoustic and linguistic variations across dialects while preserving speaker identity. Extensive objective and subjective evaluations demonstrate that FMSD-TTS significantly outperforms baselines in both dialectal expressiveness and speaker similarity. We further validate the quality and utility of the synthesized speech through a challenging speech-to-speech dialect conversion task. Our contributions include: (1) a novel few-shot TTS system tailored for Tibetan multi-dialect speech synthesis, (2) the public release of a large-scale synthetic Tibetan speech corpus generated by FMSD-TTS, and (3) an open-source evaluation toolkit for standardized assessment of speaker similarity, dialect consistency, and audio quality.

## 1 Introduction

In the field of Tibetan speech processing, particularly in tasks such as automatic speech recognition (ASR), speech translation (ST), and speech-to-speech dialect conversion (S2SDC), the availability of large-scale, high-quality, paralleled datasets covering the three major Tibetan dialects is crucial. Currently, the most widely used Tibetan multi-dialect dataset is provided by Zhuoma and Zhu (2022). However, it contains only around 900 pairs of parallel speech samples, which significantly limits the performance and advancement of Tibetan speech processing tasks. Moreover, most existing Tibetan speech datasets still rely on manual recording and processing, which is both time-consuming and uncontrollable. To address this challenge, a text-to-speech (TTS)-based approach for synthesizing multi-dialect datasets is urgently needed.

To address this data scarcity, TTS technology offers a promising avenue for dataset synthesis. While recent years have witnessed significant advancements in TTS, achieving highly natural and speaker-similar speech for single speakers Ren et al. (2020); J. Kim (2021); Shen et al. (2023) and even diverse voices in multi-speaker TTS systems Chen et al. (2021); Huang et al. (2023); Chen and Garner (2023), effectively applying these technologies to synthesize multiple distinct dialects within a low-resource language like Tibetan remains a formidable challenge. Key difficulties include faithfully capturing subtle phonetic differences between dialects, maintaining speaker identity across dialects, and achieving robust performance with limited training data.

In the field of Tibetan multi-dialect TTS, research remains limited. Xu et al. (2021) proposed a multi-task network to simultaneously generate both Amdo and Ü-Tsang dialects. The model first generates a mel-spectrogram and then utilizes different vocoders to synthesize speech in the corresponding dialects. However, this approach is relatively heavy, requiring multiple vocoders, and may struggle to generate speech with distinct dialectal characteristics, as the dialect representation is injected too late in the process.

To this end, we propose FMSD-TTS, a few-shot model that integrates speaker and dialect representations into the TTS model. Speaker representations are extracted from reference speech using ECAPA-TDNN Desplanques et al. (2020), while dialect representations are obtained through an embedding layer. The two types of representations are then fused using a linear layer. To enable precise control over dialectal characteristics and effectively capture the nuanced phonetic distinctions among various dialects, we design a dialect-specialized dynamic routing network (DSDR-Net) to replace the conventional Feedforward Network (FFN) in the Transformer architecture. This network integrates a conditional computation mechanism within the FFN layer, dynamically routing the information flow to a dialect-specific sub-network based on the input dialect ID. This mechanism effectively assigns a distinct set of expert parameters to each dialect, allowing the model to learn and represent unique acoustic features such as rhythm and intonation. Compared to traditional shared-parameter networks, this approach offers finer-grained control over dialect-specific feature modeling. In summary, our primary contributions are:

- •
  We propose FMSD-TTS, a novel Tibetan few-shot, multi-speaker, multi-dialect TTS system incorporating a DSDR-Net. FMSD-TTS significantly outperforms strong baselines in dialect consistency and speaker similarity, effectively capturing fine-grained acoustic and linguistic variations across Tibetan dialects.
- •
  We construct and publicly release a large-scale Tibetan multi-speaker, multi-dialect speech dataset generated via FMSD-TTS. The high quality and practical utility of this dataset are demonstrated through its successful application to a demanding S2SDC task, providing a valuable new resource for the community.
- •
  We develop and release a comprehensive evaluation toolkit for Tibetan dialect speech synthesis, facilitating standardized assessment of audio quality, speaker similarity, and dialect similarity.

## 2 Related works

### 2.1 Text-to-Speech

Modern Text-to-Speech (TTS) systems have evolved through various architectural paradigms. End-to-end models like VITS J. Kim (2021); Kong et al. (2023), which utilize conditional variational autoencoders (cVAEs) (Sohn et al., 2015) and normalizing flows Papamakarios et al. (2021), achieve high-quality synthesis but can face training stability issues and inference latency due to their complexity and flow-based components. Other end-to-end trends include diffusion models (e.g., NaturalSpeech Tan et al. (2024); Shen et al. (2023)) known for excellent quality but often slower inference, and alignment-free models (e.g., F5TTS Chen et al. (2024)) that simplify pipelines.

Semi-end-to-end approaches, which first generate an intermediate representation (e.g., mel-spectrogram) then synthesize audio using a vocoder, often offer better stability and control. Foundational works include Tacotron2 Shen et al. (2018) and FastSpeech2 Ren et al. (2020) with explicit duration prediction. More recently, Matcha-TTS Mehta et al. (2024) has shown strong performance using explicit duration modeling and OT-CFM (Peebles and Xie, 2023) for mel-spectrogram refinement. The robustness and modularity of such structured approaches are particularly beneficial for low-resource and multi-dialect scenarios. FMSD-TTS draws upon a Transformer-based architecture, aiming for this stability while incorporating specialized mechanisms for precise speaker and dialect control, differing from fully end-to-end flow or diffusion-based systems.

### 2.2 Multi-Speaker TTS and Speaker Representation

A common and effective strategy involves leveraging pre-trained speaker verification networks to extract speaker embeddings, which then condition the TTS model. Widely used speaker encoders include X-Vector Snyder et al. (2018), ECAPA-TDNN Desplanques et al. (2020), and CAM++ Wang et al. (2023). These encoders are often employed in one-shot, few-shot, or zero-shot multi-speaker TTS (ZSM-TTS) scenarios due to their strong generalization capabilities from short reference audio. For instance, AdaSpeech Chen et al. (2021) modeled acoustic features at speaker, utterance, and phoneme levels, while AdaSpeech2 Yan et al. (2021) introduced a self-adaptation procedure for speaker characteristics. AdaSpeech4 Wu et al. (2021) further refined this by using conditional layer normalization for speaker representations. Other ZSM-TTS approaches include Chen and Garner (2023), who introduced a diffusion transformer (DiT) for adaptive modeling.

The challenge intensifies when needing to disentangle and control both speaker and dialect information simultaneously, especially in few-shot settings. FMSD-TTS addresses this by using a pre-trained speaker encoder for robust speaker representation and introducing dedicated dialect embeddings, which are then fused and processed by the novel DSDR-Net to ensure distinct dialectal characteristic modeling.

### 2.3 Tibetan Multi-Dialect TTS

Research specifically targeting Tibetan multi-dialect TTS is still in its early stages, largely due to data scarcity and linguistic complexities. The primary related work is by Xu et al. (2021), who proposed a model generating a common mel-spectrogram subsequently processed by different WaveNet-based v. d. Oord et al. (2016) vocoders for Amdo and Ü-Tsang dialects. Key limitations of this approach include the complexity of multiple heavy vocoders and the late-stage injection of dialect information, which may not adequately capture distinct core dialectal features. FMSD-TTS aims to overcome these issues by integrating dialect representation earlier within a unified acoustic model and utilizing a single vocoder, with the DSDR-Net designed for finer-grained dialect feature modeling.

## 3 Methods

Our work introduces FMSD-TTS, a few-shot multi-speaker multi-dialect TTS model based on Matcha-TTSMehta et al. (2024) for Tibetan dialects dataset generation. We designed this system to overcome the limitations of the previous approach Xu et al. (2021), such as the reliance on a multi-output structure and the need for training vocoders for different dialects. The key innovations of our model and their roles are as follows:

- •
  Speaker-dialect fusion module: Integrates speaker and dialect representations into both the text encoder and the flow prediction network to enhance dialect-aware speech generation.
- •
  DSDR-Net: Incorporates a conditional computation mechanism within the Transformer’s FFN layer to enable precise control over dialectal characteristics and effectively capture nuanced phonetic distinctions across dialects.
- •
  Reference loss: Aims to disentangle speaker representation from dialectal information, ensuring the purity of speaker embeddings.

### 3.1 Overview

Fig. 1(a) illustrates the overall architecture of FMSD-TTS. The process begins with tokenization, which transforms Tibetan characters into a long-format tensor representation. A reference encoder and a dialect embedding module then extract embeddings from the mel-spectrogram of a reference audio sample and the provided dialect ID, respectively. Subsequently, the tokenized text is fed into a text encoder, where speaker and dialect embeddings are integrated through a speaker-dialect fusion module, conditioning the linguistic representations on both speaker identity and dialectal characteristics. The duration predictor estimates the duration of each phoneme based on the hidden representations produced by the text encoder. The predicted durations, expressed as frames per phoneme, are then used to guide the upsampling process. Following upsampling, the flow-prediction network, combined with speaker and dialect representations, synthesizes a high-quality mel-spectrogram from the upsampled features. Finally, a pretrained vocoder converts the generated mel-spectrogram into a raw waveform, thereby completing the multi-speaker, multi-dialect TTS synthesis process.

### 3.2 Speaker-Dialect Fusion Module

The speaker-dialect fusion module extracts speaker embeddings from reference audio and dialect features from dialect ID, then integrates them with the text’s hidden representations. Fig. 1(b) shows the structure of the speaker-dialect fusion module, which consists of three primary components: the speaker embedding extraction process, dialect embedding extraction process, and fusion process.

The speaker processing begins with random cropping of reference audio $`w`$ to 3-second segments for subsequent feature extraction, to improve the robustness of speaker embedding extraction.

```math
w_{\mathrm{crop}}=w_{\tau:\tau+T}
```

where $`\tau`$ is a random variable sampled from $`\tau\sim Uniform(0,||w||_{1}-T)`$, $`w_{\mathrm{crop}}`$ denotes the cropped audio segment. We extract speaker embeddings $`h_{\mathrm{spk}}`$ through a reference encoder $`Enc_{\mathrm{ref}}`$ with subsequent normalization:

```math
h_{\mathrm{spk}}=\text{Norm}(\text{Enc}_{\mathrm{ref}}(w_{\mathrm{crop}}))
```

where $`\text{Norm}=\frac{x}{||x||_{2}}`$ denotes L2 normalization.

In the dialect embedding extraction process, the first step involves mapping each dialect $`d`$ to a dialect ID $`did`$.

```math
\text{did}=f_{\text{map}}(d)=\begin{cases}0,&\text{if }d=\text{`wz'}\\
1,&\text{if }d=\text{`ad'}\\
2,&\text{if }d=\text{`kb'}\end{cases}
```

Subsequently, the embedding layer applies L2 normalization to generate the dialect embedding.

```math
h_{\mathrm{did}}=\text{Norm}(\text{Emb}_{\mathrm{did}}(did))
```

In the fusion process, the speaker and dialect representations are concatenated into a unified feature, which is then processed by a fully connected network. The resulting fused feature is added to the text hidden feature $`h_{text}`$ to inject style information.

```math
\hat{h}_{\mathrm{text}}=h_{\mathrm{text}}+\text{Linear}(h_{\mathrm{spk}}\|h_{\mathrm{did}})
```

### 3.3 DSDR-Net

The DSDR-Net integrates a conditional computation mechanism within the FFN layer, dynamically routing the information flow to a dialect-specific sub-network based on the input dialect identifier. This mechanism effectively assigns a distinct set of expert parameters to each dialect, allowing the model to learn and represent unique acoustic features such as rhythm and intonation. Compared to traditional shared-parameter networks, this approach offers finer-grained control over dialect-specific feature modeling. As shown in Fig. 1(c), the DSDR-Net consists of two types of FFNs: a public FFN shared across all dialects and private FFNs specialized for specific dialectal styles.

Firstly, the hidden feature of the text $`h_{text}`$ is processed with a multi-head self-attention:

```math
h_{\text{attn}}=\text{Softmax}\left(\frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_{k}}}\right)V
```

where $`Q,K,V`$ are linear projections of $`h_{text}`$, and $`d_{k}`$ is the key dimension. Then, the module automatically selects the most appropriate processing path based on the input dialect ID:

```math
\text{FFN}_{\mathrm{private}}=\{\text{FFN}_{0},\text{FFN}_{1},\text{FFN}_{2}\}
```

```math
\text{FFN}_{\mathrm{private,d}}=\text{FFN}_{\mathrm{private}}[d]
```

Finally, the hidden state $`h_{\text{attn}}`$ is calculated by applying both the public FFN and the selected private FFN, and the outputs from the two parts are summed together:

```math
\hat{h}_{\text{attn}}=\text{FFN}_{\mathrm{public}}(h_{\text{attn}})+\text{FFN}_{\mathrm{private,d}}(h_{\text{attn}})
```

### 3.4 Reference Loss

Reference loss is designed to disentangle speaker representation from dialectal information, ensuring the purity of speaker embeddings. Cosine similarity is chosen to describe the relationship between speaker embedding and dialect embedding. The function of reference loss $`loss_{\text{ref}}`$ can be represented as:

```math
loss_{\text{ref}}=\frac{h_{spk}\cdot h_{did}}{||h_{spk}||\cdot||h_{did}||}
```

## 4 Experiments

### 4.1 Dataset and Evaluation

Dataset We compiled a multi-dialect Tibetan speech dataset containing over 210 hours of recordings, including 44 hours of Ü-Tsang, 45 hours of Kham, and 90 hours of Amdo speech, collected from more than 1,500 native speakers. The selected audio clips range from 2 to 20 seconds in length. For the training set, we sampled 40,000 recordings per dialect, resulting in a total of 120,000 samples. The validation and test sets each consist of 300 samples per dialect, totaling 900 samples. More detailed information is provided in Appendix A.

Setting In the main experiments, the audio was sampled at 16,000 Hz and converted into 80-bin Mel-spectrograms using a short-time Fourier transform (STFT) with a window size of 1,024 and a hop size of 256. During FMSD-TTS training, the learning rate was set to $`1\times 10^{-4}`$, and the batch size was set to 16 per GPU. We used the Adam optimizer Kingma and Ba (2017) with weight decay to improve training stability and enhance generalization. The weight decay coefficient was set to $`1\times 10^{-2}`$ to mitigate overfitting. The model was trained for 500,000 steps.

For vocoder training, we utilized random segments of the mel-spectrogram with a segment size of 32 frames. The batch size was set to 12 per GPU, and the number of training steps was set to 100,000. The initial learning rate was set to $`1\times 10^{-4}`$. We adopted the AdamW optimizer Loshchilov and Hutter (2017) with $`\beta_{1}=0.8`$ and $`\beta_{2}=0.99`$ to control the moving averages of the gradients and the squared gradients, respectively. A learning rate decay factor of 0.9999996 was applied to gradually reduce the learning rate during training, promoting better convergence and improving generalization performance. The vocoder was trained based on the official BigVGAN implementation gil Lee et al. (2023).

All experiments were conducted on a machine equipped with an AMD Ryzen 9 5900X 12-Core Processor and two NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs.

Evaluation

1.  1.  Subjective Metrics: We conducted subjective evaluations using mean opinion scores (MOS) to assess naturalness (nMOS) and speaker similarity (sMOS) on a 9-point scale ranging from 1 to 5 with 0.5-point increments. The results are reported with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). Additionally, we propose a novel evaluation metric called dialect mean opinion classification (dMOC), where listeners identify the dialect class of audio samples. The performance of this metric is quantified by computing the F1 score between the predicted classifications and the ground truth labels.
2.  2.  Objective Metrics: For our objective evaluation, we adopt a comprehensive set of metrics, including dialect classification accuracy (DCA), dialect embedding cosine similarity (DECS), and speaker embedding cosine similarity (SECS). To evaluate SECS, we extract speaker embeddings from both the reference and synthesized audio using a SimAM-ResNet100 model pre-trained on the VoxCeleb2 dataset Lin et al. (2024a), and calculate the cosine similarity between them. This similarity ranges from -1 to 1, with higher values indicating greater similarity. We compute the similarity only for samples with the same Tibetan text, and average the results across all speakers. Dialect classification of the synthesized speech is performed using a pre-trained speech dialect recognition (SDR) model. To assess the dialectal characteristics of synthesized speech, we introduce the DECS metric, which is computed as the cosine similarity between the dialect embedding of the generated audio and the average dialect embedding of real speech from that dialect. The dialect embeddings are obtained using a speech dialect embedding (SDE) model trained via contrastive learning, inspired by the CLAP framework Cai et al. (2025). For computational efficiency, we also measure the real-time factor (RTF) of inference time. Further training details of the SDR and SDE models are provided in Appendix B.

### 4.2 Model Settings

Proposed Models FMSD-TTS is built upon the official implementation of Matcha-TTS Mehta et al. (2024). A vocabulary file containing 216 characters was constructed. The hidden dimension of the speaker embedding is set to 192, while that of the dialect embedding is set to 128. The output dimension of the fusion module is also set to 128. The dimensions of FFN in the DSDR-Net are set to 192. ECAPA-TDNN (Desplanques et al., 2020) is used as the reference encoder, pre-trained on the VoxBlink2 dataset (Lin et al., 2024b). Fig. 2 presents a t-SNE visualization of the features extracted by ECAPA-TDNN, demonstrating its strong out-of-domain generalization ability and its capacity to encode speaker identity independently of dialectal variation.

Baseline Models To date, Xu et al. (2021) remains the only work addressing multi-dialect Tibetan TTS. However, their model is based on an outdated LSTM architecture and lacks explicit duration modeling. To establish a stronger baseline, we built a model based on the SC-CNN Yoon et al. (2023) and VITS2 Kong et al. (2023) architectures. Our baseline also retains key elements from their work, including the use of multiple vocoders for audio generation and the conversion of Tibetan text using the Wylie transliteration scheme.

### 4.3 Main Result

To evaluate the performance of the proposed model, we conducted both objective and subjective evaluations in comparison with several baseline models. The results are presented in Table 1. As indicated by the nMOS scores, our method generates speech with higher naturalness than the competing approaches. In terms of speaker similarity, our model achieved the highest scores on both sMOS and SECS, demonstrating its effectiveness in preserving speaker identity.

Moreover, our approach outperforms other models on perceptual and objective quality metrics, as evidenced by the results of dMOC, DCA, and DECS. We also assessed the computational efficiency of each model using Real-Time Factor (RTF). Although our method attains an RTF of 0.38—which is not the lowest among all models—it is still well below the real-time threshold ($`RTF<1`$), thereby supporting real-time speech generation and offering a favorable trade-off between quality and efficiency.

### 5.5 Performance of Synthetic Multi-Dialect Dataset

To generate a high-quality parallel multi-dialect dataset, we trained the FMSD-TTS model using approximately 200 hours of speech data per dialect. Additionally, we expanded the model’s hidden channel size to 384 to enhance its capacity for representing complex speaker and dialectal variations.

Based on the trained model, we constructed a parallel multi-dialect dataset consisting of 2,700 pairs of dialectal speech samples. To evaluate the quality of the generated dataset, we conducted a speech-to-speech dialect conversion (S2SDC) task. For this task, we adopted DurFlex-EVC (Oh et al., 2025), an emotional voice conversion model, as the core conversion framework, and utilized BigVGAN (gil Lee et al., 2023) as the vocoder to synthesize the final waveform outputs.

These results suggest that both the conversion model and vocoder perform reasonably well on the synthesized dataset, and that higher sampling rates contribute to better audio quality in S2SDC tasks.

## 6 Conclusion

In this work, we proposed FMSD-TTS, a few-shot text-to-speech model that integrates both speaker and dialect representations for Tibetan multi-speaker, multi-dialect speech synthesis. By leveraging ECAPA-TDNN for speaker embedding and a learned embedding for dialect identity, we designed a unified representation fusion strategy and introduced a Dialect-Specialized Dynamic Routing Network (DSDR-Net) to better control dialectal variation. Based on this model, we constructed and released a large-scale, parallel Tibetan multi-dialect speech dataset, enabling broader research in Tibetan speech synthesis and conversion. To evaluate the quality and utility of the generated speech, we explored the speech-to-speech dialect conversion (S2SDC) task and conducted subjective assessments including naturalness, speaker similarity, and dialect consistency. Additionally, we built a comprehensive evaluation toolkit tailored to Tibetan speech generation tasks. We believe our work offers valuable resources and methodological insights for future research in low-resource, multi-dialect TTS and dialect conversion.

## 7 Limitations

Despite the promising results, our work still has several limitations. The proposed method is evaluated solely on non-autoregressive TTS models, and its performance on autoregressive architectures such as Tacotron or VITS remains unexplored. In addition, the speaker encoder employed in our pipeline is pre-trained on non-Tibetan data without fine-tuning, primarily due to the lack of speaker-labeled Tibetan corpora. These limitations may affect the generalizability of our model and lead to suboptimal speaker consistency. Furthermore, our investigation into dialect conversion is still preliminary. Although we adopt DurFlex-EVC for the speech-to-speech dialect conversion (S2SDC) task, we did not perform comparative experiments with other voice conversion models, largely due to the lack of established baselines or prior studies on Tibetan dialect conversion. We plan to address these challenges in future work by exploring autoregressive models, collecting speaker-annotated Tibetan data, and conducting more comprehensive evaluations on dialect conversion.
