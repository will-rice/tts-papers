# Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models

###### Abstract

Spoken Language Models (SLMs) aim to learn linguistic competence directly from speech using discrete units, widening access to Natural Language Processing (NLP) technologies for languages with limited written resources. However, progress has been largely English-centric due to scarce spoken evaluation benchmarks and training data, making cross-lingual learning difficult. We present a cross-lingual interleaving method that mixes speech tokens across languages without textual supervision. We also release an EN–FR training dataset, _TinyStories_ ($`\sim\!42`$k hours), together with EN–FR spoken _StoryCloze_ and _TopicCloze_ benchmarks for cross-lingual semantic evaluation, both synthetically generated using GPT-4. On $`360`$M and $`1`$B SLMs under matched training-token budgets, interleaving improves monolingual semantic accuracy, enables robust cross-lingual continuation, and strengthens cross-lingual hidden-state alignment. Taken together, these results indicate that cross-lingual interleaving is a simple, scalable route to building multilingual SLMs that understand and converse across languages. All resources are made open-source to support reproducibility¹¹1HuggingFace Datasets Available at: [Adel-Moumen/Spoken_TinyStories](https://huggingface.co/datasets/Adel-Moumen/Spoken_TinyStories).

Index Terms—  Spoken language models, cross-lingual learning, interleaving, multilinguality

## 1 Introduction

Spoken Language Models (SLMs) aim to learn language directly from speech, without relying on textual supervision. This line of work emerged from _‘textless’ NLP_ \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib20 "On Generative Spoken Language Modeling from Raw Audio")\], which combines discrete speech representations \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib17 "Discrete Audio Tokens: More Than a Survey!")\] and neural language modelling \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib39 "Language models are few-shot learners"), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib2 "Improving Language Understanding by Generative Pre-Training")\] to capture both acoustic and linguistic regularities from audio alone. Within this framework, _Generative Spoken Language Modelling_ (GSLM) formalises the objective by training an autoregressive LM on sequences of discrete speech units and reconstructing waveforms with a unit vocoder, with the motivation of broadening access to NLP in settings where written resources are scarce.

Despite rapid progress, the _cross-lingual_ setting, which aims to train a single SLM over multiple languages, remains comparatively under-explored \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib6 "SpiRit-LM: interleaved spoken and written language model"), [36](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib4 "Scaling speech-text pre-training with synthetic interleaved data"), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib8 "Scheduled Interleaved Speech-Text Training for Speech-to-Speech Translation with LLMs")\]. Two practical bottlenecks persist: (i) evaluations are predominantly English-centric, hindering measurement of cross-lingual semantic competence; and (ii) training methods that encourage sharing across languages often depend on _text_, for example via speech–text _interleaving_ in Text–Speech LMs (TSLMs) \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib5 "”Toward Joint Language Modeling for Speech Units and Text”"), [25](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib6 "SpiRit-LM: interleaved spoken and written language model"), [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib26 "Text-speech language models with improved cross-modal transfer by aligning abstraction levels"), [35](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib29 "Scaling speech-text pre-training with synthetic interleaved data")\], which is not directly compatible with _‘textless’_ SLMs. As a result, the original promise of _‘textless’_ NLP learning from audio alone at scale and across languages has not yet been fully realised.

We address these limitations by introducing a _cross-lingual interleaving_ scheme that mixes speech tokens from different languages within the same training sequence, using sentence-level alignments but no text tokens. Exposure to interleaved multilingual contexts encourages a shared representational subspace and promotes transfer, while remaining compatible with pure _‘textless’_ pipelines. To make this practical and measurable, we curate bilingual, sentence-aligned spoken resources and new spoken semantic benchmarks. Our contributions are as follows:

1.  (i)
    We propose a cross-lingual interleaving strategy that alternates speech segments from different languages within a single sequence, promoting a shared representational subspace.
2.  (ii)
    We release a French–English, sentence-aligned spoken corpus derived from *TinyStories* \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib32 "Tinystories: how small can language models be and still speak coherent english?")\] (approximately $`42`$k hours in total) together with bilingual spoken _StoryCloze_ and _TopicCloze_ benchmarks \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib35 "A corpus and cloze evaluation for deeper understanding of commonsense stories")\] for semantic evaluation synthetically generated using GPT-4 \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib40 "GPT-4 technical report")\].
3.  (iii)
    We show on $`360`$M and $`1`$B parameter SLMs that the method yields _positive transfer_ to monolingual semantic tasks and strong _cross-lingual_ performance, under a matched training-token budget. Additionally, representation analyses indicate stronger cross-lingual alignment in the hidden states.

Section [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S2 "2 Related Work ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models") situates our approach with respect to SLMs, speech–text interleaving, and multilingual training. Section [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S3 "3 Spoken Language Modelling ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models") formalises the modelling setup and the cross-lingual interleaving scheme; Sections [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S4 "4 Cross-Lingual Spoken Datasets ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models")–[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S6 "6 Experimental Results ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models") present datasets, experiments, and analyses.

## 2 Related Work

### 2.1 Speech Language Models

Early SLMs instantiate the _‘textless’_ pipeline by modelling discrete speech units and decoding back to waveforms. GSLM \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib20 "On Generative Spoken Language Modeling from Raw Audio")\] trains a decoder-only LM over units and synthesises speech with a unit-based vocoder; pGSLM \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib21 "Text-free prosody-aware generative spoken language modeling")\] augments units with prosodic tokens to improve expressiveness; and dGSLM \[[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib22 "Generative spoken dialogue language modeling")\] introduces a dual-tower variant for two-channel dialogues. While these systems can produce locally fluent speech, long-form coherence is challenging. SpeechSSM \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib23 "Long-Form Speech Generation with Spoken Language Models")\] explores state-space models \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib42 "Efficiently modeling long sequences with structured state spaces")\] for long-form generation, TWIST \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib24 "Textually Pretrained Speech Language Models")\] shows that initialising from a pre-trained text Large Language Model (LLM) can boost semantics, and AudioLM \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib25 "AudioLM: a Language Modeling Approach to Audio Generation")\] cascades semantic and acoustic token streams. More recently, Slamming \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib27 "Slamming: training a speech language model on one GPU in a day")\] provides a competitive compute-efficient recipe, and Align-SLM \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib28 "Align-SLM: textless spoken language models with reinforcement learning from AI feedback")\] fine-tunes SLMs with direct preference optimisation. Our work stays within the speech-only paradigm but targets _cross-lingual_ sharing through a new learning method.

### 2.2 Joint Speech–Text Interleaving

TSLMs extend SLMs with text to encourage cross-modal transfer. VoxtLM/SUTLM \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib5 "”Toward Joint Language Modeling for Speech Units and Text”")\] jointly trains on ASR, TTS, and continuation, and identifies _interleaving_ (switching between text and speech inside a sequence) as particularly effective for building a shared space. Spirit-LM \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib6 "SpiRit-LM: interleaved spoken and written language model")\] scales this recipe and emphasises interleaving as a key quality driver; SmolTolk \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib26 "Text-speech language models with improved cross-modal transfer by aligning abstraction levels")\] proposes architecture refinements for vocabulary expansion; and GLM4-Voice \[[36](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib4 "Scaling speech-text pre-training with synthetic interleaved data")\] leverages a text-to-token model \[[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib29 "Scaling speech-text pre-training with synthetic interleaved data")\] to generate audio tokens on-the-fly, sidestepping explicit aligners \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib3 "Robust Speech Recognition via Large-Scale Weak Supervision"), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib7 "Scaling speech technology to 1,000+ languages")\]. However, these approaches depend on text and interleave within a single language, limiting direct applicability to textless, cross-lingual SLM training. We instead interleave _speech tokens across languages_ without introducing text.

### 2.3 Cross-Lingual Training

Multilingual training for speech LMs is gaining traction but remains under-evaluated on spoken semantic understanding beyond English. Spirit-LM \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib6 "SpiRit-LM: interleaved spoken and written language model")\] includes multiple languages at scale but reports only English spoken evaluations; GLM4-Voice \[[36](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib4 "Scaling speech-text pre-training with synthetic interleaved data")\] trains on Chinese and English yet evaluates English semantics; and scheduled interleaving for speech-to-speech translation \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib8 "Scheduled Interleaved Speech-Text Training for Speech-to-Speech Translation with LLMs")\] interleaves _monolingually_ with no cross-language interaction during continuation. In text, mixing languages can induce positive transfer \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib9 "Code-Switching Curriculum Learning for Multilingual Transfer in LLMs")\]. Our method brings this intuition to the _‘textless’_ speech regime by interleaving sentence-aligned speech sequences across languages, enabling both cross-lingual abilities and improved monolingual semantics under a matched training-token budget.

## 3 Spoken Language Modelling

The task of modelling spoken language from raw audio typically comprises three stages: (i) quantising a waveform $`\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^{T}`$ into a sequence of $`L`$ discrete speech units with $`L\ll T`$; (ii) training an SLM under a next-token objective on the quantised sequence; and (iii) synthesising a waveform from the discrete units via a neural decoder. We first describe speech tokenisation, then the SLM objective, and finally our cross-lingual interleaving scheme.

### 3.1 Speech Language Models

Speech tokenisers. Speech tokenisers transform raw waveforms into discrete representations. Let $`\mathcal{X}`$ denote the domain of audio samples and let a waveform be $`\mathbf{x}=(x_{1},\dots,x_{T})\in\mathcal{X}^{T}`$. An encoder $`f:\mathbb{R}^{T}\!\rightarrow\!\mathbb{R}^{T^{\prime}\times d_{e}}`$ produces a sequence of continuous features $`f(\mathbf{x})=(\mathbf{v}_{1},\dots,\mathbf{v}_{T^{\prime}})`$, sampled at a lower frame rate $`T^{\prime}\ll T`$. A quantiser $`q`$ maps these to discrete units $`\,\mathbf{s}=(s_{1},\dots,s_{T^{\prime}})`$ with $`s_{i}\in\{1,\dots,K\}`$, where $`K`$ is the codebook size. Common choices include $`k`$-means and residual vector quantisation (RVQ).

In this work we adopt Mimi \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib10 "Moshi: a speech-text foundation model for real-time dialogue")\], a neural audio codec \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib19 "SoundStream: An end-to-end neural audio codec"), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib31 "High Fidelity Neural Audio Compression")\] implemented as a convolutional auto-encoder with RVQ in the latent space with semantic distillation from the WavLM representations \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib14 "WavLM: Large-scale self-supervised pre-training for full stack speech processing")\]. Following prior evidence that early codebooks capture semantics, we _only_ model the first (semantic) codebook during language modelling.

Speech language models. Given a sequence of $`L`$ speech tokens $`\mathbf{s}=(s_{1},\dots,s_{L})`$ over vocabulary $`\mathcal{V}_{s}`$, an SLM parameterises

```math
p_{\theta}(\mathbf{s})=\prod_{i=1}^{L}p_{\theta}\!\left(s_{i}\mid s_{<i}\right).
```

Decoder-only transformers \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib1 "Attention is All You Need")\] are typically used as SLM and trained by minimising the autoregressive negative log-likelihood

```math
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{LM}}=-\sum_{i=1}^{L}\log p_{\theta}\!\left(s_{i}\mid s_{<i}\right).
```

Each token $`s_{i}`$ is embedded via $`E\in\mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{V}_{s}|\times d}`$ to $`\mathbf{e}_{i}=E[s_{i}]`$. A stack of $`m`$ causal transformer blocks maps $`(\mathbf{e}_{1},\dots,\mathbf{e}_{L})`$ to contextual states $`(\mathbf{h}_{1},\dots,\mathbf{h}_{L})`$, with $`\mathbf{h}_{i}`$ depending only on $`s_{<i}`$. A projection $`U\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times|\mathcal{V}_{s}|}`$ yields logits $`\boldsymbol{\ell}_{i}=U^{\top}\mathbf{h}_{i}`$ and the predictive distribution $`p_{\theta}(s_{i}\!\mid\!s_{<i})=\mathrm{softmax}(\boldsymbol{\ell}_{i})`$.

### 3.2 Cross-Lingual Interleaving Training

Standard SLM training predicts the next speech token within a monolingual sequence. Interleaving has been proposed in TSLMs to bridge text–speech modality gaps by switching at word boundaries \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib5 "”Toward Joint Language Modeling for Speech Units and Text”"), [25](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib6 "SpiRit-LM: interleaved spoken and written language model"), [36](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib4 "Scaling speech-text pre-training with synthetic interleaved data"), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib16 "Scaling Properties of Speech Language Models")\], but such methods rely on text tokens and are thus incompatible with _textless_ SLMs; moreover, they do not readily support mixing multiple languages \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib9 "Code-Switching Curriculum Learning for Multilingual Transfer in LLMs")\].

We formulate a _cross-lingual interleaving_ scheme that operates directly on speech tokens by interleaving segments from multiple languages. Assuming sentence-level segmentation and cross-lingual sentence alignment, let $`A^{(l)}_{j}`$ denote the $`j`$th segment in language $`l`$, represented as a sequence of speech tokens. Given a set of languages $`\mathcal{L}`$, we sample a language label $`l_{j}\in\mathcal{L}`$ for each segment index $`j`$ and define the interleaved sequence as

```math
s=\big(A^{(l_{1})}_{1}\,\|\,A^{(l_{2})}_{2}\,\|\,A^{(l_{3})}_{3}\,\|\,\dots\big),
```

where $`\|`$ denotes concatenation at sentence boundaries.²²2Word-level switching is also possible when word-level alignments are available; unless stated otherwise, we use sentence-level switching. We then train with the standard next-token prediction objective on $`s`$, so that each prediction is conditioned on preceding segments that may come from different languages. Since Eq. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S3.E3 "In 3.2 Cross-Lingual Interleaving Training ‣ 3 Spoken Language Modelling ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models") is language-agnostic, it can in principle be applied to any language for which speech tokens are available; this is consistent with observations that code-switching-style training can transfer beyond distant languages \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib9 "Code-Switching Curriculum Learning for Multilingual Transfer in LLMs")\]. Having defined the mechanism, we next describe the sentence-aligned spoken corpora that make such training feasible (see Section [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S4 "4 Cross-Lingual Spoken Datasets ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models")).

Training SLMs is compute-intensive and data-hungry: effective systems require large speech corpora, while cross-lingual interleaving (see Section [3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S3.SS2 "3.2 Cross-Lingual Interleaving Training ‣ 3 Spoken Language Modelling ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models")) additionally demands alignment. In addition, we introduce bilingual spoken versions of _StoryCloze_ and _TopicCloze_ for evaluating cross-lingual semantic understanding (Table [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S4.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 4 Cross-Lingual Spoken Datasets ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models")). We next summarise how these datasets are constructed.

### 4.1 Cross-Lingual TinyStories

_TinyStories_ is a synthetic corpus of short narratives whose vocabulary targets 3–4-year-olds, generated by large language models to probe commonsense reasoning in text LMs. Its spoken variant has proved effective for SLM training, improving both modelling and generative metrics \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib16 "Scaling Properties of Speech Language Models"), [21](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib27 "Slamming: training a speech language model on one GPU in a day")\]. Stories are self-contained, causally structured, and fit within a typical SLM context windows.

In order to preserve cross-lingual semantic coherence, we construct sentence-level alignments that preserve order and meaning. A high-quality MT system (GPT-4) \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib40 "GPT-4 technical report")\] translates each sentence with full story context; the entire validation set and $`600`$k training sentences are translated into French while maintaining English alignment. We then synthesise speech with a multi-speaker TTS system \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib33 "Streaming sequence-to-sequence learning with delayed streams modeling")\]³³3https://github.com/kyutai-labs/delayed-streams-modeling (a $`1.6`$B model using delayed-streams modelling first introduced in Moshi \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib10 "Moshi: a speech-text foundation model for real-time dialogue")\]).

We subsample $`500`$ cross-lingual stories from the EN-FR _sTinyStories_ validation set, for each candidate voice we synthesise EN–FR pairs with the same voice. Using cosine similarity between Speaker Verification (SV) \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib14 "WavLM: Large-scale self-supervised pre-training for full stack speech processing")\]⁴⁴4HuggingFace Model: microsoft/wavlm-base-sv embeddings, we retain voices scoring $`>0.90`$ on average across paired sentences, yielding $`44`$ voices (26 female, 18 male). We synthesise the final sets with these $`44`$ voices, reusing the same speaker across languages to promote cross-lingual speaker consistency. The _sTinyStories_ corpus statistics are given in Table [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S4.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 4 Cross-Lingual Spoken Datasets ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models").

### 4.2 Cross-Lingual StoryCloze and TopicCloze

Assessing cross-lingual semantic abilities of SLMs is challenging due to the scarcity of spoken benchmarks beyond English \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib6 "SpiRit-LM: interleaved spoken and written language model"), [36](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib4 "Scaling speech-text pre-training with synthetic interleaved data")\]. Inspired by *StoryCloze* \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib35 "A corpus and cloze evaluation for deeper understanding of commonsense stories")\] and its spoken instantiation \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib24 "Textually Pretrained Speech Language Models")\], we introduce bilingual, sentence-aligned spoken variants—_sSC_ and _sTC_—in French and English.

Each benchmark comprises $`4`$k five-sentence stories: the model observes the first four sentences and must assign higher probability (log-likelihood) to the true ending than to an adversarial alternative. _sSC_ uses semantically incompatible endings, probing causal/temporal commonsense; _sTC_ draws negatives from different topics, probing topical coherence.

Corpus construction mirrors that in Section [4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S4.SS1 "4.1 Cross-Lingual TinyStories ‣ 4 Cross-Lingual Spoken Datasets ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models"): sentences are translated with GPT-4 using full story context, then synthesised with the same TTS system to preserve timing and prosody. To minimise confusion from speaker variability, we use a single female speaker (the top SV performer on validation) across both languages. Dataset statistics are given in Table [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S4.T2 "Table 2 ‣ 4 Cross-Lingual Spoken Datasets ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models").

## 5 Experimental Setup

Having introduced the interleaving mechanism and the datasets that support it, we now describe our training, and evaluation protocol.

### 5.1 Models and training

We use the Mimi tokeniser \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib10 "Moshi: a speech-text foundation model for real-time dialogue")\] at $`12.5`$ Hz with vocabulary size $`K{=}2048`$ and RVQ with $`32`$ codebooks; only the first (semantic) codebook is modelled. SLMs are initialised from a $`1`$B-parameter Llama 3.2 family checkpoint \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib34 "The llama 3 herd of models")\] and a $`360`$M-parameter Qwen2 family checkpoint \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib38 "Qwen2 technical report")\], following TWIST \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib24 "Textually Pretrained Speech Language Models")\], with a $`2048`$-token context window ($`\approx 2.73`$ minutes of speech). These initialisations are used for the $`1`$B and $`360`$M models. The textual embedding tables are replaced with audio embeddings for the new tokens.

Training uses $`4`$ H$`100`$ ($`80`$ GiB) GPUs, per-GPU batch $`153{,}600`$ tokens (total $`614{,}400`$ tokens/step). The Adam \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib41 "Adam: a method for stochastic optimization")\] optimiser is used with settings of $`(\beta_{1},\beta_{2})=(0.9,0.98)`$, gradient clipping $`1.0`$, weight decay $`0.1`$, and a linear warm-up of $`5\%`$ to $`5{\times}10^{-4}`$ followed by linear decay. Inputs are packed by concatenating samples until the target length is reached.

Training proceeds in three stages to isolate the effect of interleaving: (1) EN-only pre-training for $`50`$k steps to simulate a high-resource SLM; (2) cross-lingual interleaving for $`20`$k steps with a language sampling probability of $`0.5`$ to encourage positive transfer from the high-resource language (EN) to a new language (FR); and (3) alternating monolingual fine-tuning in FR and EN for $`15`$k steps to enable monolingual generation in both languages. Unless noted, the $`1`$B and $`360`$M models follow the same schedule. All comparisons are made with a matched training-token budget, meaning that each system sees the same number of speech tokens (and hence the same total speech duration at $`12.5`$ Hz). By matching the total training-token budget, we ensure that any gains arise from interleaving rather than from data volume or sampling differences.

### 5.2 Baselines

We compare to: (i) FR-only monolingual, trained solely on French; and (ii) EN+FR without interleaving, trained on mixed data but restricted to monolingual sequences. Both baselines follow the same protocol as mentioned before.

### 5.3 Datasets

For EN we use *LibriHeavy* \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib36 "Libriheavy: a 50,000 hours asr corpus with punctuation casing and context")\] ($`56`$k hours) plus our EN _sTinyStories_ (see Section [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S4 "4 Cross-Lingual Spoken Datasets ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models")) split for a total of $`76`$k hours. For FR we use our FR _sTinyStories_ ($`\sim 21`$k hours) split. Interleaving uses the full _sTinyStories_ sentence-aligned EN–FR corpus described above.

### 5.4 Evaluation

*sBLiMP* \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib37 "The zero resource speech challenge 2021: spoken language modelling")\] tests syntactic competence: for each grammatical/ungrammatical pair, the model should assign higher probability to the grammatical item.

*sWUGGY* \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#bib.bib37 "The zero resource speech challenge 2021: spoken language modelling")\] measures lexical discrimination by preferring a real word over a phonotactically similar nonce word.

_sSC_ and _sTC_ (see Section [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S4 "4 Cross-Lingual Spoken Datasets ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models")) evaluate semantic understanding by requiring preference for the true ending over an adversarial alternative.

Cross-lingual evaluation. We evaluate _cross-lingual_ _sSC_ and _sTC_ (EN$`\!\rightarrow\!`$FR, FR$`\!\rightarrow\!`$EN): prompts are in one language and continuations in the other. A robust cross-lingual SLM should achieve comparable performance across these conditions.

## 6 Experimental Results

### 6.1 Cross-lingual interleaving helps monolingual capabilities

Starting from monolingual baselines in Table [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S6.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 6 Experimental Results ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models"), French-only training already acquires non-trivial semantic competence with scores $`56.44`$% and $`69.85`$% on _sSC_ and _sTC_ for the $`360`$M model, respectively. The $`1`$B model shows the same level of competence. Interestingly, in this data regime the $`360`$M model often matches or exceeds the $`1`$B model on French, a pattern consistent with a capacity–data mismatch: with limited French hours, the smaller model is easier to optimise and less prone to overfitting or optimisation instabilities. Relative to English baselines, the _sSC_ task tends to be slightly more favourable to French, while _sTC_ is generally easier overall and, for French, yields an average increase of $`2.64`$ points over English.

Introducing interleaving _without_ a final stabilisation phase produces a predictable trade-off. Semantic scores in English decrease by a small margin (e.g., for the $`1`$B model, from $`56.06`$% to $`54.40`$%.), whereas syntactic and lexical probes (_sBLiMP_/_sWUGGY_) drop more noticeably (from $`61.96`$% and $`69.92`$% to $`52.73`$% and $`56.74`$%). An intuitive explanation is that exposure to rapidly alternating languages perturbs low-level regularities (agreement, morphology, phonotactics) more than high-level semantics. Despite this, French _sSC_ improves modestly even though the model sees substantially fewer French tokens than the monolingual baseline; in this regime, the $`360`$M model reaches $`57.08`$% while the baseline with $`3{\times}`$ more tokens obtains $`56.44`$%. The gains therefore cannot be attributed to data volume and are better explained as transfer from English into French. _TopicCloze_ in French may dip at this stage, suggesting that, on balance, raw interleaving trades a little monolingual polish for multilingual competence.

A brief bilingual stabilisation phase (fine-tuning on EN+FR without interleaving) restores most of the lost ground in English and consolidates the transfer into French. After stabilisation, English performance typically returns to within a hair’s breadth of the English-only baseline, particularly on lexical discrimination. Indeed, for the $`1`$B model, _sBLiMP_ and _sWUGGY_ recover to $`61.75`$% and $`69.15`$%, aligning with the EN baseline. At an equivalent French token budget, the stabilisation stage surpasses its monolingual FR baseline by a few points on both _sSC_ and _sTC_, achieving $`58.31`$% and $`70.39`$% versus $`55.31`$% and $`67.07`$%. This two-stage recipe consistently benefits both model sizes.

### 6.2 Cross-lingual abilities emerge with interleaving

Looking again at Table [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.01865v2#S6.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 6 Experimental Results ‣ Cross-Lingual Interleaving for Speech Language Models") and the cross-lingual evaluations (EN$`\to`$FR and FR$`\to`$EN), mixed EN+FR training _without_ interleaving underperforms when prompted in one language and continued in the other. Focussing on the 360M results, the baseline achieves 50.56% and 51.25% on _sSC_ and 55.58% and 57.34% on _sTC_ (EN$`\to`$FR and FR$`\to`$EN, respectively). These scores are above chance, especially on _sTC_. This gap likely reflects that _sTC_ is simpler than _sSC_; thus, small but non-trivial gains on _sSC_ translate into larger gains on _sTC_.

With interleaving, substantially larger cross-lingual performance is observed: _sTC_ approaches monolingual accuracy and _sSC_ narrows to within a few points. For the 360M interleaved model, _sSC_ reaches 56.44% and 55.37%, and _sTC_ reaches 65.20% and 65.84% (EN$`\to`$FR and FR$`\to`$EN). Comparing to the same model’s monolingual results, _sSC_ scores are 55.90% (EN) and 57.08% (FR), while _sTC_ scores are 64.00% (EN) and 68.67% (FR). These results show that (i) the model develops cross-lingual abilities that approach monolingual performance, and (ii) relative to the EN+FR baseline, interleaving yields markedly stronger cross-lingual continuation. The effect is particularly strong at 360M, indicating that smaller models, under limited French, extract proportionally more benefit from multilingual context. With the stabilisation phase, we preserve these cross-lingual abilities with a small degradation: 55.10% and 53.92% on _sSC_, and 62.90% and 63.35% on _sTC_ (EN$`\to`$FR and FR$`\to`$EN), representing average relative degradations of 1.39 points (_sSC_) and 4.45 points (_sTC_), offset by gains in monolingual performance. The same trends hold for the 1B models.

Finally, we measured layer-wise cosine similarity between hidden states for 1,000 aligned EN–FR sentences and found that interleaving leads to consistently stronger cross-lingual alignment than training on mixed data without interleaving, with average cosine similarities of 0.73 (EN+FR baseline), 0.75 (interleaving), and 0.76 (interleaving+stabilisation) for the 1B models.

## 7 Conclusions

We introduce a new cross-lingual interleaving scheme that mixes speech tokens across sentence-aligned languages without textual supervision, and we release an EN–FR training dataset, _TinyStories_ ($`\sim\!42`$k), together with spoken _StoryCloze_ and _TopicCloze_ benchmarks for EN–FR semantic evaluation. Under a matched training-token budget on $`360`$M–$`1`$B SLMs, the method improves monolingual semantic accuracy, enables robust cross-lingual continuation, and strengthens cross-lingual hidden-state alignment. Because the method places minimal constraints on optimisation dynamics, it is well suited to scaling multilingual SLMs, thereby enabling models to understand and converse across languages.
