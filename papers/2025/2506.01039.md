# PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data

Songjun Cao, Qinghua Wu, Jie Chen, Jin Li, Long Ma Tencent Youtu Lab, China

###### Abstract

As parallel training data is scarce for one-shot voice conversion (VC) tasks, waveform reconstruction is typically performed by various VC systems. A typical one-shot VC system comprises a content encoder and a speaker encoder. However, two types of mismatches arise: one for the inputs to the content encoder during training and inference, and another for the inputs to the speaker encoder. To address these mismatches, we propose a novel VC training method called PseudoVC in this paper. First, we introduce an innovative information perturbation approach named Pseudo Conversion to tackle the first mismatch problem. This approach leverages pretrained VC models to convert the source utterance into a perturbed utterance, which is fed into the content encoder during training. Second, we propose an approach termed Speaker Sampling to resolve the second mismatch problem, which will substitute the input to the speaker encoder by another utterance from the same speaker during training. Experimental results demonstrate that our proposed Pseudo Conversion outperforms previous information perturbation methods, and the overall PseudoVC method surpasses publicly available VC models. Audio examples are available ¹¹1https://songjuncao.github.io/pseudovc/.

###### Index Terms:

voice conversion, semi-supervised learning, information perturbation.

## I Introduction

Voice conversion (VC) is a task that transfers the voice of a source speaker to a target speaker, while maintaining the linguistic information. One-shot voice conversion is a special case where only one utterance of the target speaker is available. Recently, many works \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib1), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2), [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib3), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib4), [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib5), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib6)\] has been proposed to advance the development of the one-shot VC task.

As parallel training data is hard to achieve \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib7), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib8), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib9)\], many VC models are trained by reconstructing the source speech. Those systems \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib1), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib6)\] typically consist of a content encoder, a speaker encoder and a decoder ²²2Here we simply the model structure, as other information (pitch) can be modeled independently in some works., as depicted in Fig. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S1.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ I Introduction ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data"). In order to illustrate the mismatch problem more intuitively, we assume the outputs of both training and inference to be $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$, where $`c_{i}`$ and $`s_{m}`$ denote content information and speaker information, respectively. The content encoder extracts linguistic information from the source utterance $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$, where $`c_{i}`$ and $`s_{m}`$ denote content information and speaker information, respectively. The speaker encoder extracts speaker-related information from the source utterance $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$. The decoder then reconstructs the source utterance $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ based on the extracted content and speaker information. However, there are two mismatches between training and inference phases: 1. The inputs to the content encoder during training and inference are $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ and $`x{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$. 2. The inputs of the speaker encoder during training and inference are $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ and $`x{(c_{j},s_{m})}`$.

To alleviate the first mismatch problem, some information perturbation approaches \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2), [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib11), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib12)\] are proposed to transform the input of the content encoder from $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ to $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$ during training. This transformation aims to modify the speaker-related information while preserving the content information $`c_{i}`$. Although these perturbation methods can mitigate the mismatch problem to some extent, they still exhibit limitations in the diversity and naturalness of the generated $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$, which can adversely affect the performance of voice conversion. To overcome this problem, we propose an effective method termed Pseudo Conversion in this paper. This method utilizes pretrained VC models to convert the source utterance $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ into a pseudo utterance $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$, which will be fed into the content encoder. The timbre of generated pseudo utterances $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$ can be diverse and natural, thereby reducing the gap between training and inference.

Regarding the second mismatch problem, there has been relatively little exploration in the literature \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib1)\]. Inspired by scheduled sampling \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib13)\], we propose a simple method called Speaker Sampling. During training, we replace the input of the speaker encoder from $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ to another utterance spoken by the same speaker with a certain probability.

## II Related Work

For the perturbation on the input of content encoder, SPEECHSPLIT2.0 \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib11)\] uses Vocal Tract Length Perturbation (VTLP) \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib14)\] to modify the timbre by warping the frequency. NANSY \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib12)\] introduces a method that perturbs the information of the source utterance through three signal processing techniques. Additionally, \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\] utilizes an SR-based data augmentation approach to distort speaker information in the source utterance, thereby assisting the model in learning to extract clean content information. All these methods aims to modify the timbre of the source waveform, while preserving the linguistic information. However, despite these efforts, there remains a gap in terms of naturalness and speaker diversity between the perturbed utterances and real utterances.

DDDM-VC \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib1)\] mixes the speaker representation by using a binary selection between the original and shuffled representations in the same batch. So the speaker of selected representation may be different from the source utterance. In contrast, our approach for the speaker encoder input involves using other utterances from the same speaker during training.

## III Proposed Method

### III-A Overall architecture

As illustrated in Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ III-B Training strategy ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data"), our model is based on FreeVC \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\]. The content encoder of our model contains a WavLM model \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib15)\], a bottleneck extractor and a normalizing flow \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib16)\]. For the speaker encoder, we utilize a pretrained speaker verification model that has been trained on a large-scale corpus \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib17)\]. The posterior encoder is composed of non-causal WaveNet residual blocks used in WaveGlow \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib18)\]. The decoder is the HiFIi-GAN V1 generator \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib19)\].

```math
L_{total} = {L_{rec} + L_{kl} + {L_{adv}{(D)}} + {L_{adv}{(G)}} + {L_{fm}{(G)}}}
```

Following \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\], the training loss is defined by Equation [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S3.E1 "In III-A Overall architecture ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data"). This loss function comprises several components: the KL divergence loss $`L_{kl}`$, the reconstruction loss $`L_{rec}`$, the adversarial loss $`L_{adv}{(D)}`$ for the discriminator $`D`$, the adversarial loss $`L_{adv}{(G)}`$ for the generator $`G`$ \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib20)\], and the feature-matching loss $`L_{fm}{(G)}`$ \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib21)\].

### III-B Training strategy

#### III-B1 Pseudo Conversion

Inspired by semi-supervised learning \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib22), [23](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib23), [24](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib24)\], we propose a novel information perturbation method to alleviate the first mismatch problem defined in Section [I](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S1 "I Introduction ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data"). Firstly, we train a one-shot VC model $`\mathcal{M}_{t}`$ following \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\], which is named as the VC teacher model. Then, we generate pseudo utterances using $`\mathcal{M}_{t}`$ through the following process.

```math
{x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}} = {\mathcal{M}_{t}{({x{(c_{i},s_{m})}},{x{(c_{j},s_{n})}})}}
```

```math
{{\mathcal{S}{({x{(c_{i},s_{m})}})}} = {\{{x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}}\}}},{n = {1,\ldots,N}}
```

Assuming the source utterance is $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$, where $`c_{i}`$ and $`s_{m}`$ denotes the content and speaker. We can convert the source utterance to a pseudo utterance $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$ using Equation [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S3.E2 "In III-B1 Pseudo Conversion ‣ III-B Training strategy ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data"). Here, $`x{(c_{j},s_{n})}`$ denotes a reference utterance with speaker $`s_{n}`$, which is randomly selected from the training dataset. $`\mathcal{S}{({x{(c_{i},s_{m})}})}`$ is the set of pseudo utterances corresponding to the source utterance $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$, and $`N`$ indicates the total number of generated pseudo utterances.

The constructed $`({x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}},{x{(c_{i},s_{m})}})`$ are referred to as pseudo paired data. During training, one pseudo utterance $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$ will be randomly selected from $`\mathcal{S}{({x{(c_{i},s_{m})}})}`$ and fed into the WavLM model, while the source utterance $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ serves as the target for prediction.

The above process of generating pseudo utterances is termed Pseudo Conversion. While it draws inspiration from traditional semi-supervised learning, there are notable differences in the details. In the semi-supervised learning, pseudo labels generated by the teacher model are used as the model’s outputs. In our method, pseudo utterances generated by the teacher model are used as the model’s inputs. This approach aims to enhance the robustness of the model by providing it with diverse pseudo paired data during training.

#### III-B2 Speaker Sampling

As shown in Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ III-B Training strategy ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data"), the speaker embedding $`g`$ extracted by the speaker encoder plays a vital role in our model. It will be fed into the Flow, Decoder and Posterior Encoder components.

However the second mismatch problem defined in Section [I](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S1 "I Introduction ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data") will affect the performance. Similar mismatch problem exists in sequence prediction task \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib13)\], which can be alleviated by scheduled sampling. Similarly, we propose a simple training strategy to alleviate this problem, which is called Speaker Sampling. In this approach, we modify the input to the speaker encoder from $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ to $`x{(c_{j},s_{m})}`$ during training with a certain probability $`\alpha`$. Here, $`x{(c_{j},s_{m})}`$ is randomly selected from other utterances of the same speaker $`s_{m}`$.

The overall training process combining Pseudo Conversion and Speaker Sampling is formulated in the Algorithm [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#alg1 "Algorithm 1 ‣ III-B2 Speaker Sampling ‣ III-B Training strategy ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data").

1:Input: Training utterance $`{x{(c_{i},s_{m})}} \in \mathcal{X}`$. Generated pseudo utterance number $`N`$. Training iteration number $`Q`$. The probability of speaker sampling $`\alpha`$.

2:Output: Trained VC model $`\mathcal{M}`$

3:Train $`\mathcal{M}_{t}`$ on $`\mathcal{X}`$ following \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\]

4:for each utterance $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ in $`\mathcal{X}`$ do

5:     for $`n = 1`$ to $`N`$ do

6:         Randomly select utterance $`x{(c_{j},s_{n})}`$ from $`\mathcal{X}`$

7:         Generate pseudo utterance $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$ via Eq. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S3.E2 "In III-B1 Pseudo Conversion ‣ III-B Training strategy ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data")

8:     end for

9:     Combine pseudo utterances into set $`\mathcal{S}{({x{(c_{i},s_{m})}})}`$

10:end for

11:for $`q = 1`$ to $`Q`$ do

12:     Randomly draw a batch $`\mathcal{B}`$ from $`\mathcal{X}`$

13:     for each utterance $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ in $`\mathcal{B}`$ do

14:         Randomly select $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$ from $`\mathcal{S}{({x{(c_{i},s_{m})}})}`$

15:         $`x'{(c_{i},s_{n})}`$ will be fed into the content encoder

16:         Generate a random number $`r`$ in the range $`\lbrack 0,1\rbrack`$

17:         if $`r < \alpha`$ then

18:              $`x{(c_{j},s_{m})}`$ will be fed into the speaker encoder

19:         else

20:              $`x{(c_{i},s_{m})}`$ will be fed into the speaker encoder

21:         end if

22:     end for

23:     Train VC model $`\mathcal{M}`$ with loss of Equation [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S3.E1 "In III-A Overall architecture ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data")

24:end for

25:return trained model $`\mathcal{M}`$

## IV Experiment

### IV-A Experimental Setup

Our experiments are conducted on VCTK \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib25)\] and LibriTTS \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib26)\]. We use VCTK corpus for training. The split of training validation and testing follows \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\] exactly. The test-clean subset of LibriTTS is used for test. We resample the audio samples from 24 kHz to 16 kHz.

We use the pre-trained WavLM-large encoder to extract 1024-dimensional vectors for every 20ms of 16 kHz audio. We adopt the pretrained speaker model of \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib17)\] as speaker encoder, which is fixed during training. Our model is trained up to 200k steps using 8 NVIDIA V100 GPUs. The batch size is set to 64, with a maximum segment length of 128 frames. We set $`N`$ of Equation [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#S3.E3 "In III-B1 Pseudo Conversion ‣ III-B Training strategy ‣ III Proposed Method ‣ PseudoVC: Improving One-shot Voice Conversion with Pseudo Paired Data") to 25, meaning that 25 pseudo utterances will be generated for each source utterance.

### IV-B Baseline models

For the overall performance comparison, we choose three public models, which are:

1.  1.  PH \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib3)\], a one-shot VC model named Phoneme Hallucinator. It is based on set expansion. The number of hallucinated features is set to 30000.
2.  2.  DDDM-VC \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib1)\], a recently proposed diffusion-based generative model.
3.  3.  FreeVC \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\], a widely-used voice conversion framework. It is the exact baseline of our PseudoVC, as the two methods adopt similar model framework and training data.

For the ablation study of our proposed Pseudo Conversion method, we select three information perturbation methods for comparison. Each of those methods generates 25 perturbed utterances:

1.  1.  VTLP is a signal processing method used by \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib14)\]. The warping factor is set to $`\alpha \sim {U{(0.9,1.1)}}`$. We use the implementation ³³3https://github.com/biggytruck/SpeechSplit2.git.
2.  2.  NANSY is proposed in \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib12)\]. The method has three functions, which are random frequency shaping, pitch randomization and formant shifting. The hyperparameters of those functions are consistent with those in \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib12)\]. We refer to the project ⁴⁴4https://github.com/dhchoi99/NANSY.git.
3.  3.  SR is used by \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\]. This conversion is based on vertical spectrogram-resize operation. As described by \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\], the resize ratio ranges from 0.85 to 1.15. The details can be found in the official FreeVC project ⁵⁵5https://github.com/OlaWod/FreeVC.git.

### IV-C Evaluation Metrics

#### IV-C1 Objective metrics

We utilize Amphion \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib27)\] to calculate the word error rate (WER) using the Whisper large-v3 model \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib28)\]. Additionally, we measure speaker similarity through speaker encoder cosine similarity (SECS) using the Resemblyzer \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib29)\] model. Following \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.01039v1#bib.bib2)\], we randomly select 400 utterances (200 from VCTK, 200 from LibriTTS) as source speech, and we choose 12 speakers as target speakers, which results in 4800 utterances.

#### IV-C2 Subjective metrics

For the subjective metrics, we measure the 5-scale mean opinion score (MOS) and similarity mean opinion score (SMOS) for the speech naturalness and speaker similarity. 1 is the lowest perceived quality and 5 is the highest perceived quality. 15 participants evaluate the scores of 300 utterances which are randomly selected from the objective test dataset.

#### IV-E2 Impact of teacher models

In this section, we study the impact of different teacher models. Results for models b1/b2/b3 indicate that they yield similar performance levels. In traditional semi-supervised learning tasks, pseudo labels are typically treated as training targets, and it is generally observed that higher-quality pseudo labels lead to improved results. However, in our approach, we utilize pseudo utterances as input to the model. Consequently, the minor differences among the teacher models have a limited effect on the final performance.

## V Conclusions

In this work, we first analyze the mismatch problem between training and inference in the one-shot voice conversion task. Then we propose a novel training strategy combining Pseudo Conversion and Speaker Sampling to mitigate the mismatch problem. Finally we verify the effectiveness of our proposed method, which outperforms previous methods.

It is important to note that our method employs a two-stage training process, as the teacher voice conversion model must be trained initially. In future work, we aim to explore the development of a one-stage training framework.
