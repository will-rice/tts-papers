Rebuttal for ICASSP 2025 Submission

We sincerely thank the reviewers for their valuable comments and suggestions. We appreciate the opportunity to clarify and improve our work based on their feedback.

## Response to Reviewer \#1

1. Similarity to SpeechTokenizer and Limited Innovation:

We acknowledge that our work builds upon the foundation established by SpeechTokenizer \[1\]. However, our main contribution lies in enhancing the acoustic representation within the residual vector quantization framework by introducing ECAPA-TDNN as a teacher model for the residual codebooks beyond the first level. While SpeechTokenizer focuses primarily on semantic encoding using HuBERT, it does not explicitly address the encoding of detailed acoustic features such as speaker characteristics and prosody in the residual layers. Our method aims to capture these nuances by distilling acoustic information from ECAPA-TDNN into the residual codebooks, thereby improving the quality of reconstructed speech and performance on various downstream tasks.

Using ECAPA-TDNN for distilling acoustic features in this context is, to our knowledge, a novel approach. Although ECAPA-TDNN has been used for speaker verification and representation learning, integrating it into a residual vector quantization framework for speech tokenization introduces a new dimension to codec design. This method effectively bridges the gap between semantic and acoustic encoding in neural codecs, which has not been adequately addressed in prior work.

2. Inconsistent Model Comparison:

We regret any confusion caused by dataset discrepancies. In our experiments, we ensured a fair comparison by retraining FreeVC on the same LibriSpeech dataset that we used for our proposed model. We will make this explicit in the revised manuscript. By aligning the training datasets, we aimed to isolate the effect of the model architectures and training objectives on performance. We agree that dataset consistency is crucial for fair evaluation and will provide detailed descriptions of the training procedures for all baseline models.

3. Limited Evaluation Metrics:

We acknowledge that relying solely on PESQ may not provide a comprehensive assessment of codec performance. In the revised paper, we will include additional objective metrics such as STOI (Short-Time Objective Intelligibility) and SDR (Signal-to-Distortion Ratio) to evaluate speech quality and intelligibility. Furthermore, we will conduct subjective evaluations through Mean Opinion Score (MOS) tests to capture human perceptual preferences. We will also provide audio samples on a companion website for reviewers and readers to listen to the reconstructed speech.

## Response to Reviewer \#2

1. Necessity of Enhancing Acoustic Information in Codec Models:

While semantic codecs excel at capturing linguistic content, they often fall short in preserving speaker identity, emotion, and prosody, which are critical for natural and expressive speech synthesis. Enhancing acoustic information in codec models addresses this gap, enabling more faithful speech reconstruction and better performance in tasks like voice conversion and emotion recognition. Our work demonstrates that incorporating acoustic features into the residual quantization layers significantly improves the model’s ability to retain these important speech characteristics.

2. Potential Limitations of Distilling Acoustic Information Using ECAPA-TDNN:

We understand the concern about potential limitations. However, our experiments indicate that using ECAPA-TDNN as a teacher model effectively enhances the residual codebooks’ ability to capture acoustic nuances without imposing an upper bound on the codec’s potential. The ECAPA-TDNN provides rich and discriminative acoustic representations, which, when distilled into the codec, improve its performance on multiple tasks. We will include ablation studies to analyze the impact of different teacher models and discuss potential limitations in more detail.

3. Need for Stronger Baseline Comparisons and Comprehensive Metrics:

We agree that comparing our model with other state-of-the-art codecs like DAC and Semantic Codec would strengthen our evaluation. We will include these baselines in our experiments and provide comprehensive metrics, including both objective and subjective evaluations. For the reconstruction tasks, we will report PESQ, STOI, SDR, and conduct MOS tests to provide a holistic assessment of model quality.

4. Details Regarding Experimental Setup:

We apologize for any lack of clarity regarding our experimental setup. In the revised manuscript, we will provide detailed descriptions of the training and testing datasets, data preprocessing steps, model hyperparameters, and training procedures. Specifically, we used the LibriSpeech train-other-500 subset for training and the test-clean and test-other subsets for evaluation. We will ensure that all experimental details are transparently reported to facilitate reproducibility.

## Response to Reviewer \#3

1. Inclusion of Human Study and MOS/CMOS Scores:

We appreciate the suggestion to include human evaluations. In response, we will conduct Mean Opinion Score (MOS) tests with human listeners to assess the subjective quality of the reconstructed speech. These evaluations will provide insights into the perceptual quality of our model compared to baselines. We will report these results in the revised paper.

2. Providing Audio Samples:

We will create a supplementary website where we will host audio samples of the reconstructed speech for all models evaluated. This resource will allow reviewers and readers to subjectively assess the quality of the outputs and hear the differences between our model and the baselines.

3. Clarification on Training Dataset:

We used the LibriSpeech train-other-500 subset for training our tokenizer model. We appreciate the request for clarification and will specify this clearly in the revised manuscript.

4. Explanation of “Using a Single Token Value per Frame”:

In Section 3.4, “using a single token value per frame” refers to the evaluation setup where we use only the first-level codebook ($`\mathbf{z}_{0}`$) for tasks that require semantic content, aligning with the methodology of SpeechTokenizer. In contrast, for tasks that benefit from richer acoustic information, we utilize the residual codebook ($`\mathbf{z}_{1}`$) that has been trained to capture acoustic features via ECAPA-TDNN distillation. This approach demonstrates that our model retains both semantic and acoustic information, whereas SpeechTokenizer primarily focuses on semantic encoding without explicitly modeling detailed acoustic features in the residual codebooks.

5. Difference in Acoustic Information Compared to SpeechTokenizer:

While both our model and SpeechTokenizer use HuBERT for the initial codebook ($`\mathbf{z}_{0}`$), the key difference lies in how the residual codebooks are trained. SpeechTokenizer does not explicitly train the residual codebooks to capture acoustic information; they are left to model whatever residual the neural network learns during reconstruction, which may not effectively capture rich acoustic features. In our model, we explicitly distill acoustic information from ECAPA-TDNN into the residual codebook ($`\mathbf{z}_{1}`$), enhancing its ability to represent speaker identity, prosody, and emotion. This explicit distillation process enables our codec to retain more comprehensive acoustic information than SpeechTokenizer.

## Additional Clarifications and Improvements

We recognize the importance of thorough experimental validation and clear presentation. In addition to addressing the reviewers’ comments, we will:

- •
  Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of our model, including the impact of ECAPA-TDNN distillation on the residual codebooks.
- •
  Include comparisons with recent works such as DAC and Semantic Codec to position our contributions within the current state of the art.
- •
  Expand discussions on potential limitations and future directions, such as exploring other acoustic models as teachers or integrating end-to-end training approaches.
- •
  Ensure that all figures, tables, and descriptions are clear and provide sufficient detail for replication.

Once again, we thank the reviewers for their constructive feedback, which will greatly enhance the quality and clarity of our paper.
